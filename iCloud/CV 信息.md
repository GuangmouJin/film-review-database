**Give an example of a process, document, checklist, or support workflow you improved. What was the problem, what did you change, and what was the impact?**

One process I significantly improved was the employee onboarding and offboarding workflow at Lotus Technology.

When I joined the company, it was still a relatively new organisation and many internal processes were either incomplete or managed manually. Onboarding and offboarding activities were coordinated through emails and ad-hoc communication between HR, IT, Finance, and hiring managers. This created several challenges, including unclear approval ownership, inconsistent account provisioning and deprovisioning timelines, incomplete equipment requests, and limited visibility across teams.

To address this, I worked with stakeholders across HR, Finance, and IT to design and document a structured onboarding and offboarding process. We introduced standardised workflows that clearly defined approvers, account activation and deactivation timelines, mandatory training requirements, equipment request details, and ownership for each stage of the process. The workflow was then implemented through our BPM platform to provide greater transparency and consistency.

The impact was significant. New joiners received the correct equipment and system access on time, departing employees had accounts disabled in a timely and controlled manner, and all teams had clear visibility of their responsibilities. The process improved operational efficiency, reduced manual coordination effort, strengthened access control and compliance, and significantly reduced the risk of accounts or permissions being overlooked during employee transitions.

This experience reinforced the importance of combining technology, process design, and stakeholder collaboration to create scalable and secure operational workflows.


**Describe a real technical support issue you personally worked on where the cause was not obvious at first. Please include the symptoms, what you checked, what you ruled out, how you fixed or escalated it, and what you would do differently next time?**

One issue I worked on involved an overseas user whose Outlook desktop application suddenly stopped working and displayed a message indicating that the Microsoft 365 licence had expired. Interestingly, the user could still access Outlook Web App without any issues.
At first, the problem appeared to be licence-related. I checked the user's Microsoft 365 licence assignment and confirmed that the licence was active and correctly assigned. To isolate the issue further, I asked the user to log into a test device that I had prepared remotely, where Outlook worked normally. This suggested that the user account and licence were functioning correctly.
I then performed additional testing by signing into the affected device using my own account. Outlook worked successfully under my profile, which helped rule out a broader Office activation or application issue. Based on these tests, I concluded that the problem was likely related to the interaction between the user's profile, device configuration, or local authentication state.
I attempted several remediation steps, including reinstalling Microsoft Office, removing and re-enrolling the device through Intune, and revalidating device registration. However, the issue persisted.
As the user was based overseas and no spare device was available locally, I coordinated a remote rebuild of the machine. After reinstalling Windows, re-enrolling the device into Intune, and reconfiguring the user's environment, Outlook functioned normally again and the issue was fully resolved.
Although the exact root cause could not be definitively confirmed, my troubleshooting process allowed me to systematically eliminate potential causes and narrow the issue to the local device environment. Looking back, I would capture additional diagnostic logs earlier in the process to improve the chances of identifying the underlying cause before rebuilding the device.
