#BFI 长评-理想主义与现实主义的矛盾。影片展现了在现实社会背景下二者那种不可调和的矛盾状态，虽然影片中爱情贯穿整部电影，但在我看来主线仍然围绕着两个角色的象征意义。女主作为一个理想主义者，持续坚持追求社会公平与正义，愿意为理想而牺牲。虽然在中段呈现的爱情线大大削弱了她的角色深度，但是复杂的社会关系，尤其是深刻的情感羁绊在结尾处其实是强化了她的品质的。结尾处也非常凸显其主体性。男主则作为一个现实主义者，他有才华但对政治漠不关心，象征了一种更务实的生活态度，追求个人满足和舒适生活，避免与体制发生正面冲突。电影主要的张力是来自于二者都了解彼此情况下面对社会压力呈现出纠结与迷茫。影片通过两人关系的起伏隐喻了个人信仰与社会妥协之间的对立。女主的政治信念与男主的逃避代表了美国社会中理想主义与现实主义之间的矛盾，而这种冲突不仅反映在个人层面，也象征了战后站在十字路口的美国。

电影的编剧Arthur Laurents本人就曾因麦卡锡主义被列入黑名单，而失去工作和护照。影片中的女主角Katie的形象部分来自他年轻时身边的激进分子Fanny Price，他将自己的经历和时代背景融入了剧本，当时他和许多朋友因麦卡锡主义时代的“猎巫”被列入黑名单，他讲述好莱坞电影几乎没有涉及这段黑暗历史的作品，而他希望将其作为本片重要的元素，在剧本改写过程中，他与导演和制片之间出现了巨大分歧。他坚持保留政治主线，而其他人希望削弱政治内容，加强爱情故事，塑造更有存在感的男主，最终他被解雇，剧本由11位编剧反复修改，这导致制作多次延期。后来导演让他回来修补剧本，经过多次改写后制片才对男主这个角色满意，觉得男主不再是爱情中的受害者，而是拥有自己的力量，影片最终成功上映。

男主角最初是计划由Ryan O’Neal出演，但由于他与女主演员关系变化退出了项目，而后Robert Redford成为新的候选人。我每次看到Robert的形象…都能把他与布拉德皮特重合上…而且我记得他现实对皮特的影响也非常大。





#BFI 影片展现了在现实社会背景下理想主义者和现实主义者那种不可调和的矛盾状态，虽然影片中爱情贯穿整部电影（其实也是我不太喜欢的部分），但在我看来主线仍然围绕着两个角色的象征意义。影片的创作背景也是在看电影前通过折页简单了解了一下，也解释了我觉得其中爱情有些占比太高的原因。

然而，在剧本改写过程中，劳伦斯与波拉克、斯塔克和雷德福之间出现了巨大分歧。劳伦斯坚持保留政治主线，而其他人希望削弱政治内容，加强爱情故事，塑造更有存在感的赫柏。最终，劳伦斯被解雇，剧本由11位编剧反复修改。这导致制作多次延期，并留下诸多问题。

尽管制作过程充满波折，波拉克最终让劳伦斯回来修补剧本。经过多次改写，雷德福对他的角色更加满意，他不再是爱情中的受害者，而是拥有自己的力量。影片最终成功上映，成为经典之作。

翻译：《往日情怀》创作背景与制作过程

在1973年，芭芭拉·史翠珊（Barbra Streisand）已经是世界上最著名的明星之一。然而，她刚刚经历了一次罕见的职业失利——1972年的《Up the Sandbox》票房表现不佳。哥伦比亚影业制片人雷·斯塔克（Ray Stark）与史翠珊有长期的合作关系，他希望为她打造一个新项目来推动她的事业发展。他认为，一部新电影会是一个完美的契机。

斯塔克找到了资深编剧亚瑟·劳伦斯（Arthur Laurents），劳伦斯曾在1962年为史翠珊执导百老汇处女作《I Can Get It for You Wholesale》。斯塔克要求他为史翠珊想出一个故事点子。

劳伦斯开始回忆自己1930年代在康奈尔大学的大学时光。他在回忆录中提到，1937年是“西班牙内战的校园和平罢工和追捕‘红色’学生的年代”。他特别记得一位名叫法妮·普莱斯（Fanny Price）的女生——一个“激进的校园火焰人物”，并好奇她后来怎么样了。劳伦斯说：“普莱斯是我女主角的一个五彩缤纷的起点，但仅仅是个起点。她除了推翻资本主义之外，还想要什么呢？”

塑造角色

劳伦斯还从自己大学时期强烈的写作欲望中汲取灵感，帮助塑造了凯蒂·莫罗斯基（Katie Morosky）这一角色。
“好的故事是由人物驱动的，而不是情节，”劳伦斯说。“法妮对成为作家的热情为凯蒂增添了一维深度。她的文章被拒绝，与她在和平罢工中的激进表现形成了鲜明对比。随后她被告知她是个作家——这一切很不错，故事逐渐成型。可它并没有真正成型，因为我无法相信法妮是个作家。热情、信念、欲望？当然，她有这些。但才华？一点儿都没有。没有理由，只是直觉，但我知道我没错。”
最终，法妮被重塑成了一个不屈的凤凰，她的名字不再是法妮，而是凯蒂。

制作矛盾与剧本改写


**《往日情怀》（The Way We Were）**中的男女主角分别象征了两种截然不同的价值观和人生道路：理想主义与现实主义。通过这段爱情故事，影片揭示了个体在时代与社会背景下的选择与矛盾，表达了对历史、爱情和人性复杂性的深刻反思。以下是影片隐喻和象征意义的分析：

1. 男女主角的象征意义
	•	凯蒂·莫罗斯基（Katie Morosky）——理想主义者
凯蒂是一个激进的政治活动家，追求社会公平与正义，愿意为理想而牺牲。她象征了那些坚持信念、不畏艰险、希望通过改变世界来实现个人价值的人。她的热情与执着反映了战后美国左翼理想主义者的特质，尤其是那些在麦卡锡主义阴影下坚持信仰的人。
	•	赫柏·加德纳（Hubbell Gardiner）——现实主义者
赫柏是一个英俊、有才华但对政治漠不关心的作家。他象征了一种更务实的生活态度，追求个人成就和舒适生活，避免与体制发生正面冲突。他对凯蒂的理想主义感到矛盾，既被她的热情吸引，也因无法认同她的激进立场而疏远。

2. 影片的隐喻
	•	理想与现实的冲突
影片通过两人关系的起伏，隐喻了个人信仰与社会妥协之间的对立。凯蒂的政治信念与赫柏的逃避态度代表了美国社会中左翼理想主义与中产阶级务实主义之间的矛盾。这种冲突不仅反映在个人层面，也象征了战后美国社会的分裂。
	•	时代的选择与代价
影片背景涵盖了美国从大萧条到麦卡锡主义时期的社会变迁。在这种动荡的历史背景下，人们不得不在理想与安全之间做出选择。凯蒂的坚持和赫柏的妥协分别代表了两种对时代的回应，而他们的爱情最终无法跨越这一鸿沟，象征着理想与现实在历史中的难以调和。

3. 两种派别的选择
	•	理想主义的选择与代价
凯蒂始终坚持自己的信仰，拒绝向体制妥协。这种选择虽然让她内心充实，但也使她在爱情中孤独。她对赫柏的要求与期待，既是对他的爱，也是希望他能成为自己理想的一部分。凯蒂的坚持让她失去了与赫柏共度生活的机会，但她对自己的选择无怨无悔。
	•	现实主义的妥协与困境
赫柏选择了回避冲突，过上相对安稳的生活。这种选择虽然让他免受政治迫害，但也让他失去了与凯蒂之间深刻的情感联结。他对凯蒂的爱是真实的，但他的妥协让这段感情无法长久。他代表了那些选择远离政治风波、追求个人幸福的人，但也暗示了这种选择的空虚与遗憾。

4. 影片的核心隐喻：理想与现实的不可调和性

影片最终表明，无论是凯蒂的理想主义还是赫柏的现实主义，都有其合理性和局限性。两人之间的爱情象征了两种价值观的短暂交融，但这种交融终究无法维持长久。这不仅是个人选择的悲剧，也是时代赋予他们的困境。

结论

**《往日情怀》**通过凯蒂和赫柏的爱情故事隐喻了理想与现实之间的矛盾和冲突，展现了在特定历史背景下个人的抉择与命运。两种派别各有自己的选择和代价：理想主义追求改变但容易孤独，现实主义选择安稳却可能失去内心深刻的情感联系。影片的隐喻不仅针对战后美国社会，更是对人类普遍困境的深刻反思。

The Way We Were
In 1973 Barbra Streisand was already one of the world's biggest stars. However, she had recently suffered a rare career misfire with the box office disappointment Up the Sandbox (1972). Columbia Pictures producer Ray Stark, who had a long professional relationship with Streisand, wanted a new project for her to star in.
A new movie would be just the thing, he thought, to give her a career boost.
Stark approached veteran writer Arthur Laurents, who had also directed Streisand in her 1962 Broadway debut / Can Get It for You Wholesale, to come up with a story idea. Laurents began thinking about his old college years at Cornell during the 1930s. His senior year in 1937 was, according to his 2000 memoir Original Story By, 'the year of campus peace strikes to end the Spanish Civil War and witch hunts to find undergraduate Reds.' He remembered in particular a girl he had gone to school with named Fanny Price who had been a 'fiery campus radical' and wondered what had become of her. Price, he said, was 'a colourful beginning for the character of my heroine but little more than a beginning. What did she want beyond the overthrow of capitalism?'
Laurents also drew from his intense desire to be a writer during college to help shape the character of Katie Morosky vis-a-vis his old classmate Fanny Price.
'Characters rather than plot drive the better stories,' he said. 'For the movie story I was developing, Fanny's passion to be a writer added a new dimension to her character. The rejection of her essay, contrasting sharply with her belligerence at the Peace Strike, followed by the scene where she's told she is a writer - all that was good, the story was slowly taking shape. Except it really wasn't. And couldn't because I couldn't believe Fanny was a writer. Passion, conviction, desire? Yes; brimming over; she had them all. Talent? No; not for one minute could I believe she had any. No reason, just instinct but I knew I was right. But recognising what she lacked gave me something better, something basic to her character: resilience. In the end, Fanny was indestructible, a phoenix, and her name wasn't Fanny it was Katie.'
Laurents also made the conscious decision to make Katie Jewish. 'She had to be a Jew,' he said; 'Barbra herself had arrived as one. And Katie could only be a Jew because of her insistence on speaking out, her outrage at injustice, her passion, her values, and because I was a Jew. Besides, it was fresher and high time that the movies, the only industry founded by Jews, had a Jewish heroine.' Laurents mined more material from his early days in Hollywood during the 1940s when he and many of his friends and colleagues had been blacklisted as a result of the McCarthy-era Communist witch hunts. Hollywood films had dealt very little with this dark period in its history, and Laurents was passionate about making it a big part of The Way We Were's story. It became personal for him, especially since it was his first original screenplay. Up until then, his scripts had been adaptations of others' work. 'But this was an original based on me, my life in college, during the war and during the Hollywood Witch Hunt,' he said. 'I had never seen a movie or a play set in that period in which those I knew either lived by fighting for the Ten or died by turning informer. There was no middle ground. I was blacklisted, I had my passport taken away. I believed it wasn't un-American to be a member of the Communist Party, it was un-American to be on the House Un-American Committee. I still believed that and I wanted to say it through a heroine who was a Jewish Communist. In a Hollywood movie.'
It was Laurents who pushed for Sydney Pollack to be the director on The Way We Were. He had been impressed with Pollack's work on the period 1969 drama They Shoot Horses, Don't They? and lobbied Ray Stark to use him.

During the rewrite process on the screenplay, Arthur Laurents often found himself at odds with Pollack, Stark and Redford. Laurents resisted changing how Hubbell was written and wanted to keep the political storyline front and centre. Pollack, Stark and Redford, however, wanted to cut some of the politics, beef up the love story and make Hubbell a much stronger presence. Frustrations were running high on both sides. Soon Laurents got the news from Pollack that he was fired.
'The hurt was unexpected,' said Laurents. 'It was very painful, I was surprised how painful. It was only a movie, for God's sake. But of course, it wasn't only a movie.
The story was mine; it came from me, the characters came from me, there was some of me in all the people and what happened to them. It hurt and what made it hurt more was why I was fired. Robert Redford was dissatisfied with his part. That much, Sydney told me; why he was dissatisfied he never told me.'
One explanation that Pollack later offered regarding Laurents' dismissal had to do with his inability to resolve the political storyline. 'It was about a relationship complicated by HUAC, but those vivid subtexts were lost. It's not that he didn't understand HUAC, but he didn't contextualise it properly. I did not alter Laurents' story line "manipulatively," as Arthur accused. I did it because I had a hunch Bob and Barbra would be magical together, and I knew I had to engage Bob's intelligence.
Following Laurents' unceremonious departure, Sydney Pollack brought in a total of 11 different writers - among them Alvin Sargent and Dalton Trumbo -to work on getting the screenplay where he and Redford wanted it. The constant stream of rewrites caused production to be delayed time and again. In the end, having too many cooks in the proverbial kitchen created more problems than it solved. 'They hadn't kept track of the rewrites of the rewrites,' noted Arthur Laurents. 'There were holes in scenes, the story was garbled, Barbra was unhappy, Redford wasn't any happier...'
Eventually, Pollack called Laurents and asked him to come back. 'They wanted me to repair the damage, cover it and make sure that what remained to be shot was kept on track, said Laurents. 'I was past hurt and anger; I had no feeling of vindication or victory, pyrrhic or otherwise. I cared to the extent that I had pride in my work. I'd take a crack at salvaging and protecting it as much as I could but I would not let myself be hurt again.
