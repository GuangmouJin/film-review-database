#BFI 维斯康蒂的一部关于路易维希二世的传记片，电影分为两部分，有中场休息，在第一部分着重讲述了主角的生平，一个理想主义者是如何舍弃了政治拥抱艺术的，而且还谈及了他的情感与身份认同的问题，第二部分则是在他在乎的事物背离他的想法后自我逐渐失控的过程，与此对照的则是巴伐利亚王室的没落。整体的观感上第二部分脱离了个体而侧重点更多的放到了巴伐利亚的政治上，虽然是为了对照他历史上的死亡，但感觉割裂感非常强，还有就是第二部分的音量感觉比第一部分高很多，很多时候台词，配乐或音效较强的的时候音响会有破音的情况，太难受了。

角色上，第一部分的塑造还是非常成功的，感觉最近看雷导的《拿破仑》也是类似的表达，呈现更多的是着重于角色内心的塑造，身份，理想和社会期望等多重的压力让这个角色确实更加的贴近于现实的人，结尾处的死亡是非常模糊的处理，查了一下好像现实也是如此模糊。

视觉是影片最突出的部分，最吸引人的不仅是那华丽的布景和群像戏的调度，还有那脸部特写长镜头的使用，角色的很多欲望的表达都是通过这种特写镜头达成的，还有大量的变焦镜头突出情绪或情感变化也非常的有张力。镜子使用的也很好，不光是利用镜子构图呈现出的角色关系，还有不少利用镜子表达迷失的自我。光影使用也很棒，虽然光线一些非现实，但设计出来的侧打光对于角色塑造非常重要，夜色的光影效果实在是太美了。当然不得不说各种城堡的实景拍摄，真的是大手笔，尤其是试图复制凡尔赛宫的海伦基姆湖宫，那一瞥的外景就已经很惊艳了，其内的镜厅则实在是太震撼了，不光我觉得震撼…角色在电影中都“笑”了。


尽管路德维希二世在政治上被视为失败的统治者，但他的文化遗产对巴伐利亚乃至整个德国的旅游业和艺术界产生了深远影响：
	•	文化遗产：他所建造的城堡，如新天鹅堡，成为世界闻名的旅游景点，影响了迪士尼童话城堡的设计，并被誉为“德国浪漫主义的象征”。
	•	艺术赞助：他对瓦格纳的支持使其作品得以蓬勃发展，德国的歌剧艺术因此受益。
	•	民间记忆：如今的巴伐利亚人对这位“童话国王”抱有复杂情感，既敬仰他的艺术成就，也惋惜其悲剧性命运。

总结

路德维希二世的一生充满传奇色彩，他深受艺术理想的驱使，却未能成功履行国王的政治责任。他的死亡至今仍是一个未解之谜，但无论如何，他对德国文化的贡献依然历久弥新，他的宫殿成为巴伐利亚地区最具吸引力的文化遗产。




影片的另一大亮点在于场景调度。维斯康蒂在大多数作品（《白夜》与《家庭的肖像》是显著例外）中，始终坚持实景拍摄的原则。他的创作始于真实场景，那些过去曾经或可能发生故事的地方。正如《魂断威尼斯》一样，维斯康蒂和他的团队利用实景营造了一种真实氛围，让一切都显得无比自然，而非出自摄影棚。对真实性的执着追求是维斯康蒂的标志性风格，但在不同作品中呈现方式各异。通常而言，早期的黑白作品比后来的彩色电影更加简约。这部分原因在于，维斯康蒂的关注重心逐渐从无产阶级环境（如《沉沦》、《大地在波动》和《美丽的家庭》）转移到衰落的上层资产阶级与贵族阶层的奢华世界（如《夏宫遗梦》和《豹》）。在后期作品中，他对装饰性的兴趣增加，彩色电影的视觉表现力也使得画面更加丰富多彩。在《路德维希》中，与《魂断威尼斯》不同，色彩主要趋于自然主义。影片试图将观众带入一个奇异的世界，这种奇异并非主观幻象，而是客观存在，反映的是路德维希的现实，而非如《魂断威尼斯》中的主人公阿申巴赫的幻觉。

折页提及维斯康蒂在构思《路德维希》时最显而易见的灵感来源是莎士比亚的《理查二世》。虽然维斯康蒂从未排演过这部戏剧，但他几乎可以肯定熟知这部作品，同时也可能熟悉马洛的《爱德华二世》，后者探讨了类似主题，并带有强烈的同性恋元素。

关于路德维希的一切都因其君王身份而与众不同。他不仅仅是一个因个人兴趣而影响工作的性情孤僻者，肩负的王位责任本质上充满矛盾。一方面，它让他有自由去资助瓦格纳，另一方面，却让他的性取向显得更加不合法。

《路德维希》是一部宏大的电影。它不仅篇幅漫长，视觉和听觉上也极其丰富，涵盖了多个主题。正如提供叙事蓝本的19世纪伟大小说一样，这部电影可以根据不同的中心主题进行多种解读。对许多观众而言，一个显而易见的主题是“出柜”与路德维希对自身性取向的困惑与失败。另一个主题则是对旧政治秩序消逝的哀叹。然而，影片之所以能取得成功，在于它为这些不同主题提供了一定的自由空间，同时又将其紧密结合。在我看来，整部电影的核心——如爱森斯坦所言的“主导元素”——是国王身份与同性恋倾向之间的毁灭性结合。两者各自都使路德维希成为一个“异类”，但若少了其中之一，他的命运或许尚可承受。在这个以“父权”为本的世界中，路德维希作为失败的王朝继承者，其地位注定无法持久，悲剧结局势不可免。

如果说《魂断威尼斯》是关于幻象，那么《路德维希》即便在最奢华的场景下，仍然关于现实。尽管影片对主人公抱以怜悯和敬畏，但呈现的视角却是冷静且毫不留情的。




Ludwig
Shown at the sweeping, epic length Visconti initially intended, and not without some vague parallels to his own life, this stately biopic focuses on the life of wildly eccentric, closeted gay royal Ludwig II. Known for constructing mad fairytale castles dotted across the Bavarian landscape, the king eschewed political power in exchange for his love of art, Wagner and architecture, only to find his idealism wrecked and his mental state edging towards insanity.

Ludwig belongs to the group of Visconti's films which I call dynastic. These are films which trace the vicissitudes of a named family - the Valastro in La terra trema, the 'casa Salina' in The Leopard, the Essenbecks in The Damned, etc. - culminating usually in the family's dissolution. Visconti applied this narrative model across the entire social spectrum, though its classical place of application, as in the nineteenth-century novels which he loved so much, is families with property and a reputation to defend. Here in Ludwig he moves away from the world of peasants, proletarians, bourgeoisie or provincial aristocracy to tackle a grander dynastic theme, the collapse of a royal house.
And the flawed individual who is at the centre of the film is not someone with the usual blend of strengths and weaknesses such as one might find sitting next to one on the bus, but a king, the bearer of a unique calling, brought up from infancy to believe that he occupies a place apart.
The obvious model for Visconti to have had in mind for Ludwig is
Shakespeare's Richard Il. Although this is not a play he ever staged, it is one he almost certainly knew and he probably also knew Marlowe's Edward II, which treats a similar theme and has a strong homosexual motif. Like Ludwig, Shakespeare's Richard starts as a legitimate monarch and remains painfully conscious of this fact even as he becomes aware of the way power is being stripped from him. Like Ludwig, too, Richard is an aesthete, who enjoys the notion of kingship in a self-regarding way but neglects the ordinary responsibilities of political life. Finally, both Richard and Edward are depicted, with varying degrees of explicitness, as neglectful of their conjugal duty and the protection of their line.
What is suggestive here is not the narrative coincidences, which on closer inspection are not all that great, but the assumption shared between Visconti and Shakespeare that legitimacy is important but can also be a burden.
Everything about Ludwig is different because he is a king. He is not just a temperamental loner whose tastes get in the way of his doing his job.
Furthermore the burden that is thrust on him is intrinsically conflictual. If on the one hand it allows him the freedom to patronise Wagner, on the other hand it makes his sexual desires all the more unlawful.
Whatever Visconti's debt to Shakespeare, however, their political and historical contexts are very different. Shakespeare was a sixteenth-century royalist and apologist of the Tudor monarchy. Visconti was a twentieth-century Marxist and republican. The story of Ludwig Il, last King of Bavaria, appealed to him against the grain of his political convictions. As so often in Visconti, there seem to be two stories going on. One is the story of an impersonal social and political process whereby an outdated institution is cast aside in favour of less arbitrary forms of rule. And the other is a more elegiac tale of individuals threatened with the loss of the lifeworld which sustains them. In earlier films such as Rocco and His Brothers (1960) or The Leopard (1963) the two stories are kept in balance.
Although the films end badly (Rocco) or ambiguously (The Leopard) for the protagonists, the process of which they are victims is at least some kind of progress. Already in The Damned (1969), however, what replaces the old patriarchal world of the Essenbecks is not progress at all but the cataclysmic disaster of Nazism. And here in Ludwig there is no sense that the world inaugurated by the ministers with their black umbrellas, top hats and frock-coats is in any way preferable to the colourful world of courts and dynasts that it replaces.
Ludwig is a vast film. Not only is it very long, but it is visually and aurally rich, and covers a range of themes. Like the great nineteenth-century novels which provide its narrative models, it is open to a variety of readings, depending on which theme is chosen as the central one. One such theme, for many spectators, is the 'coming out' motif and the story of Ludwig's largely ineffective attempts to come to terms with his sexuality. Another is the historical, the lament for the passing of the old political order. But the film only works as well as it does by allowing its various themes a certain free rein while at the same time holding them together. In my reading of the film, what holds the whole thing together - the dominant, as Eisenstein would have put it - is kingship and the destructive pairing of this with Ludwig's homosexuality. Each in a sense makes him a man apart and each would be bearable without the other. But, in an order which is, literally, patriarchal, Ludwig's position as failed dynast is untenable and a tragic ending is the only one possible.
The other things that make the film work well are properties of the mise en scène. In the majority of his films (White Nights and Conversation Piece are conspicuous exceptions), Visconti shows a rigorous dedication to the principle and practice of location filming. Locations may be dressed in various ways, but Visconti's starting-point is a place where the action would, or could, have taken place. As in Death in Venice, Visconti and his team have used the locations to create an atmosphere that breathes authenticity and where nothing looks studio because nothing is studio. This insistence on authenticity is a characteristic Visconti trademark but it works differently in different films.
In general the earlier (and black and white) films are sparser than the later (colour) ones. This is partly a function of context, as Visconti moves his attention up the social scale from the proletarian environments of Ossessione, La terra trema and Bellissima to the faded haut-bourgeois and aristocratic opulence of Senso and The Leopard. But there is also, in the later films, an interest in the decorative in its own right and in the potential of colour film to render visual surfaces in different ways. In Ludwig, unlike Death in Venice, the colour is mostly naturalistic. Insofar as the film attempts to draw the audience into a strange world, this strangeness is objective. It reflects what Ludwig is, rather than, as in Death in Venice, what Aschenbach feels. If Death in Venice is all about illusion, Ludwig, even at its most extravagant, is about reality. While in Death in Venice we are led into Aschenbach's illusions and watch them crumble with him, in Ludwig the view is unsparing, inviting pity and terror nonetheless.
