# 《蒙特雷旅馆》（Hotel Monterey）翻译

# 
#BFI 很美妙，看前小伙伴说阿克曼很大程度收到了斯诺的影响，刚好另一家影院在做斯诺的策展，今天看完斯诺就来看阿克曼，也是美妙的巧合。影片就如同昨天看《阿克曼自画像》中呈现的一样，空间的场所变得格外的重要。

视觉上也相当实验，逼仄的环境让人有些喘不上气，大量的空镜场景，记录性质的拍摄也让看到镜头的人有些错愕，倒是很真实。大量的固定镜头拍摄蒙特利旅馆，少有运动镜头，这也是巧妙的地方，由于影片是一场封闭空间内时间的游戏，那特殊的部分就会更强的放大，从而产生张力。影片中几次运动镜头展现出一扇标有“出口”的窗户和结尾处俯拍的街景，那光亮真的是太美好了，不过总感觉标记着“出口”的窗户可能还有其他寓意。

影片完全没有声音，但电影院的空调声音很大，到后半段的时候有些烦躁，恰巧隔壁的哥们儿睡着了，呼吸声也变得粗了起来…然后我……突然想到可以直接戴降噪耳机…反正也没声音，感觉非常有用，早想到好了。






珍妮·贝格斯特罗姆（Janet Bergstrom），《视与听》（Sight and Sound），1999年11月

阿克曼一时冲动地离开布鲁塞尔前往纽约。她曾说：“除了《狂人皮埃罗》，这次经历是决定我电影生涯的关键因素。”

在纽约，她观看了布拉哈格（Stan Brakhage）的电影，但最重要的是迈克尔·斯诺（Michael Snow）的作品。他的电影完全基于电影语言本身，没有任何叙事或情感……它纯粹是语言本身，没有任何附加的杂质，也无法让观众投射自我。

阿克曼在1971年至1974年间断断续续地在纽约生活了一年半，其间数次返回欧洲。她几乎是在抵达后不久就结识了巴贝特·曼戈尔特（Babette Mangolte），后者不仅成为了她的朋友和导师，还为她打开了纽约先锋电影、戏剧和音乐的世界，并且担任了她这一时期所有影片（除《8月15日》（Le 15/8，1973）和《我，你，他，她》（Je, tu, il, elle）外）的摄影师。曼戈尔特热衷于尝试新的技术和设备，以配合阿克曼的创作理念，并帮助她通过借来的设备和志愿者，以零预算的方式继续拍摄电影。

在这次纽约之行中，阿克曼创作了两部令人惊叹的短片——《房间》（La Chambre）和《蒙特雷旅馆》（Hotel Monterey），它们在美式极简电影的意义上都是实验性的。《房间》（1972）是一部十分钟的无声电影，深受斯诺的影响，镜头在一个小公寓内以连续的圆周运动扫描，有时会改变方向。然而，与斯诺不同的是，片中出现了一位年轻女性——阿克曼本人，她躺在床上，直视“我们”（即占据视觉场域的摄影机）。虽然她进入画面时，摄影机的距离和节奏没有发生变化，但每次她出现时都会做出一个简单的动作（比如前后摇晃，或吃一个苹果……）。房间中充满了美丽的自然光，缓和了影片可能带来的刻板感。

相比之下，《蒙特雷旅馆》要宏大得多。它拍摄于次年，用一个小时的时间描绘了一家廉价住宅旅馆及其住客，并赋予了摄影机所占据的画外空间以一种强烈的在场感，而这种在场感从未与任何特定人物或角色相联系。阿克曼将这部电影的叙事推进形容为“在空间和时间上的上升”，从傍晚时分的一楼开始，最终在黎明时抵达屋顶。摄影机缓缓穿行于大厅，进入电梯，电梯门在不同楼层开合，走廊时而空无一人，时而有某人坐在房间里。这种无目的的等待感极为强烈，类似于爱德华·霍珀（Edward Hopper）的画作——后来的《让娜·迪尔曼》（Jeanne Dielman, 23 quai du Commerce, 1080 Bruxelles）也被拿来与霍珀的作品作比。阿克曼回忆道：“当我第一次看到那家旅馆时，我很震惊。如果我立刻拍点什么，那只会像一则新闻报道。但我思考了这家旅馆整整六个月，通过调度和对电影语言的探索，我捕捉到了十倍于直接记录的真实。”

《蒙特雷旅馆》还促成了阿克曼与德尔菲娜·塞里格（Delphine Seyrig）的合作。塞里格是法国新浪潮的标志性人物之一，在1973年夏天南锡电影节（Festival de Nancy）的评审团中，将奖项授予了这部电影。这次相遇使得《让娜·迪尔曼》的拍摄成为可能。

尚塔尔·阿克曼谈《蒙特雷旅馆》

采访者：梅丽莎·安德森（Melissa Anderson），movingimagesource.us，2010年1月19日

问：蒙特雷旅馆位于哪里？
阿克曼：应该是在百老汇大道和96街。我记得它已经被拆除了。

问：你与这家旅馆有什么联系？
阿克曼：我认识了一个住在那里的日本人。有时，当我不知道该去哪里过夜时，我就睡在他两个房间中的一个房间的沙发上。我对这家旅馆着迷，因为它是一家福利旅馆，里面住着许多老年人。

问：你1970年代的许多电影（除了《家书》）都以房间为中心。是什么让你如此迷恋这些狭小而私密的空间？
阿克曼：封闭的空间。如果你看我在特拉维夫拍的《那里》（Là-bas，2006），你会明白，从某种意义上说，房间既是一种保护，也是一个牢笼。作为二战幸存者的第二代孩子——也就是说，我的母亲曾被关押在集中营里——我在某种程度上延续了“牢笼”这一概念。我总是把自己置于牢笼之中，但我总会开个小门打破它，然后去拍电影！但在某种程度上，我处于一种奇怪的状态：既喜欢我的牢笼，又热爱它，同时也憎恨它，与它对抗。但当自由变得过于接近时，我又会害怕，然后回到牢笼中。

这是一种精神分析式的解释，但从电影的角度来看，我喜欢的是墙壁、线条。我拍摄时总是将摄影机放在我的视线高度，然后正对着画面。这种方式既具有抽象影像的力量，又保留了具象的现实感。在《蒙特雷旅馆》中尤为明显。如果你看看那些走廊，它们是线条，但同时它们也是走廊，渐渐地你会忘记它们的具体形态，只看到线条、色彩和材质。这就是为什么我对室内拍摄如此着迷——门、光线、走廊，它们立即形成了一个框架。

问：在《蒙特雷旅馆》中，我们看到了一系列的走廊，之后偶然出现了一个小小的窗户，可以窥见远处的一辆微小的汽车。
阿克曼：是的，非常极简。但在这样的电影里，这就像是一个重大事件。它比一场车祸更重要。

问：另一个“重大事件”是结尾的高潮——哈德逊河的景象。
阿克曼：是的，它产生了一种效果。在如今的许多电影里，制作者需要不断地增加更大的“冲击”才能制造影响。但我呢？几乎什么都不做，就能让观众产生这种感受。

《8月15日》（Le 15/8）

巴黎的八月中旬（片名即日期：8月15日），阳光洒进一间静谧的公寓，一位年轻女子在其中独自交谈、思考、反思自己的生活、日常琐事和细小事件。摄影机用长时间的固定镜头捕捉她的身影和动作，随着她在房间内移动，光影的变化展现出一个夏日白昼的流转。





Hotel Monterey
On the spur of the moment Akerman left Brussels for New York. 'Along with Pierrot le fou, that was the determining factor in my cinematographic existence.
I saw Brakhage's films there, but most importantly, Michael Snow's. His films work exclusively on the language of the cinema, without any story or sentiment... it is language itself, without parasites, without the possibility of identification.'
Akerman lived in New York for about a year and a half between 1971 and 1974, interspersed with several trips back to Europe. Almost immediately she met Babette Mangolte, who became her friend, her mentor and her gateway to the New York avant-garde film, theatre and music scene, as well as the cinematographer on all her films of this period except Le 15/8 (1973) and Je, tu, il, elle. Mangolte was eager to try out new techniques and equipment to fit Akerman's conceptions and had the contacts Akerman needed to continue to make no-budget films largely using borrowed equipment and volunteers.
Akerman made two stunning short films during this first New York trip - La Chambre and Hotel Monterey, both experimental in the American sense of minimal filmmaking. La Chambre (1972) is a ten-minute silent directly influenced by Snow in which a camera surveys a small apartment in a continuous circular movement, sometimes reversing direction. But the departure from Snow is evident in the presence of a young woman, Akerman herself, who lies in a bed and looks directly at 'us' (the visual field occupied by the camera). Though neither distance nor pacing is changed when the woman enters the field of vision, each time we see her she performs a simple action (she rocks back and forth, she eats an apple.... The room is suffused with a beautiful natural light that also offsets the potential austerity.
Hotel Monterey is much more ambitious. Made the following year, it takes an hour to describe a low-cost residence hotel and its inhabitants in a way that endows the off-screen space inhabited by the camera with a felt presence that is never associated with any person or character. Akerman described the narrative progression as 'an ascent through space and time' beginning on the ground floor in the evening and ending on the roof at dawn. The camera moves inexorably through the lobby, into the elevator as the doors open and close on different floors, down corridors that are often empty and sometimes into a room where someone might be sitting. The sense of waiting without a specific objective is overwhelming, something like Edward Hopper's paintings, to which Jeanne Dielman would be compared. 'I was shocked when I saw that hotel,' Akerman said. 'If I had made something right away, it would have been like a news report. But I thought about that hotel for six months: through that mise en scene and that work on language, I attained ten times more truth.' Hotel Monterey also brought Akerman together with Delphine Seyrig, one of the icons of the French nouvelle vague who was part of the jury that awarded it a prize at the Festival de Nancy in the summer of 1973. And Seyrig made Jeanne Dielman possible.
Janet Bergstrom, Sight and Sound, November 1999

Chantal Akerman on 'Hotel Monterey'
Where was the Hotel Monterey?
It was on 96th Street and Broadway, I think. It's been destroyed.
What was your relationship to the hotel?
I met a Japanese guy who was living there. And sometimes when I didn't know where to sleep, I slept on the sofa in one of his two rooms. I was fascinated because it was a welfare hotel, with many old people living there.
Many of your films from the 1970s, with the exception of News from Home, are defined by rooms. What so fascinated you about these very small, intimate spaces?
Closed spaces. I think if you see the film I did in Tel Aviv, Là-bas (2006), you will understand that, in a way, the room is a protection, but it's also a jail. And as a child of the second generation, which means that my mum was in the camps, ! perpetuate the jail thing. I would always put myself in a jail, but I always have a little doorway that I break, and then I make movies! But in a way, l'm in a kind of strange [situation]: always liking my jail, loving my jail, and hating it, and fighting against it, but then, when it's getting too close to freedom, l'm afraid, [and go back] to my jail.
That's a kind of psychoanalytic explanation, but also in terms of movies, what ! like is the walls, those lines. The way I shoot, I always put the camera at my height, and then I do it straightforward. It has the strength of an abstract image and of a concrete image. That's very much the case in Hotel Monterey. If you see those corridors, they are lines, but there is also a corridor, and suddenly you forget it's a corridor, it's only lines, and colour, and material. That's why I'm also fascinated by shooting inside - doors, lights, corridors. You have immediately a frame.
After we see so many corridors in Hotel Monterey, there are moments when we see just a tiny bit of window, from which we can see a microscopic car below.
Yes, very minimal. And it's like a big event. It's more important than a car crash.
Another big event is the grand finale: the view of the Hudson River.
It gives an effect. When you see all those films with many big effects, they need to make more and more and more to give you any kind of impression. But me, with almost nothing, I can give you that impression.
Interview by Melissa Anderson, movingimagesource.us, 19 January 2010
Le 15/8
Mid-August in Paris (the title is a date: August 15) in a sunny, quiet apartment a young woman talks, thinks, reflects about herself, everyday life and little events in a long, uninterrupted monologue. The camera pictures her and her gestures in long, fixed shots moving around the rooms, the space, the light and shadows of a summer day.
