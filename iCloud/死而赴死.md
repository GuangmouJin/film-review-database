《最终死去的人》（The Man Who Finally Died）


#BFI 长评-何去何从。改编自1959年英国同名电视连续剧，剧情隐喻比较强，不合理的情节看的有些疲惫，背景是冷战时期，悬疑惊悚感塑造的蛮强的，但内核讲的似乎是身份认同和父权崩塌，呈现的是战后国家的自我认同。那种对于父亲状态的执着，德国身份并逃去英国的背景和德国当时的分裂状态都给我这种感受。战后的德国科学家的复活与死亡带有着很强的隐喻，而在这个大背景下也能看出导演的倾向。男主的身份背景与目标是很鲜明的，以个体的遭遇反馈的似乎也是整个民族和国家的集体遭遇。复活则是带有明显的对于身份的找回，而回到真相，似乎这部分的身份或者历史记忆则是被更强大的“时代背景”所操控，个体们是无力的，结尾处那种对“父亲”身份延续的坚持也是他仅能做的了。视听整体的表现还行，部分剪辑突兀，不过感觉对于剧情推进相对高效。

**影片背景与拍摄**
导演昆廷·劳伦斯（Quentin Lawrence）的本片改编自1959年同名电视剧，原剧中由迈克尔·古德里夫（Michael Goodliffe）饰演乔·纽曼。斯坦利·贝克选角恰当，出色地表现了他对父亲命运的困惑以及后来竭力保护一位被追捕者的挣扎。在接近高潮的一幕中，纽曼对一位被通缉的火箭专家苦苦劝说：“我只知道我一直被欺骗、被戏弄、被打，还被当作麻风病人一样对待，而我根本不明白原因。现在我懂了——我父亲给了你一个机会，而我要确保你抓住这个机会！”


库欣在电影宣传时谈到自己为何频频出演恐怖片：“人们总把我当作怪物，一副血从眼睛里滴下来的样子。当然，这是我自找的。我想重回影坛，脱离长时间的电视工作，于是接演了《科学怪人》。”有趣的是，25年后（1987年），库欣饰演冯·布雷希特的片段被用于霍尔斯滕啤酒的电视广告，新旧画面拼接，配合演员格里夫·里斯·琼斯（Griff Rhys Jones）的对白，使库欣借对话“叫他去地窖找葡萄酒”，并通过替身拉开暗门“处理”拒绝的人，颇具黑色幽默。

**导演与风格评述（Barry Forshaw）**

相比之下，《最终死去的人》虽然阵容豪华（贝克、库欣、泽特林、埃里克·波特曼、奈杰尔·格林），但影片整体仍显零散，更多依赖演员阵容的光芒，而非导演手法的高明。

**当年评价（Kine Weekly，1963年10月24日）**
这是一部扎实、专业的作品，虽然节奏略显缓慢，但剧情清晰、明星效应和“英国制造”标签，再加上不错的片名，使其具备双场放映的商业潜力。

影片以神秘性为主线，这种效果从头到尾保持得很好。“红鲱鱼”设置得恰到好处，真正反派的身份也隐藏得极好。斯坦利·贝克饰演的纽曼一角极具张力，冲破层层障碍，几乎导致自我毁灭。泽特林、库欣与波特曼组成的“可疑三人组”也展现出紧张气氛。导演虽无炫技，却恰到好处，摄影也训练有素，完成了本片所需的全部效果。

The Man Who Finally Died
When Joachim Newman receives a call to come to the aid of his estranged father, he finds himself caught up in a tangled web of intrigue. As a devious widow, Zetterling has some terrific scenes with her partner in crime, played by Peter Cushing, but Stanley Baker steals the show as the angst-ridden son.
bfi.org.uk
Director Quentin Lawrence's The Man Who Finally Died had been adapted from the 1959 television series of the same name starring Michael Goodliffe in the role of Joe Newman. Stanley Baker is well cast and excellently expresses his frustration of not knowing what happened to his father and later of trying to protect a hunted man. In a strong scene near the climax, Newman desperately tries to reason freedom with a wanted rocket expert: 'All I know is that I've been lied to, tricked, beaten up and treated like a leper for reasons | didn't understand. I understand them now; my father gave you a chance and I'm going to see you take it!' Peter Cushing has a considerable role although is basically there as a red herring until his real motives are revealed towards the conclusion. His determination to protect the life of a wanted man from the Russians is so convincing that he winds up looking like a villain when contronted by Newman.
In a way, Cushing's portrayal of Von Brecht is similar to his portrayal of Dr Namaroff two years later in Hammer's The Gorgon. Baker and Cushing, who had shared scenes in 1959's Violent Playground, interact wonderfully and provide much tension for the mysterious plot. In the film's publicity, Cushing explained his current stint in horror movies: 'People will always think of me as sort of monsterish, with teeth and blood dripping from every eyeball. Of course, I brought it upon myself; I wanted to get back into films after a long spell on TV, so l accepted the part of Frankenstein.' An interesting note about this film is that 25 years later in 1987, footage of Cushing as Von Brecht was used in a Holsten Pils television commercial. New footage was spliced in with actor Griff Rhys Jones who while describing the product is told by Cushing to check his basement for some vintage wine. When Jones refuses, a double for Cushing pulls a trap door lever on the helpless Jones.
Christopher Gullo, In All Sincerity... Peter Cushing (2004)
The name of Quentin Lawrence is primarily known (if at all) to those with a knowledge of the more obscure byways of British cinema, and his reputation can hardly be said to match that of more stellar names in the genre. But in a relatively small body of work, Lawrence was able to produce films of a certain individuality and even panache, while always working within the constraints (budgetary and otherwise) of commercial cinema. After some experience in films in the late 1940s, Lawrence (who was born in Gravesend, Kent) began to make a mark in television, with two challenging (if crudely made) science fiction serials to his credit, The Strange World of Planet X in 1956 and The Trollenberg Terror in the same year (he was later to helm the cinema version of the latter for Baker & Berman films, retaining much of the original's eccentric quality despite the restrictions of low-budget special effects). The first film on which he was able to display his conscientiously acquired filmmaking credentials was the taut Cash on Demand in 1961, a highly effective adaptation of a television play by Jacques Gillies in which Lawrence's economical direction is immeasurably aided by the nonpareil acting of Peter Cushing and André Morell. Lawrence's next assignment promised much, not least for its boasting of a truly impressive cast which (in retrospect) might have appeared absolutely sure-fire in producing a memorable crime drama. But despite several highly commendable elements, The Man Who Finally Died remains a thing of shreds and patches, intermittently successful and owing more to its copper-bottomed cast (Stanley Baker, Peter Cushing, Mai Zetterling, Eric Portman and Nigel Green) than to Lawrence's efficient (if relatively uninspired) contribution.
Barry Forshaw, British Crime Film: Subverting the Social Order (Palgrave Macmillan, 2012)
A contemporary review
This is a sound, workmanlike job, a shade slow at times, but the clearly told story, combined with certain star values, the British label and a good title make it a reasonable proposition. Worthwhile double bill.
The whole production depends upon mystery for its effect and this is well developed and sustained throughout. The red herrings are well distributed and the identity of the real villain is excellently kept. Stanley Baker, as Joe Newman, gives a craggy performance, crashing through other people's susceptibilities almost to his own disaster, and Mai Zetterling, Peter Cushing and Eric Portman are a suitably suspicious trio of conspirators, whilst Niall McGinnis disarms as the wily insurance man. The direction, without thrills or fuss, is all that is asked of this kind of film, and the camera has been well trained.
Kine Weekly, 24 October 1963
