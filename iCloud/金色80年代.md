# 金色80年代


《黄金八十年代》——香特尔·阿克曼的商场爱情喜剧与消费社会寓言

影片在形式上明显借鉴了雅克·德米的歌舞片风格，画面也让我想到了明奈利的那部《一个美国人在巴黎》。电影对于爱情的诠释也让我想起了叔本华那句悲观主义的言语，人生就像钟摆，在痛苦与无聊之间摇摆，无法获得真正的满足和幸福，爱情似乎也一样。

视觉上，比较喜欢的是镜头运动，色彩，布景和服饰，整个环境的构成非常梦幻，镜头随着角色间的互动变化，不太喜欢的可能还是剪辑和构图，有些地方剪的非常突兀，好像还有穿帮，但不得不提那明艳的色彩，真的是太美了。

听觉上，歌曲的部分非常好，之前的纪录片作品《八十年代》已经有展现这部分内容了，但是还是同样的配音听起来怪怪的，口型对不上还是有些难受。



1. 电影背景与核心主题

2. 视听风格：向雅克·德米的致敬

	•	Mado，一个理发师学徒，爱上了Robert，然而Robert却倾心于Lili——一个性感、爱玩但不可靠的女人，同时还是Monsieur Jean的情妇。
	•	Robert最终应该接受现实，像他的父亲Monsieur Schwartz一样，为责任与家庭牺牲自己的爱情。然而，就像他母亲Jeanne曾经爱上一位美军士兵Eli一样，爱情的理想与现实总是充满矛盾。
	•	Jeanne多年后再次遇见Eli，对方希望她能和自己私奔，但她最终选择留在现有的生活中。
	•	购物中心内的闲散男性、洗发女工和路人构成了一种希腊合唱团式的观察者，不断在一旁评判和评论这些角色的抉择，像是在观看一场无法逃离的戏剧。

购物中心就像一个鱼缸，每一个角色的举动都被其他人观察和评判。这是一场永无止境的表演，而他们中的大多数人都渴望逃离，却又无法真正走出这个循环。

4. 角色关系与对消费社会的讽刺

影片中的角色们看似渴望改变自己的生活，但他们构建的所有梦想，实际上都是为了让自己更好地留在原地。
	•	Lili厌倦了Monsieur Jean，最终选择和Jeanne拒绝的Eli私奔。然而，仅仅两个月后，她又带着夸张的伪装（头巾、墨镜）回到了商场，最终在Robert的婚礼上再次制造混乱。
	•	酒吧女孩Sylvie一直收到她远在加拿大的男友写来的信件，她对这种远距离的幻想感到满足。有人问：“他讨厌加拿大吗？”她回答：“是的。”那人再问：“所以他要回来了吗？”她微笑着回答：“还没有。”对于Sylvie来说，这种**“等待”本身就是一种逃避现实的方式**，而她的世界会因男友真正回来而崩塌。

所有人都在这个封闭空间里上演着爱情、欲望、责任与牺牲的戏剧，但他们的感情表达充满重复性和模式化。人们借用现成的浪漫幻想来填补生活的空白，就像他们从购物中心里随意挑选衣服一般。

5. 购物中心的“缺席中心”

在这场消费主义寓言中，最核心的两个角色——Schwartz夫妇，反而是最“缺席”的存在：
	•	Jeanne（德尔菲娜·塞里格饰）的微笑一半是为了推销服饰，另一半是因为她已经选择了从生活中抽离。
	•	Monsieur Schwartz（Charles Denner饰）衣着考究，却对商场的空间布局充满控制欲，他的商业野心与焦虑不安的姿态形成了鲜明对比。他不断游走于商场内，就像一个失去目标的统治者。

这对夫妇既是商场的中心，也是最“隐形”的存在。他们的情感早已被商业现实所取代，只有当Jeanne走进电影院，在一部老旧的浪漫电影前倾诉心事时，她才暂时找回了曾经的激情。

6. 结语：歌舞片形式下的社会批判

《黄金八十年代》看似轻松欢乐，实则隐藏着对现代社会的深刻批判。
	•	购物中心不仅是一个商业空间，更是一个社会关系的封闭系统。
	•	角色们的爱情、梦想和幻想都在这个封闭空间中循环，最终没有人真正离开。
	•	电影在喜剧、音乐和讽刺之间保持平衡，使其成为阿克曼作品中最具娱乐性但又不失深度的一部。

最终，《黄金八十年代》并不需要被过度分析，它就像理发店里泡沫般的洗发水，轻盈而流畅，带着机智、温情和悲伤，展现了当代人如何在消费主义的环境中迷失自我。即使观众对歌舞片不甚感兴趣，这部作品仍然值得一看，因为它让人们在享受音乐和幽默的同时，也能思考自己在现代世界中的位置。





Golden Eighties
The screening on Monday 3 February will include a Q&A with cellist and composer Sonia Wieder-Atherton
Shopping precincts are as good an indication as any of our moral condition.
Just as the Passage Choiseul, where in the early years of the century Céline's anti-hero watched his parents sinking deeper and deeper with every passing moment, identified the wellsprings of petit-bourgeois resentment, so now in the 1980s, from Las Vegas to Les Halles, the tinted marbles, the hothouse plants and the fountains create an environment that literally encapsulates the hedonism of a life lived permanently in a conservatory. These people are sheltered from the elements, maintained at the perfect temperature, insulated from the inconveniences of the outside world but with all the advantages and semblances of it (shops, cafes, cinemas, passers-by...). The shopping mall, today, is the nexus of postmodernism and consumerism.
To judge from its title Golden Eighties, Chantal Akerman's film is an explicit comment on the malaise of our civilisation, a sidelong and witty look at our aspirations and moral values. After the years of lead come the years of gold-dust! These neatly shod female feet which populate the title sequence, briskly criss-crossing the strawberry-coloured slabs of stone, bent on crucial and important missions, these are the locomotive of the economy.
But Golden Eighties has another economy too. It was Jacques Demy who first realised that the enclosed space of a gallery and its symmetrical design is the perfect foil for a musical, with the constraints of the setting justifying and shaping the choreography of the formation dancing and making the musical romanticism seem more fantastic in contrast. This is Akerman's tribute to Demy, although it does not have the desperation that certainly underlay his last film Une chambre en ville. Golden Eighties is jokey and fast-moving, with one of the liveliest scores, by Marc Herouet, and wittiest sets of lyrics, by Akerman
(alas, not well subtitled), for many years.
But where is the happiness in these golden eighties? This is the sad tale of Mado, the hairdresser's apprentice who loves Robert, son of the owners of the clothes shop opposite, although Robert only wants Lili, Mado's sexy, flirtatious and untrustworthy boss who is Monsieur Jean's mistress. So Robert should make a sacrifice and settle down to a life of effort and responsibility, just as his father Monsieur Schwartz had settled down with Jeanne. But at what cost? For
Jeanne had loved an American Gl called Eli who now, remarkably, comes strolling through the mall, recognises her after all these years and tries to persuade her to come away with him. This is a goldfish bowl in which every move the principals make is observed and maliciously commented on by the chorus of male layabouts and shampoo girls. A continuous, edifying spectacle
that almost everyone dreams of leaving.
The point is, however, they construct their dreams the better to remain where they are. Rather like Marie-France Pisier in Souvenirs d'en France (André
Téchiné, 1975), Lili, terminally bored with Monsieur Jean's attentions, takes off with the Eli whom Jeanne has just rejected, only to return two months later, ludicrously and heavily disguised in headscarf and dark glasses, just in time to seduce Robert and so sabotage his wedding. Sylvie, the girl who runs the bar, is thrilled with an escape that keeps her rooted to the spot, in the form of letters from her boyfriend who has gone to Canada to make his fortune. 'He hates it there,' she says. 'So he's coming back?' they all ask. 'Not yet,' replies

Sylvie with an angelic smile, and her world would clearly be shattered if he did.
All the other characters construct scenarios as unreal as the mall itself, all except Lili are incurable romantics.
Yet even their romantic yearnings ring repetitiously. Monsieur Schwartz's disquisition on the work ethic parrots the pages of L'Expansion, but bears absolutely no relation to reality or the people around him; Lili's sentiments in regard to Monsieur Jean are radically inauthentic; both are typical of the world of the shopping mall: everyone is recycling snatches of sentiment and belief that they have taken off the peg in order to help them stay exactly where they are. Exogamy, it would seem, is virtually impossible, as is thought beyond this infernally constructed spiral.
It is the Schwartz couple who are the absent centre of Golden Eighties.
Delphine Seyrig as the gracefully ageing Jeanne, whose radiant smile is half designed to sell clothes and half the result of her detachment, perfectly incarnates the mentality of caution and prudence, while Charles Denner as
Schwartz, in his over-neat suit, his concern for Lebensraum, his designs on the floor space of Lili's salon, has learned the lessons of modern commerce so well that he embodies its stresses, his grandiose ambitions contrasting markedly
with his slightly pinched appearance and slightly nervous demeanour. Neither is capable of articulating clearly what they want. When Schwartz wants to talk he goes into the mall and paces up and down; when Jeanne wants to talk she goes into the cinema and unburdens her heart in front of a screen playing old, romantic movies. Indeed, when any of the couples in Golden Eighties meet it has to be in a cubicle for they have no space outside the mall itself.
Golden Eighties does not bear thinking about too closely, and indeed does not require it. The film is as frothy as the shampoo in the hairdresser's, a film in a much more popular and accessible mode than some of Akerman's earlier works, and a film which contrives to be witty and tender and sad, despite its devastatingly accurate observation of the way we live now. And if the musical genre is one which many audiences traditionally find difficult, this film should go a long way towards effecting a reconciliation.
Jill Forbes, Sight and Sound, Spring 1987
