我们正在进行一场更大规模的对重要作品的致敬，在这里我们会去看围绕着她和 Peter Wollen、以及《谜之斯芬克斯》这部影片的一些影响。而这部作品，也许——我是说，它已经成为一部标志性的电影，如果你们当中有些人还没看过，非常欢迎在这个展映季中来观看。它就紧随这场放映之后。而在这些“语境计划”（programmes in context）中让我印象深刻的一点是：这部电影是在 Laura 那篇标志性的〈视觉快感〉论文之后，或者说介于那篇文章和之前的电影《Penthesilea》之间。但与其说是在看好莱坞的形式，我会说，这个特定的项目其实在看的是某些类型的政治性、非正式的、前卫电影，是如何为《谜之斯芬克斯》的创作提供背景和信息的——至少是其中一些，而不是全部。因此这给了我们一个机会，去延展并观察当时独立与前卫电影文化中的其他元素。而《谜之斯芬克斯》在许多方面，都成为当时独立电影文化的一个真正的触点或试金石，去审视女性在父权社会中的经验，观察不同类型的劳动，以及通过更政治性的、前卫性的形式所带来的可能性。或许我们能从中找到与《夜间清洁工》（Nightcleaners）类似的平行关系。但它确实是一部关键的标志性作品，承载了很多意义。我们在这部电影身上放置了很多东西，因为它存在于我们所熟知的那些历史中。

所以，这其实是这次 fellowship（奖助）中特别的一部分：V&A（或 VFI？原文 V F5 不确定）非常慷慨地举办了一整个 Laura 作品的回顾展。而刚刚做介绍的 Will 提议，我们应该做一个“《谜之斯芬克斯》在语境中”的单元。最初我其实并不确定应该如何处理，因为对我来说，《谜之斯芬克斯》的语境非常、非常来自于 70 年代中期英国独立、实验、前卫、政治化、社会主义倾向的电影运动和电影人。如果没有那个运动，我不认为《谜之斯芬克斯》会存在。

但强调这一点之后，我又觉得，也许转向一个更国际化的视角，会更有趣，也更不那么显而易见。而当我们到达这个思路时，对我来说回想那个特定的历史节点变得非常迷人。所以我选择的那些电影，我觉得它们都具有示范性，但同样关键的是，它们都在 70 年代早期进入英国，并带来一种挑战“什么是电影”这个概念的电影。它们要求以一种全新的方式思考电影，它们从自己所在的国家与文化背景出发，来对我们发声。

所以在这里以一种粗略的并置方式呈现，它们在彼得和我心中留下了不可磨灭的印象。尤其从回望来看，它们具有一种示范性，似乎代表了思想如何伴随着电影旅行，如何代表那一刻的变革。

而我所选出的电影——其实一点也不难挑选，一旦这个思路到位，具体的影片就很快排列上来了：

首先，就是今天我们要看的来自“新美国电影运动”（New American Cinema Movement）的 Hollis Frampton 的 1970 年作品《Zorn’s Lemma》。

然后是来自巴西“新电影运动”（Cinema Novo）的 Glauber Rocha 的 1969 年《Antonio das Mortes》。

接着是来自旧金山“新闻片集体”（Newsreel collective）的作品，伴随着他们反越战的行动，是 1971 年的《The Woman’s Film》。

还有 Straub-Huillet 的 1968 年《The Bridegroom, the Comedienne and the Pimp》（新娘、女喜剧演员与皮条客）。

以及 Godard 和 Jean-Pierre Gorin 的 1968 年《Vladimir et Rosa》（弗拉基米尔与罗莎）。

现在说得更诚实一点，《Vladimir et Rosa》对我个人来说，并没有像其他几部电影那样具有个人共鸣。但它启发了 Peter 的“反电影”（counter cinema）论文，而这篇论文对我们来说是一个巨大而重要的文本。它与我的〈视觉快感与叙事电影〉并列，是我们思想转折的两篇文本——从理论地思考电影，走向制作理论性的电影。

所以尽管《Vladimir et Rosa》没有那种个人共鸣，但它对 Peter 的论文非常关键，而那篇论文又在我们后来开始做电影时一直是基础性的。

这些电影虽然大多只相隔两三年，但都是在 70 年代初来到英国的替代性放映网络中出现的，这要归功于那些新的发行公司——因为我要强调一个显而易见的事实：你之所以能看到新的、实验性的、困难的电影，是因为有发行公司愿意冒险把它们带进来，在艰难的条件下放映它们。

于是，像 Polithechnic 电影社（Polytechnic Film Society？原文 Pollychore 不确定），后来与 The Other Cinema 合并，引进了格劳伯·罗查、激进电影和前卫作品；伦敦电影制作者合作社（London Filmmakers’ Co-op）的发行部门则带来了“新美国电影”和无政府主义电影。

然后这些思想通过杂志《Afterimage》开始聚合与成型，它出版了所有这些电影的文章——除了《The Woman’s Film》之外。

Peter Sainsbury 在一次编辑评论里写道：“Straub-Huillet 和 Godard 可能看起来是一对不太可能的组合，但在熟悉的意识形态表面之下，他们在试图建立一种不再再现、反映、解释、象征或寓言，而是进行探问的电影。这种新电影是认识论式的。”
这深深抓住了我的想象力，也吸引我去注意电影如何以其自身非常特殊的物质方式成为一种认识论实践。

《Antonio das Mortes》是《Afterimage》第一期的封面。尽管如此，这些电影对我们造成的影响，我至今仍记得它们的力量——那种把形式之美与激进思想结合在一起的力量。

我喜欢把《Zorn’s Lemma》非常严格的形式性，与《Antonio das Mortes》那种完全不同的严格形式性并置——后者是大量移动的群体、不断移动的摄影机、戏剧性等等。

我还能继续谈下去，但我相信你们都想看电影了。所以我只说几个对我来说《Zorn’s Lemma》重要的点：

第一，它有三个部分：第一部分、一个长而复杂的中心段落、以及最后一部分。而了解我和 Peter 的电影的人，会看到一种结构上的呼应——一种持久的对称兴趣。

第二，在主要部分里，影片对图案、格子、系统、类别、还有作为图像的文字的迷恋——尤其是系统、格子等——让我们所有观众去解析这个系统是什么意思、如何运作。我不会再多说，因为虽然我不相信“剧透”这种说法，但《Zorn’s Lemma》是我真的不想剧透的一部——理解这个系统是它的巨大乐趣之一。

第三，Frampton 大量使用引用和再现。影片从最开头的黑暗——用于铺陈模式——到最后雪地中的光亮，有一种特别的美。而在第三部分，他引用了中世纪学者 Robert Grosseteste 的文字——他是重要的教会政治人物，同时也对科学与思想的互动感兴趣，他写过一篇关于“光”的论文，被 Frampton 翻译、编辑并使用在影片里。因此这种引用与“光”的关联非常重要。

最后一点，最显而易见的：这是一部用 16mm 拍摄的电影，并且带着十足的 16mm 特征。在最后的部分，他使用了 16mm 卷片的延展特性，就像我们后来在一些影片中做的那样。你会看到从一卷到另一卷的切换——我记得最后的段落里有三次切换。

而今晚放映的真正美妙之处在于：这部影片是以 16mm 来放映的。这对我来说非常特别。

Hollis Frampton on 'Zorns Lemma'
I first began using a movie camera at the end of Fall of 1962. At that time I was being systematically forced into cinema in a way by my still work. I'd been working for a long time in series, sometimes long series, and there were things that began to trouble me about the still series. Such as, if you have a burch of photographs that you believe cohere even in book space, let alone on a gallery wall or something like that, there's no way to determine the order in which they're seen, or the amount of time for which each one is seen, or to establish the possibility of a repeat ... so that had already made me think of the film. As a kind of ordering and control, a way of handling stills.
Then at the same time I was thinking a lot about the standard paradoxes about photography. You have all these spatial illusions, tactile illusions even, whereas there is a cultural reflex somewhere to believe that when you're looking at something it's real. Let's say, Even if that is the impression you're assembling only from the barest of abstract kind of thing... and at the same time the thing is undeniably absolutely flat, it doesn't have impasto, it has nothing, it is perfectly superficial, it only has an outside. That paradox seemed to me most strongly embodied in some stills I had made of words, environmental words, where the word as a graphic element that brought one back to reading (and being conscious of looking at a mark on a surface) emphasised the flatness of the thing. And at the same time the tactile and spatial hints that were compounded with it, the presence of the word within the image, were full of illusion. So that I'd begun to make a bunch of these still photographs. And I thought, 'Well, I'll make them into a film', and I shot better than 2,000 words in 35mm still. With the idea that I was going to just put them on a stand and shoot them. And I did a little of that as a matter of fact. It's perfectly dead It was simply going absolutely no place... Well, that's how the thing began, as a concern with that spatial paradox or set of spatial paradoxes, and the kind of malaise that it generated as you get farther and farther into it. There stil are a few of those original black and white photographs. They all have some real object lying on top of it. The oldest one is the word fox', from the old Brockdyn Fox theatre, that I think is the first one I made ... dark blue sky, some little stran flowers or paper flowers on top of it as a memento to the sentimental nature of the occasion.
There are 3 parts, first part is 5 minutes long, soundtrack with no image, a woman recites in a schoolteacherly voice 24 rhymes from the Bay State Primer which was designed to teach late 18th-century and early 19th-century children the alphabet. The primer is oriented towards death, towards accepting authority, a kind of rote learning in the dark, I suppose. The second section opens with an enunciation of the Roman alphabet itself, with as little context as possible. The letters are made of metal, actually they were typed on tin foil and photographed in one-to-one closeup. That's how it developed.
In the body of the second section, the main section of the fim, which is 45 minutes long, there are 2,700 one-second cuts, one second segments, 24 frame segments, of which about half consist of words; the words were alphabetised. The reason for alphabetising them really was to make the order of them as random as possible, that is to say to avoid using my own taste and making little puns out of them or something like that, much as the encyclopaedists of the Enlightenment thought they could somehow categorise all human knowledge or a large part of it under the initial letter of the name of the subject. So that it just happens that quaternions are found in volume so-and-so under 'Q' - it's crazy when you think about it.
As it is, it does generate some intelligible phrases, some odd pairings anyway.
Let's see, there's a kind of Hart Crane line early on that reads 'nectar of pain', there's a phrase of Victorian pornography, limp member', which sticks out like a sore thumb, a limp thumb or something - straight out of My Secret Life or A Man and a Maid. Well, that happens of course - the words were mostly, not all of them but mostly, shot from the environment. They're store signs, posters and so on. And one finds out very quickly that very many words begin with c' and 's' and so forth, very few begin with 'x' or 'q'. One quickly begins to run out of 'q's' and 'x's' and 'z's'. What happens here is that essentially one is using a chance operation. What always happens when using a chance operation is that along with generating some things that you want it also generates holes. Fate has problems. It's always true. And one has to think a great deal more about the holes, having taken care of the operations.
Well, I don't know at what point the notion of substituting other images for words as they disappear in each alphabetic slot supervened. Particularly, I first thought all the images would be different. It would be what John Simon called (fake German accent) "Just a jumble of imaches' ... And for quite a long time I held that notion of the film. The greatest bulk of time was really shopping in Manhattan for the words themselves. I can't say I did it day after day for seven years, but I did it for seven years, and I shot actually four times as many words as I used, as well as duplications. The word 'shot' comes up again and again; I think I used the word 'shot' five times. From which to choose essentially. Some just didn't work out for one reason or another. Rather than make 1,350 entirely separate shots. I didn't want to use stock footage. I could achieve essentially the same degree of randomness by using 24 and by dissecting them, exploding them, and once that occurred to me, the possibility of developing an iconography ... as separate from the words and what they were doing and so forth, presented itself. From then it was easy, I did shoot some images that I did not use in fact. There's one image I remember of sawing wood, sawing a board, that I tried several times to get together. Many of the images are in some sense sculptural, to do with kind of generative acts concerning
3-dimensional space rather than 2-dimensional space.
Hollis Frampton interviewed by Peter Gidal, 24 May 1972
Published in Peter Gidal (ed), Structural Film Anthology (BFI, 1976)
