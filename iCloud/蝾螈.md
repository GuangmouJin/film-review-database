# 蝾螈

#BFI 瑞士新浪潮作品，内核仍然带着极强的社会批判性，影片通过对于女主角的刻画讽刺了当时瑞士社会对于女性的边缘化，当然其中的保守主义的批判也是非常的明显的。

结构上，这可能是我最近看过比较复杂的结构了，感觉是多重现实的嵌套、影片在某种程度上可以被看作是戏中戏的结构，采用不同视角和解读方式形成了一种关于现实与虚构的对话。电影在前半部分给我的感觉是通过两位作家Pierre和Paul的视角来拼凑女主的生活，他们一个采用现实主义的调查方式，另一个依赖想象和文学化的诠释。在这一过程中，女主始终处于被观察和被建构的地位，这在一定程度上造成了她的客体化，她被塑造成一个神秘，反叛和不受控的女性形象，而非一个有完整自我表达权利的主体。然而，看到一半我突然想到影片开场其实就是女主角的旁白，其实这恰恰打破了客体化叙事的结构。她开始就是主动讲述自己的故事，而不是让别人代她发声，这一转变赋予了她主体性，使得观众从作家们的窥探中解脱，直接面对她的表达。电影的结尾并没有明确交代女主的真实经历，而是将“真相”留在观众的想象之中，进一步强调了现实与虚构之间的辩证关系。但回到影片开场，枪击的“真相”和女主的“行走”都比较直接的展现了故事的原貌，这里也能比较清晰的感觉其中对于媒体的讽刺，但当回想起女主的独白，又不得不让我怀疑，影片的第一个镜头是构建还是回忆。这种感觉非常的美妙，一方面以两位作家在尝试拼凑女主生活的过程所产生的多种可能性来思考其事实性，另一方面在思考这些可能是被构建出来的“真相”，而非实际发生的事件（让我想到了《洞》和《绕道》这两部作品），这种现实与虚构的流动性实在是太迷人了。

角色上，女主的角色是十分鲜活的，其实那个形象让我想到了《雏菊》中的角色形象，甚至某种程度上他们是同一种表达，非常喜欢以童话，笑话或音乐或来掩盖现实的做法，感觉她也像是一个导演来出品自己的作品，也让现实与虚幻的界限更加模糊了。影片中的“进厂”部分的女主客体化的呈现也是很符合对于资本主义的讽刺，后续的反凝视我认为做的也蛮好的，尤其是对于权力的展现，并没有把女主放在一个客体的位置，而是利用跳脱的形式讽刺了男性对于女性的剥削和控制。影片的片名也是隐喻表达了个体在制度化社会中生存的可能性，即便处于极端环境，依然能够顽强地活下去。

视听上，很多运动长镜头拍的非常美，整体也蛮符合新浪潮的特点，非线性叙事，跳剪和记录片式的镜头，黑白影像还是蛮压抑。旁白还是蛮有趣。

制作与影响

坦纳据说以2.6万英镑的预算拍摄了本片，使用16毫米胶片，并在短时间内完成，随后放大到35毫米上映。坦纳在瑞士电视台的纪录片制作经验，在《蝾螈》中得到了充分体现，使影片在有限资源下展现出极高的完成度。这部影片的成就，为英国等欧洲国家的独立电影人提供了有益的启示。

电影深入探讨了个体如何在资本主义社会中寻求自我认同。萝丝蒙德作为一个拒绝被标签化、反抗体制的女性形象，成为当时社会反叛精神的象征。


	4.	存在主义思考
影片对现代社会生活的荒诞性提出了质疑，萝丝蒙德的放荡不羁实际上是对一种更真实生活方式的渴望，与瑞士社会高度制度化、守旧的现实形成强烈对比。

叙事风格与表现手法
	•	非线性叙事与松散结构
电影没有一个传统的戏剧冲突，而是采用片段化、碎片化的方式呈现人物和事件，给观众提供了开放式的解读空间。

	•	黑白摄影
冷静的黑白影像强化了影片的哲学气质，同时增强了对社会现实的疏离感。

结局解读

影片的结局保持了开放性，并没有明确交代萝丝蒙德的未来，也没有给出所谓的“真相”。这进一步强化了影片对现实的不确定性和多重解读的强调，观众被引导去思考个体与社会、自由与责任的关系。

总体评价

《蝾螈》是一部充满思想深度的电影，它以轻盈而疏离的方式，批判了瑞士社会的保守性，并探讨了个体如何在体制化的环境中追寻自由。影片在人物塑造上极具先锋精神，并对当时的欧洲新浪潮风格进行了独特的本土化处理。

如果你对新浪潮电影感兴趣，尤其是瑞士新浪潮的现实主义表达和哲学思考，那么《蝾螈》无疑是一部值得深思的佳作。


《蝾螈》（The Salamander）影评翻译

阿兰·坦纳在影片中告诉我们，**“蝾螈”**是一种具有吸引力但有毒的蜥蜴，它可以穿越火焰而毫发无损。然而，坦纳在这里所指的“火”并非激情或灾难，而是工作、人与人之间的交流、景观的单调乏味，这种乏味犹如一种自然现象般令人窒息，而这种压抑在瑞士社会中尤为明显。

从阶级、地理和性别的角度来看，片中的主人公萝丝蒙德（Rosemonde）——正是片名所指的“蝾螈”——出生在一个充满压抑与单调的世界。她是一个有11个孩子的农村家庭的第七个孩子，他们居住在一个寒冬持续六个月的荒凉山谷。12岁时，她被送往日内瓦，寄居在一个沉迷于战争的中产阶级小叔家，成为他的家庭佣人。即使是与叔叔的假期也令人难以忍受，但萝丝蒙德最终成功逃离警察的追踪，逃到法国南部蔚蓝海岸。

在此背景下，两位男性角色——皮埃尔（Pierre）和保罗（Paul）——进入了故事。他们计划合作，为电视节目撰写一个基于萝丝蒙德生活的剧本。
	•	皮埃尔（由让-吕克·比多饰）是一名记者，携带着大量录音设备和摄像机，试图通过详尽的调查来拼凑出萝丝蒙德的真实生活；
	•	保罗（由雅克·德尼饰）则是一名作家，他更倾向于通过想象来理解她的故事。

保罗在研究萝丝蒙德的名字时，推测她来自一个大家庭，并迅速拼凑出一个与现实接近的故事。而皮埃尔则固执地通过调查她的过去，解开种种权威人士对她的“控诉”：房东批评她的生活习惯“令人震惊”，雇主指责她“迟到史无前例”，叔叔称她“本性不良”。然而，当皮埃尔最终与萝丝蒙德见面后，他的记者权威被颠覆，不得不屈服于她的要求，陪伴她，建议她，甚至与她共度一个寒冷而乏味的周末。另一方面，保罗最初对参与此事持消极态度，但逐渐变得投入，并最终意识到自己对萝丝蒙德“一无所知”。

保罗说：“记者只是重复他们刚学到的东西，而我是试图理解我已经知道的事物。”最终，两人都放弃了他们的剧本。

叙事方式与主题解读

这种皮兰德娄式（Pirandellian）的叙事结构，展现了“两个（或四个，包括坦纳和合作者约翰·伯格）寻找角色的作者”的处境。坦纳确保观众不会比这两位作家走得更远。影片开头缓慢的慢动作镜头，展现叔叔在擦枪时中枪倒地，而萝丝蒙德惊恐的脸在画面的角落出现，这种视觉处理重现了事件，但并未揭示她的清白或罪责。观众和剧中人物一样，必须等待萝丝蒙德自己决定何时坦白。

每当皮埃尔试图获得萝丝蒙德的故事，她往往选择调高点唱机的音量，或干脆编造一个童话故事。坦纳通过这种方式表明，**真相并非可以直接探究，而是只能通过间接的方式浮现。**类似地，影片的镜头语言也很少正面拍摄角色，而是经常选择从背后、侧面，或以观察的方式展现。音乐时而伴随着场景，时而突然消失，提醒观众他们正在观看一部电影，而非现实。

结论

《蝾螈》是一部精致而深刻的作品，它以轻松而疏离的语调，探讨了身份、性别、社会束缚等主题，同时采用了独特的叙事方式，使观众始终处于一种探索与怀疑的状态。影片不仅批判了瑞士社会的僵化，也以开放式的结局，为观众留下了对自由和真实的无限思考。






The Salamander
A salamander, Alain Tanner tells us during his film, is an attractive but venomous lizard which can go through fire and emerge unscathed. By fire Tanner doesn't mean passion or catastrophe; rather he has in mind a monotony of work, of people, of conversation and landscape (all of them, apparently, particularly Swiss) which is as menacing as any natural phenomenon.
By class, geography and sex, Rosemonde, the salamander of the title, is born to a world of oppressive tedium. Seventh in a rural family of 11 children who live in a bare valley doomed to six months of winter, she is at 12 packed off to a war-obsessed petit bourgeois uncle in Geneva who uses her as a housekeeper. Even holidays with the uncle are unendurable; but Rosemonde, with the police on her tail, manages to escape to the Cote d'Azur. One day as the uncle is obsessively cleaning his gun, he is wounded in the shoulder. He claims that Rosemonde has shot him; she insists the gun went off accidentally.
The case is dropped, and Rosemonde escapes to a superficially independent life of tedious jobs.
So much for case history. At which point enter Pierre and Paul - and a dichotomy which may owe something to Tanner's co-writer, John Berger. They will collaborate on a television script based on Rosemonde's life. Pierre (Jean-Luc Bideau), the journalist, armed with a battery of tape recorders and cameras, will arrive at the facts via an exhaustive investigation of Rosemonde and her life; Paul (Jacques Denis), the writer, will discover the facts in his imagination. Extrapolating from Rosemonde's name (a Saint's Day, therefore a younger child in a large working-class family...), Paul swiftly pieces together a story which, we later discover, closely resembles the real one.
Pierre stubbornly begins a lugubrious inquest into Rosemonde'a past, unleashing a baleful litany of abused authority from a landlady ('Shocking disorder!'), an employer ('Broke all records for lateness!"), and the uncle ('She's a bad lot!'). When Pierre finally meets Rosemonde, his own journalist's authority topples and he finds himself acceding ironically to her requests to feed her, advise her, make love to her, spend a frozen weekend in a dreary hotel near her family. Paul, initially with great reluctance and later with great commitment, finds himself on the same route. Despite its accuracy, he is forced to abandon his script: 'Now I feel I no longer know anything about her.
A journalist repeats what he's just learned. Whereas l'm trying to make sense of what I already know.' Finally both men abandon their scripts.
In this Pirandellian situation of two (or four, with Tanner and Berger) authors in search of a character, Tanner ensures that the audience will be no farther ahead on the trail. The opening slow-motion shot of the old man falling wounded while cleaning his gun, with Rosemonde's frightened, disembodied face in a corner of the frame, visually reproduces the fait divers on which Pierre and Paul will base their story, but gives nothing away on the question of Rosemonde's innocence. We, like Pierre and Paul, must wait until Rosemonde feels like confessing. To Pierre's requests for her life story, Rosemonde replies by turning up the volume on a jukebox or making up a fairytale. The truth is not discoverable by direct enquiry; it can only emerge obliquely. In the same way, Tanner's camera rarely confronts its characters head on. Instead it often locates itself on their backs, peers over their shoulders, or wanders away as they are speaking. Music will set up a scene and then suddenly disappear, startling us into an uncomfortable awareness that we are in a cinema watching a movie. An ironically omniscient commentary often previews what a character will do next, or mockingly describes what we have already seen.
The tone and style of the film - expectations constantly thwarted or ironically fulfilled, scenes apparently (though not in fact) improvised, offhand visual gags (the condom-like sausage skins which Rosemonde fills in her first production line job, the startled reactions of the customers in her next job at a shoe shop when she absentmindedly massages their legs) recalls the freewheeling exercises of the early nouvelle vague. Specifically, Truffaut's shadow lurks throughout the film; Paul and Pierre, the dreamer and the realist, complementary friends and amicable rivals for one woman, might well be stepsons of Jules and Jim. But Rosemonde (played with deadly accuracy by Bulle Ogier), for all her dominance, capriciousness and darting flashes of gaiety or sadness, is no Catherine.
Although she is very aware of her sexuality, it is more likely to play a part in her victimization by society than to help her to rise above it. Disillusioned and a confirmed drifter at 23, Rosemonde resembles Barbara Loden's Wanda; but unlike Loden, Tanner translates his character's lack of ties as freedom. We last see Rosemonde after she has quit her shoe shop job, her face a joyous and confident contrast to the grey, empty faces of the monotonous crowds.
Tanner reputedly made The Salamander on a budget of £26,000, on 16mm, later blown up to 35mm, with a small crew and in a minimum number of days.
It is actually his second feature, following Charles, Dead or Alive, which he made in 1968. But it's worth recalling that Tanner made his debut in England 16 years ago, when with Claude Goretta he made Nice Time, an open-eyed look at Piccadilly Circus which was shown in one of the Free Cinema programmes. After working as an editor at the BBC, he returned to Switzerland when his work permit ran out, since when he has been making documentaries for Swiss television. His television experience shows to advantage in The Salamander, for which he received a partial grant from the Swiss government.
That he could produce this accomplished and original film with such slender resources is a lesson from which British directors might have something to learn.
Victoria Radin, Sight and Sound, Spring 1973
