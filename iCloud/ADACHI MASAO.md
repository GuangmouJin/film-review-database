LETTER TO MY FRENCH SPECTATORS
Adachi Masao (2010)
(Transcription of a video letter projected at the Cinémathèque française on 25 February 2011. Translated from the Japanese by Takahashi Shoko and from French by Ricardo Matos.)
1 am unable to travel to France because Japan does not issue me a passport. Political reasons prevent the authorities from authorising me to leave the country. This concerns not only France, but all European countries, the United States, and so on in short, most countries. I would very much have liked to attend the retrospectives devoted to Wakamatsu Köji and to myself. I submitted a request, but the answer was negative. That is why I cannot come.
For a long time, the films of Wakamatsu Koji and my own were regarded as marginal within Japanese cinema. They were treated as
"heretical" films. For example, partly out of a taste for Orientalism, Europe valued the cinema of Kurosawa Akira and Mizoguchi Kenji.
It then turned its attention to more contemporary cinema-that is, the films of Oshima Nagisa, Yoshida Kijü, and Imamura Shohei-so that interest shifted toward our generation. Over time, Europeans also came to take an interest in "heretical" Japanese cinema. That is how I see it. I do not consider myself a heretic. But if one looks objectively at the position of my works, from the point of view of their content, they can be situated at the extremes. I find it truly remarkable that a retrospective of this kind of cinema is being organised in its entirety.
At the time, in cinema —and in visual culture as a whole, if I may say so-the distinction between what was considered legitimate and what was considered heretical did not exist. We were treated as heretics because of our very radical message. Today, there is no longer any difficulty in conveying this message within the framework of a retrospective. But at the time, this message had force because it responded exactly to the concerns of its moment.
Watching these films again today can make it possible to reconsider the history of Japanese cinema. That is how I conceive this retrospective.
When I was a student, the only place to study cinema was the film department at Nihon University. Schools teaching technical skills were beginning to appear, but I went to university. At that time, the university was a kind of gathering place for passionate students. We were all fascinated by the Surrealist movement; some of us belonged to Neo-Dada groups. Through my association with these comrades, I was strongly influenced by Surrealism, which I came to regard as the most important current of thought shaping me. Both my theoretical and my practical conception of cinema are rooted in this movement. After leaving university, most people began working. After making The Bowl and Closed Vagina, I worked to raise the budget for the tilms we wanted to make, notably by producing advertising spots and documentaries. Of course, these experiences were interesting, but I always kept in mind the need to earn money to finance our own films.
Subsequently, together with my seniors, I founded the "Van Film Science Research Centre," a name that may sound somewhat austere.
There, we developed our ideas and engaged in debate. Meanwhile, the film industry continued to evolve, and we, too, had to commit ourselves seriously. So I knocked on the door of Wakamatsu Köji, who had presented Secrets Behind the Wall at the Berlin Film Festival. The film was treated as a national disgrace. I wrote numerous screenplays for him. I also made several low-budget films, always within the field of pink cinema.
I also worked with Oshima Nagisa, a friend with whom I often drank in bars. He suggested that we collaborate. It was a simpler time: directors could easily help one another if they got along. That is how I see it in retrospect. I often speak of "cinema as movement," rather than auteur cinema.
After the Neo-Dada movement, exchanges with artists and writers enriched me greatly. They taught me a great deal. Many friends helped me reflect on which film I should make and how I should make it. Together, we thought that the decline in interesting films was due to the decline of film criticism. That is how we became interested in film criticism: it was time to create a place where works could be debated, not simply presented or reacted to. We therefore published a journal entitled Film Criticism. Then I made A.K.A. Serial Killer.
Later, I developed what came to be called "landscape theory."
My life after this period often attracts more attention. Still, it was the same impulse that, after my time at the Cannes Film Festival with Wakamatsu Koji, led me to Palestine to witness the reality of the struggle for liberation. Using images shot on that occasion, we made Red Army / PFLP: Declaration of World War.
Until then, I had filmed landscapes according to my "landscape theory," but I wanted to develop this theory by going to see the landscape I was showing concretely. That was my motivation. That, in brief, is my path.
After that, I spent many years with Palestinian guerrillas. It was therefore after thirty-five years of absence that I made Prisoner/Terrorist. In making this film, I wanted to go beyond Red Army / PFLP: Declaration of World War, which took a more journalistic documentary form, and to reactivate "landscape theory." I myself had been a guerrilla for many years. I wanted to confront this reality and make a film from it. That is the film's theme. That is the path that led me to where I am today. 

What is happening in France could happen in Japan. It could also happen in the United States or elsewhere. At the time, people were mobilising in all directions, using different means, to prepare for any eventuality. Encouraging the internationalisation of movements, colonialism still existed. Occupying powers continued to deprive Third World countries of their freedom. In this context, I wanted to make a revolution throughout the Third World. New theories emerged, such as that of the "revolution of remote regions." As for me, I wanted to fight colonialism through cinema.
At that time, people were beginning to feel a sense of urgency. I wanted to make both movements and filmmaking more active and more effective. In my case, this meant the struggle for the liberation of Palestine. I wanted to return to Japan with this intention. That is how I began the struggle.
In the training camps for liberation in Palestine, there were enormous numbers of volunteers from all over the world. All nationalities were present. Several film projects about the struggle emerged. Jean-Luc Godard had arrived a year before us. Many people came to shoot documentaries. I saw them as being on the side of those who were fighting in support of the Palestinians. Things had changed. I was no longer the one who staged, but the one who observed and helped. At that moment, making my own films was no longer my central concern, because the Palestinians had been completely crushed. This is still the case. Whatever attacks they endure, they rise and fight with their own hands. That is when I understood. 

As long as we exist, as long as the question of occupation and liberation exists, internationalism will live on—whether within the framework of a political movement or an armed struggle. The question is how we actualise it. Throughout this time, ideas for films I wanted to make upon my return to Japan were boiling within me.
My frustration was on the verge of exploding. Now, I am beginning to take up these ideas one by one. I chose the theme of the prisoner, a subject close to me.
Why choose cinema? People say I went from filmmaker to guerrilla, then back to filmmaker. That may be true if one looks at it from the outside. But from my point of view, I am always doing the same thing. By using cinema as both a medium and a means, I can relentlessly carry out experiments and research. Instead of replacing the camera with the rifle, why not hold both in one's hands? I believe I can continue like this in the future.
It had been a long time since I had focused on making films before Prisoner/Terrorist. Some may find this film purely spectacular, but that was not my intention. I will surely make other films that are more explosive or more distorted. I do not make films about what I have lived through by simply describing a trajectory. Instead, I seek to convey thoughts grounded in my experience, yet capable of going beyond it.
Adachi Masao, "Letter to My French Spectators," in Le bus de la révolution passera bientôt près de chez toi. Écrits sur le cinéma, la guérilla et l'avant-garde (1963-2010), ed. Nicole Brenez and Hirasawa Go (Paris: Rouge Profond, 2012).
