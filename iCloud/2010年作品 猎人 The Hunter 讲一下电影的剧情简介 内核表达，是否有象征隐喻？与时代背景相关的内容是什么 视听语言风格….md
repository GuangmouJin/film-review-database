#BFI 长评-逃入荒野不说话的人。电影的故事，风格，视听呈现和结尾的处理都很喜欢。影片带有极强的政治性，虽然剧本未直接点名政治事件，但感觉与伊朗当时的状态有很大关系。折页着重提及了影片的开场，感觉也映射了男主曾经可能的身份。在波斯文的片头字幕下，伴随着重金属音乐，摄像机在一张彩色照片中游走，擦过点彩派的表面与男性的脸孔，最终拉远，揭示出1983年的伊斯兰革命卫队，正准备骑着摩托车碾过他们面前铺着的巨大美国国旗复制品。画面切黑片名出现。

风格上，影片带有着布列松极简主义的风格，开场的去戏剧化使用的是日常的重复，以此建立了整部电影的节奏，而后续情节上大量留白和省略，没有过多的着墨在过渡情节也很像布列松不少作品的处理，导演饰演的男主其实也很少情绪外露，表情也极度克制，大多通过行动表达情绪，甚至演员的台词都非常少，两个抓捕了他的警察可能是戏剧冲突最强的部分，但是也采用了规避的冲突的形式，最后甚至男主倒地的枪击镜头也采用的是大远景的呈现。

视觉上，效果非常奇特，大量的固定大远景及手持中近景，整体特写很少，并且也很少出现全景，感觉造成的效果是，他们都被那“社会”包裹着，大远景没有全景那样受困的状态，但固定镜头下角色却仿佛直接被环境吞没，他在不同的环境下均显的渺小无力。而中近景和特写都强没刻意强调情绪，特写似乎可以更加把情绪外放，而中近景的状态则是一种压抑的情绪表达，手持下我们可以看到角色的动作和情绪波动但却与角色更有距离感，同时影片中后半段的镜头时长也更长，我们跟随角色也仿佛感受到了那种走不出去的烦躁，结合着雨天湿漉漉的空气让观众不光体会到了那时间的流动还有环境中的压抑。光影和色彩的呈现也蛮好的，前后对比明显，荒野甚至都不是绿色，而是一种枯黄的状态，低饱和度的色调呈现的依然是压抑。影片中男主身上自带绿色有明显隐喻，而后续被直升机看到后更改成了红色的车。

听觉上，片中配乐还是蛮多的，但是都局限在某些特定的情节，“高潮戏”的部分基本是无配乐的，多以自然环境音为主。

影片在伊朗本土受到严格限制，却在柏林电影节等国际舞台引起关注，被解读为隐性的政治电影。折页提及，当他的妻子和女儿神秘失踪时，一位警官告诉他，他的妻子在警方与反叛者的交火中意外身亡（这发生在伊朗上一次总统大选期间，大选和随后的镇压之前）。当他去医院打听时，他（以及我们）看到的只是其他失踪或被遗弃的孩子，包括警方无人问责，正是影片主题的重要部分。而如同当代生活中许多事情一样，我们等待和期待的解释从未出现，当他在女儿生日那天左右探访母亲，母亲由导演的亲生母亲出演。考虑到本片引用了格勒斯坦的《一场大火》（1961）以及他次年监制的《房子是黑的》（两部影片分别出现在主角的公寓电视和他父母家中），这显然并非巧合。

**《猎人》**

在波斯文的片头字幕下，伴随着重金属音乐，摄像机在一张彩色照片中游走，擦过点彩派的表面与男性的脸孔——最终拉远，揭示出1983年的伊斯兰革命卫队，正准备骑着摩托车碾过他们面前铺着的巨大美国国旗复制品。画面切黑，片名《猎人》出现。

切到一条高速公路隧道，再切到树林中装填步枪的特写，然后是同一位主人公（由编剧兼导演拉菲·皮茨饰演）在夜晚熊熊篝火前持枪的画面。接着出现德黑兰繁忙高速公路的俯拍镜头——再切到一个阴森的洗车场，仿佛位于地狱附近，喷雾间升起的烟雾缭绕。最后，主人公被潜在雇主告知，作为前科犯，他不符合日班的条件，只能上夜班。但我们很快得知，他的妻子萨拉已经有一份日班工作，这意味着当他接下夜间看守的职位后，能和妻子以及六岁的女儿相处的时间将会非常少。

令人惊讶的是，许多对皮茨第四部长片的评论都自信地——而我认为是不准确地——称它为“极简主义”。在声画元素如此丰富、对当代伊朗生活展现如此充分的情况下（包括德黑兰在现代化与污染交织下的混乱景象，比我十年前造访时所见更贴切的影片我还没见过），他们到底是怎么想的？尽管《猎人》结尾的一些细节（比如在迷宫般的森林中，伴随人物的是哀伤而带有打击感的电吉他声）让人联想到《死无葬身之地》（1995）——一部确实可以合理称为“极简主义”的影片，与吉姆·贾木许的其他作品一样——但我认为，本片的主要驱动力绝非极简主义；在这里，“极简”是一种基于误解的隐含贬义。

不过，皮茨在片中担纲的主演表演——除了与家人在一起时，几乎始终一副扑克脸——的确有点像是罗夏墨迹测验；这正是它奇特的力量所在，使得角色同时具有同情性与恐怖感。我们不必知道他为什么入狱，他那被压抑、几乎难以控制的暴力气息，他像一颗随时会爆炸的定时炸弹的存在感，本身就足够有意义。同样，这个流畅且简洁呈现的故事里，还有许多悬而未决的问题。开场那张照片——我根据Alissa Simon在《综艺》评论中的描述部分确认了它——与影片后续到底有什么关联？主角叫什么名字？他在树林里拿着步枪做什么？如果他真是猎人，为何我们从未看到他打猎？（实际上，除了他拥有一支步枪外，唯一暗示他是猎人的，是他拒绝了工作中一个年轻人想要跟他一起去打猎的请求。）

我们是否该从主角的车子和公寓墙壁都是绿色这一点，读出某种政治意义？如果是，影片并未对此作出明确解释。当他的妻子和女儿神秘失踪时，一位警官告诉他，他的妻子在警方与反叛者的交火中意外身亡（这发生在伊朗上一次总统大选期间，大选和随后的镇压之前）；他们需要通过尸检来确认子弹来自警方还是反叛者，而且他们对他女儿的下落一无所知。之后，在他被要求在太平间辨认妻子的遗体后不久，又被要求辨认女儿的遗体——两次都是从同样的倾斜机位拍摄，他凝视着画外的尸体，脸上没有任何情绪。然而在此之前，我们已经看到他拿着女儿的照片，在街头徘徊、开车穿过社区，向陌生人展示，希望找到线索；当他去医院打听时，他（以及我们）看到的只是其他失踪或被遗弃的孩子。无所知——包括警方无人问责——正是影片主题的重要部分。而如同当代生活中许多事情一样，我们等待和期待的解释从未出现。当他在女儿生日那天左右探访母亲（顺便说一下，由皮茨的亲生母亲出演）时，他编造借口，掩盖妻女失踪及（可能或已经发生的）死亡事实，我们也无从得知他这样做的理由；给人的潜在印象只是，压抑已经成为他生命中不可或缺且必要的一部分。后来，我们看到他“回忆”或想象妻子在一次探监时向某个画外人承认，她曾对丈夫谎称自己怀孕，并补充道：“我宁愿死，也不愿让他知道我撒了谎。请帮帮我——我必须有一个孩子。”（大概是向对方恳求允许她在探监时与丈夫发生关系。）但正如影片中许多内容一样，这只是一个叙事痕迹，而不是完整的叙事。

然而，当主角在远处的高速公路上随机枪杀了两名警察，并逃往乡间被追捕抓获时，这不仅仅是一次场景转换，就像《险路勿近》（1952）、《夜幕低垂》（1957）或《枪击钢琴师》（1960）中的那种调度变化。它也是一次类型转变，通过剧情的战略转折，消解了之前积累的大多数未解之谜——在追捕他的两名警察中，一名志愿者想立刻将他击毙，另一名义务兵坚持要将他送上法庭。（类似的伊朗警察情境，也出现在贾法尔·帕纳西最近的两部长片中。）

影片的两部分都保留了对当代伊朗的快照式刻画，带有与易卜拉欣·戈莱斯坦在1965年的《砖与镜》中相似的精确、诗意与绝望感，包括同样对荒谬官僚体系的恐惧（以及对医院中被遗弃婴儿的呈现）。考虑到本片引用了戈莱斯坦的《一场火》（1961）以及他次年监制的《房子是黑的》（两部影片分别出现在主角的公寓电视和他父母家中），这显然并非巧合。

我猜，一些评论者之所以错误地称《猎人》为“极简主义”，是因为他们没有考虑到“本质主义（essentialism）”的概念——即为了更强的力量与清晰度而删去非必要的叙事细节。坚持基本面并不等于减少或简化基本成分。布列松——另一位常被误认为是极简主义者的本质主义导演，尤其在60年代，他的严谨常被视为古怪的程式化——一贯让演员依靠自身状态和肢体语言来表达，而不是通过有意的表演来传达。（值得一提的是，皮茨是在最后时刻、出于实际需要，才顶替了原本打算找的非职业演员出演本片主角的，这并非最初的设想。）

此外，皮茨的本质主义具有重要的伦理维度。主角在高速公路上随机射杀两名警察，与最后那名义务兵在远处射杀身穿另一名警察制服的他，在道德上是等价的。在这两种情况下，可以说，盲目的仇恨压倒并消解了立场和阵营的意义，正如盲目且任意的不公启动了这场悲剧的齿轮。一个处于潜在或现实内战状态的国家，不仅是伊朗，也包括美国、埃及等许多地方。对皮茨来说，这种内战及其催生的仇恨会抹平所有其他问题；而那些未解之谜，最终既是因，也是果。

——乔纳森·罗森鲍姆，《Cinema Scope》，2011年3月16日

The Hunter
Underneath the Persian credits, over heavy metal music, the camera roams around inside a colour photograph, grazing over pointillist surfaces and male faces - finally pulling back to reveal the Islamic Revolution Guard Corps in 1983, getting ready to drive their motorcycles over a huge replica of the American flag on the pavement in front of them. Cut to black and the film's title, The Hunter.
Cut to a highway tunnel, then to a rifle being loaded in the woods, then to the same title hero (played by the writer-director, Rafi Pitts) holding the rifle in front of a raging campfire at night. Cut to an overhead shot of a busy Tehran freeway
- then to a sinister carwash that seems to be located in the general vicinity of Hell, smoky fumes rising from the spray. And finally to the hero being told by a potential employer that as a convict he doesn't qualify for a day job, he has to take the night shift. But as we discover a little later, his wife Sara already has a day job, meaning that when he takes the night watchman job, he'll have little time to spend with her and their six-year-old daughter.
It's amazing how many reviews of Pitts' fourth feature have been confidently - and, I think, inaccurately - calling it minimalist. With so much going on in terms of sound and image, and all that is conveyed about living in Iran today (including a view of Tehran in all its modernist, polluted disarray that captures the city I visited a decade ago better than any other film I've seen), what could they be thinking of? Even though a few details towards the end of The Hunter (e.g., a plaintive and percussive electric guitar accompanying figures in a labyrinthine forest) evoke Dead Man (1995) - a film that can plausibly be called minimalist, along with Jim Jarmusch's other films - I would argue that the main thrust of this film is anything but minimalist; in this case, an implied insult that seems predicated on a misunderstanding
It's true, however, that Pitts' singular performance in the title role - poker-faced except for when he's seen with his family - is a bit of a Rorschach test; indeed, this is part of its peculiar strength, making his character sympathetic and terrifying at the same time. But we don't need to know why he went to prison in order for his repressed and barely contained violence, his resemblance to a walking time bomb, to become meaningful. It's also true that the plot, however fluidly and economically presented, is full of other unanswered questions. What does the opening photograph - which l've partially identified according to its description in Alissa Simon's Variety review - have to do with the remainder of this film? What is the lead character's name? What is he doing with a rifle in the woods? If he's a hunter, why do we never see him hunting? (In fact, the only indication that he's a hunter, apart from his possession of a rifle, is the denied request of a youth at his job that he come along on one of his hunting trips.)
Should we find political significance in the fact that the hero's car and the walls of his apartment are both green? If so, the film never clarifies this. When his wife and daughter both mysteriously disappear, he's told by a police official that his wife was accidentally killed in a shootout between police and rebels (this is during Iran's last presidential election campaign, before the election and subsequent crackdown); they'll need an autopsy to determine whether the bullet came from the police or the rebels, and they don't know anything about his daughter. Some time later, after he's been asked to identify his wife's body in the morgue, he's asked to identify his daughter's body there also - and on both occasions he gazes at the off-screen bodies, from the same oblique camera angle, without any visible sign of emotion. But by then we have already seen him walking the streets and driving through neighbourhoods with a photograph of his daughter, which he forlornly shows to strangers, hoping to come up with a clue; and when he checks at a hospital, all that he (or we) can find is other lost or abandoned children. Not knowing - a central facet of the hero's helplessness, including the fact that the police remain accountable to no one - is, in fact, an important part of what this film is about. And, as in much of contemporary life, the explanations that we're waiting for and hoping for never arrive. When the hero pays a visit to his mother (played, incidentally, by Pitts' real-life mother) around the time of his daughter's birthday, he invents excuses for her and his wife not being with him, choosing to conceal the fact of their disappearances and (probable or actual) deaths, but we aren't privy to any of his rationale for this behaviour; the underlying impression is only that repression has become an integral and necessary part of his existence. Later, we find him either 'remembering' or imagining his wife in a flashback confessing to some unidentified, off-screen listener that she lied to her husband, whom she now visits in prison, about being pregnant, adding, I'd rather die than for him to know I lied. Please help me - I must have a child.' (Presumably, she's pleading to be allowed to have sex with her husband when she visits him in prison.) But like so much else in the film, this is a narrative trace rather than part of a narrative.
Yet when the hero randomly shoots two cops on a distant freeway and flees to the country, where he's pursued and captured, it's more than just a change in locale like those in On Dangerous Ground (1952), Nightfall (1957), or Tirez sur le pianiste (1960). What persists in both parts of the film is a snapshot of contemporary Iran with some of the precision, poetry, and despair that Ebrahim Golestan brought to Brick and Mirror in 1965, including the same horror of absurdist bureaucracy (and a comparable vision of abandoned infants in a hospital) - which hardly seems accidental given that this film quotes both Golestan's A Fire (1961) and a film he produced the following year, The House Is Black. (Both films are seen on television, in the hero's flat and at the home of his parents, respectively).
I would guess that some reviewers have been misdescribing The Hunter as
'minimalist' because they haven't considered the concept of essentialism - the process by which inessential narrative details get eliminated for the sake of greater force and clarity. Sticking to basics isn't the same thing as reducing or simplifying basic ingredients. Bresson - another essentialist commonly mistaken for a minimalist, especially during the '60s, when his rigour was widely regarded as eccentric mannerism - characteristically sought to make his actors expressive according to what they were and what their bodies said, not according to what they might consciously project as actors. (It's worth adding that Pitts took over the film's leading role from the non-professional he had in mind at the last moment and out of practical necessity, not according to his original design.)
Pitts' essentialism, moreover, has a vital ethical dimension. The hero shooting two cops at random on the freeway becomes morally equivalent to the draftee cop shooting him from a distance in the final sequence, simply because he's dressed in the other cop's uniform. In both cases, one could say that blind hatred trumps and makes irrelevant whatever position one has, whatever side one happens to be on, just as blind and arbitrary injustice is what sets the other wheels of this tragedy in motion. A country in a state of potential or actual civil war describes a good many places today, and not just Iran or the United States or Egypt. For Pitts, it would appear that this civil war and the hatred it engenders has a way of levelling all the other issues; leaving many questions unanswered ultimately becomes both cause and effect.
Jonathan Rosenbaum, Cinema Scope, 16 March 2011
