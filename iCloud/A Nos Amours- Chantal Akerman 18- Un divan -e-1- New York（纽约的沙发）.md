《纽约的一张沙发》（**A Couch in New York）**

**剧情简介**

一位**身心俱疲**、濒临崩溃的**纽约心理分析师**，与一位**自由奔放**的**巴黎女舞者**做出了一个大胆的决定——**交换公寓**。


	•	同时，影片也采用了**经典浪漫喜剧的另一种模式**——文化碰撞，如**劳伦斯·卡斯丹（Lawrence Kasdan）**的《法式亲吻（French Kiss, 1995）》。这两部电影甚至都采用了**保罗·孔蒂（Paolo Conte）的《Via Con Me》**作为配乐。

影片的叙事设定十分**简单而富有吸引力**：



拉康式的爱情观：

	**“爱情就是将你所没有的东西，给予一个不想要它的人。”**
——这是一个非常**拉康式**的定义。

阿克曼的幽默感：塔蒂与卢比奇的影子

**《纽约的一张沙发》比阿克曼以往的作品都更加轻松、喜剧化**，其风格让人想起：
	•	**雅克·塔蒂（Jacques Tati）**的无声幽默。
	•	**恩斯特·卢比奇（Ernst Lubitsch）的精妙喜剧，尤其是他惯用的对比与回响（comparisons and echoes）**：
	•	观众不断被引导去观察人物**走路、说话、穿衣、唱歌的方式有多么不同**，这些细节成为影片的幽默来源。
	•	阿克曼借鉴了卢比奇的理念——**如果一个角色只需一次化妆或几个小笑话就能完全改变身份，那么在某种程度上，每个人都是自由的，每个人都可以重新开始，成为新的自己。**


	•	**朱丽叶·比诺什在片中的表现清新迷人**，但**威廉·赫特却阴沉、疲惫，宛如一位黑眼圈浓重的“诺斯费拉图”（Nosferatu）**。
	•	**他们之间毫无化学反应**，这在一部浪漫喜剧中显得尤为尴尬。

然而，或许这正是阿克曼的**冒险尝试**：
	•	她试图创造一部**轻盈、明亮、解放人心的浪漫喜剧**，却**刻意避免使用通常的魅力元素**，不依赖化学反应和观众的情感投射。
	•	**她拒绝那些传统的安全网**，而是让这部电影在**错位、误解、身份漂移**中完成它自己的叙事。

**这是阿克曼的一场赌博**——她希望用幽默来解构浪漫，而不是去迎合浪漫。然而，这种尝试最终是否成功，就取决于观众愿意接受多少的“错位”与“距离感”了。

——**阿德里安·马丁（Adrian Martin），《Cinema Papers》，1997年10月**




A Couch in New York
A burnt-out New York psychoanalyst, nearing a nervous breakdown, and a free-spirited French dancer in Paris, make a decision to swap apartments.
They find themselves deeply involved in each other's social settings - the people in their lives are caught in an amusing state of mistaken identity and confusion. Akerman took a stab at making a commercial hit with this breezy romantic comedy. Evincing an odd sense of dislocation, it is well worth re-evaluating.
A contemporary review
At first glance, and particularly to anyone who is unfamiliar with Chantal Akerman's previous films, A Couch in New York may seem like a strangely pale, and even clunky version of a typical American romantic comedy from the past few years. It takes to an extreme the principle of a film like Sleepless in Seattle where lovers who are destined to be together spend most of the movie apart, or, even when they are together, they somehow miss each other, don't really relate to each other openly, honestly, directly.
A Couch in New York also uses another staple device of romantic comedy: it's a film about the comparison and the clash of different cultures and their manners, like Lawrence Kasdan's French Kiss (1995). (The two films share a delightful scat song, Paolo Conte's 'Via Con Me'.) The narrative set-up of Akerman's film is simple and intriguing: a Parisian dancer, Béatrice (Juliette Binoche), and a New York psychoanalyst, Henry (William Hurt), exchange apartments. She lives in the bustling, noisy, colourful, run-down, very multicultural district of Belleville; he lives in a vast, sterile, hi-tech New York apartment. She has a veritable army of lovers after her; and he leads a sheltered, loveless, even solipsistic existence.
For a long time, the film just switches these characters, and watches them exploring their new environments. But then the plot lurches forward and keeps zigzagging in sometimes surreal and airy ways. First, Béatrice starts treating, as an analyst, Henry's extremely neurotic patients. Then Henry himself lands back home, and eventually lands on his own analyst's couch, where he instantly adopts a fake identity as he converses with Béatrice.
Akerman has travelled the path from primitive beginnings to high sophistication in her film-making style over and over again, from film to film, and sometimes within a single film. Watching many of her movies is like experiencing the birth of the cinema - you really see and feel what it is to use a sound effect, or go for a reverse shot, or splash a colour on a wall. In her films, every stylistic element, every performance element, is isolated, noted; and then it is carefully combined in some fugue-like pattern with other elements. Her style is typically called minimalist, but that description is a little dry, because it can miss the special, minute kinds of narrative and pictorial tension in her images; and, above all, the crisp, tangy, priceless sensuality of her style. Bodily sensations, the rhythms and expansions and contractions of time, energies of all sorts, human or non-human-these are all so palpable in her cinema.
A Couch in New York is a weird sort of love story, a love story fitered through Jacques Lacan's principles of modern psychoanalysis. It is basically a very alienated story, full of emotional traps and abysses, full of awkward silences

and tearing miscommunications, It's a true comedy of mistaken identity, of misrecognition, of absences and gaps and non-reciprocal exchanges between people, particularly between men and women. One of the characters throws in that bit of pop wisdom, that love is 'giving something you don't have to someone who doesn't want it' - a very Lacanian formulation.
The relationships that happen between unformed individuals are strange, floating inscrutable events - inscrutable even to those who are inside those relationships. Love, in an Akerman film, is always, almost iterally, a "falling in love', a sudden trip or descent or collision, where the spark of desire, the erotic or romantic connection is absurdly immediate. One of Akerman's most soulful and haunting films, Toute une nuit (1982), shows a series of experiences and encounters that occur to a wide range of people between nightfall and daybreak. In one unforgettable vignette, two people in a bar happen to get up from their respective tables at the same moment. As their paths cross, these two strangers suddenly fall into each others' arms in a wild embrace, and they dance to the sad tune that's playing on the jukebox; they dance like they've been wrapped up in each other forever.
A Couch in New York is lighter and more comic than any previous Akerman film. It evokes memories of Jacques Tati, and especially Ernst Lubitsch.
Lubitsch, too, made films in which everything is based on minute comparisons and echoes: we are constantly led to observe the hilarious, starkly different ways that his characters walk, talk, dress and sing. And here is another fine Lubitsch principle which Akerman has today made her own: if any character can be transformed into their complete opposite number with the help of a makeover and a few gags, then everyone, in a sense, is free, blessed, always able to start over as someone new.
I guess the big difference between the great Lubitsch movies, and this brave new film by Akerman, is that A Couch in New York is finally rather charmless, and even a bit forced. Binoche is very fresh and appealing in her role, but Hurt is a sunken, dark-eyed, morbid Nosferatu - and, one must admit, there is absolutely no chemistry between them. But in many ways, that is Akerman's gamble: to make a breezy, bright, liberating romantic comedy without the usual charm, without the typical chemistry - without those conventional crutches, or easy ways of securing audience empathy.
Adrian Martin, Cinema Papers, October 1997
