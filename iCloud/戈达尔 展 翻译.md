# 戈达尔 展 翻译


让-吕克·戈达尔：从银幕到画廊墙
作者：多米尼克·帕伊尼
译者：丹妮拉·施雷尔

让-吕克·戈达尔，这位投身于两项看似对立追求的电影人，于2022年9月13日逝世，享年91岁。

一方面，戈达尔的电影展示了电影独有的具象力量，同时也表达了电影在改变世界方面的无力感——那些令他愤慨、反叛或悲叹的事物。在《电影史》（Histoire(s) du cinéma，1998）的一个章节《致命之美》中，戈达尔常常哀叹艺术无法对现实产生作用，电影成为浪漫主义和现代性的最后尝试。

超过五十部电影——包括短片与长片、散文电影与虚构作品——探索了这种失望，同时试图回应安德烈·巴赞提出的本体论问题：“电影是什么？” 戈达尔是否正是在与现代艺术家共有的折磨中回应了这个问题？雕塑家阿尔贝托·贾科梅蒂曾向让·热内提问：“你必须复制你所看到的吗？” 随即又摇摆着补充：“但同时，你还必须画一幅画？” 戈达尔同样接受了这样的事实：他必须想象电影，同时要兼顾真实性。


另一方面，他不断痴迷于探讨电影艺术与其他艺术形式的关系。这一立场由早期电影人（如阿贝尔·冈斯、让·爱泼斯坦、布努埃尔、奥逊·威尔斯等）确立，但并非将电影视为“艺术的综合体”。《激情》（Passion，1982）的导演戈达尔希望通过电影这一艺术形式来验证并实践所有艺术之间的交叉与互动，其核心方法论美德便是蒙太奇——他称之为“隐喻的兄弟关系”。

让-吕克·戈达尔从未放弃他早期的评论活动，将蒙太奇视为一种真正的工具：一种服务于所有艺术的思想工具（“一种形成思维的形式，这种形式也在思考”）。他开创了蒙太奇的启蒙潜力，同时也批判其混乱和笨拙的应用。20世纪下半叶，许多不同视觉艺术领域的艺术家都引用了戈达尔的作品，赞赏其富有创造力的实验性、图像大胆组合以及“碎片的艺术”。

作为无声电影人（穆瑙和爱森斯坦）和声音电影大师（雷诺阿和罗西里尼）的继承者，以及完美连接两者的电影人（弗里茨·朗），戈达尔被夹在近代的过去（20世纪的历史，映照于电影中）与未来的临近（政治参与的幻象）之间。

在《电影史》中，他大胆地进行了一次前所未有的自我反思：讲述一种技术手段可以记录历史的艺术形式的历史。

被视为性格阴郁的道德主义者、独裁的智者、不靠谱的科学爱好者，同时也是电视与广播中备受争议但极具价值的常客，戈达尔否定了所谓的“专业主义”。虽然他曾短暂参与政治活动，但对戈达尔而言，真正需要改变的不是世界，而是现有的美学和诗意原则，它们不足以应对现实的挑战。因此，在《狂人皮埃罗》（Pierrot le Fou，1965）中，正如路易·阿拉贡所钟爱的那样，波德莱尔的忧郁与兰波的乌托邦奇妙地交织在一起。

当20世纪中叶，电影似乎不再有任何创新可言时，戈达尔成为第一位察觉电影即将消亡的电影人，依托于60年代的新浪潮这一最终浪潮的预兆。

随着时间步入21世纪，这种清醒意识促使戈达尔创作和制作作品，展现出对遗产的思考：这一方向始于1994年的《JLG/JLG——十二月的自画像》。这并非普通的告别，而是戈达尔通过展览和分享作品，重新发明与创新，定义了他对电影的“退出”。

自《电影史》以来，蓬皮杜中心的展览《乌托邦之旅：1946-2006，寻找失落的定理》（2006）、《再见语言》（Goodbye to Language，2014）和《影像之书》（Le Livre d’image，2018）都预示着，在西方艺术史中，戈达尔将通过他的最终作品，呈现一种艺术和哲学上的视野与启示。

在《影像之书》中，戈达尔创造了一个时间性的物体，结合文学与电影，它不仅仅是按照传统电影展映原则进行放映，随后还作为展览在不同场所巡回展出（如洛桑维迪剧院、楠泰尔阿芒迪耶剧院、尼永城堡）。

《场景》（Scénarios）是他在自愿离世前夕完成的一部作品。虽然它被投影播放，但更适合画廊的墙面——将其移动的影像引向电影屏幕之外。

作品的形式是一个双联画：《DNA，基本元素》 和 《MRI，奥德赛》。DNA象征人类主体的独特生物印记，MRI揭示了一个脆弱身体的痛苦。

在这两个极点之间——起源与衰亡——展开了一种场景：由交织的笔记和影像编辑而成，时长18分钟。《场景》是一封告别书，呈现了这位艺术家黑暗而崇高的哀叹，影片以戈达尔的最后自画像结束：他半裸坐在床上，执着地抄录着让-保罗·萨特的《情境IV》中一段献给德国画家沃尔斯的文字。

法国艺术史家加埃唐·皮孔曾描述晚期伟大作品的力量：“这些作品仿佛散发出时间的颤动，诞生的感觉。”《场景》正是这样一件终极、最终的作品，充满了艺术家晚期作品的激情与自由，展现出艺术家在最后时刻的绝对表达。

by Dominique Païni

On the one hand, Jean-Luc Godard made films that demonstrate both a figurative power unique to cinema, while expressing cinema's powerlessness to change whatever, at any moment, caused him indignation or inspired him to rebel or lament. In arguing that beauty is fatal ('Fatal Beauty' is the title of a chapter in Histoire(s) du cinéma (1998)), Godard often bemoaned the inadequacy of art to act on the world, with cinema as the last attempt to succeed Romanticism and Modernity.
More than fifty films - shorts and features, essay films and fictions
- explore this disappointment and simultaneously attempt to provide answers that echo André Bazin's ontologically resonant question: what is cinema?
Didn't Jean-Luc Godard respond to this question in a state of torment shared by most modern artists: one to which the painter-sculptor Alberto Giacometti once testified? Preoccupied by the question of 'likeness', Giacometti once asked Jean Genet: 'You have to copy what you see, don't you?' and, immediately vacillating, added: 'And, at the same time, you have to paint a picture? Godard, too, resigned himself to the fact that he had to imagine films, while accommodating the need for verisimilitude.
On the other hand, he was endlessly obsessed with demonstrating the position that the art of film occupies in relation to the other arts.
This position, staked out by filmmakers before him (Gance, Epstein, Bunuel, Welles...), had nothing to do with cinema as a possible
'synthesis of the arts? The filmmaker of Passion (1982) wanted to verify (see) and experiment with (practice) the intersections and interaction between all the arts from the vantagepoint of cinema, whose essential methodological virtue is montage, which he referred to as 'the fraternity of metaphors

Jean-Luc Godard never abandoned his early activity as a critic, and considered montage to be an authentic organon: an instrument of thought at the service of all arts ('a thought which forms a form that thinks'). He pioneered montage's enlightening potential but denounced its confused and clumsy uses. Many artists of the second half of the 20th century, across a wide range of visual disciplines, referenced Godard's work for its fertile inventiveness, its daring combinations of images, and his 'art of the fragment.
Heir to both silent filmmakers (Murnau and Eisenstein) and filmmakers who eloquently used sound (Renoir and Rossellini), as well as the filmmaker who perfectly linked them (Lang), Jean-Luc Godard was torn between the recent past (the history of the 20th century, as mirrored in cinema) and the proximity of the future (the illusions of political engagement).
With Histoire(s) du cinéma, he ventured into an unprecedented mise en abyme: writing the story of an art form whose technical means make it possible to record history.
Deemed a moralist with a shady temperament, a despotic know-it-all, an amateur scientist, and a paradoxically valued but unpredictable regular on television and the radio, he repudiated any professionalism (within an alleged profession). Although he had briefly engaged in activism, for Godard, it was less the world that needed changing and more that existing aesthetic and poetic principles were inadequate and new ones needed to deal with it differently. Thus, in Pierrot le Fou (1965), so beloved by Louis Aragon, the melancholy of Baudelaire and the utopia of Rimbaud are singularly intertwined...
When, in the mid-twentieth century, no further cinematic innovation seemed attainable, Jean-Luc Godard was the first filmmaker to tap into the premonition of cinema's imminent demise, the desperate windfall of an ultimate Vague that the progressive modesty of the sixties claimed as Nouvelle.
In the end, Godard confessed that the mistake made by those who embodied this 'new wave' was to imagine that they were experiencing a 'beginning' by frequenting Henri Langlois' Cinémathèque Française. Thirty years later, the indefatigable cinephile confessed in his great Histoire(s) du cinéma that it was, in fact, an end.

Moving closer to the 21st century, this lucidity encouraged Godard to conceive of and produce works that already hinted at a legacy: a direction that began in 1994 with JLG/JLG - Self-Portrait in December. Far from an ordinary farewell, it was the way in which Jean-Luc Godard exhibited and shared his works that defined his exit from cinema, as he took advantage of this period of retrospective reflection to invent and innovate anew.
Since Histoire(s) du cinéma, the Centre Pompidou exhibition, entitled Voyage(s) en utopie, 1946-2006, à la recherche d'un théorème perdu (2006), Goodbye to Language (2014) and Le Livre d'image (2018), one could anticipate that, like the major artists in the history of Western art, Jean-Luc Godard would, in his final work, offer an artistic and philosophical vision and lesson.
With Le Live d'image, he created a time-based object, combining literature and film, which, rather than simply being shown according to the usual principles of film exhibition, was later circulated as an exhibition (at the Théâtre Vidy-Lausanne, the Théâtre des Amandiers de Nanterre, and the Château de Nyon). For its creator, this film marked a decisive step towards exposition cinématographique (the
'film exhibition').
Scénarios is the title he gave to a work completed on the eve of his voluntary death. Although projected, it calls for the wall of a gallery: the ushering of its moving shadows away from a cinema screen.
It takes the form of a diptych: 'ADN, éléments fondamentaux'
(DNA, fundamental elements') and 'IRM, Odyssée' ('MRI, Odyssey):
DNA being the biological signature that constitutes the uniqueness of a human subject; MRI being the medical imaging that uncovers the suffering of a weakened body.
Between these two poles, which evoke genesis and decline, unfolds a scénario of sorts: a story made up of interwoven notes and images, brought together and edited into 18 minutes.
Scénarios is a farewell, and demonstrates the endurance of this dark and sublime lament which ends with the last self-portrait of the artist Jean-Luc Godard, seated on his bed, half-dressed.

Concealing nothing of the wear and tear on his body, he tenaciously copies out a passage from a text by Jean-Paul Sartre ('Situation IV'), a logical and droll apologue dedicated to the German painter Wols.
Describing what great late-stage works can teach us, Gaëtan Picon, one of France's foremost art historians and a friend and commentator of André Malraux (which is not insignificant here), evokes 'the admirable trembling of time' that emanates from them. Some of them, he says, 'suggest the feeling of birth. And birth is nudity. As if from that closure blossomed something that has not yet seen the coming of time. As if all of history had to be annulled for the personal voice to emerge, through which the unconditionedness of the first instant is expressed, when day has just separated from night. Origins recovered, but through erasure, purification.
Indeed, some major works of art bequeathed to humanity by artists at the end of their lives move us with the feverishness of their gesture(s) and the freedom of their formal decisions - for which the artists no longer fear harsh criticism. Scénarios belongs to this category of ultimate, definitive work.

Dominique Paini is a french theorician and curator. He managed arthouse cinemas in Paris, was the head of the Cinémathèque Française from 1993 to 2000 and then became one of the directors of the Centre Pompidou: programming pluridisciplinary exhibitions such as one on Roland Barthes and Samuel Beckett. In parallel, he organised exhibitions of international scope such as Hitchcock et les arts and Voyage(s) en utopie de Jean-Luc Godard.
