# 南方


#BFI 长评-镜头背后的故事，沉默背后的暴力。纪录片，主题是以1998年发生在美国德州Jasper县的一名49岁非裔美国人的案件为主线探讨了美国社会中的种族问题。电影没有对受害者周围的人进行访谈，也没有对于整起事件有一个详尽的描述和分析，而是以一种偏向镜头叙事的角度呈现了一个南部小镇的生活环境，以南方的“风景”本身诉说历史，长镜头下的环境如同幽灵般萦绕着创伤带观众来呈现那与任何地方都没有什么不同的环境，以此对照呈现出那片土地上人们那不同的遭遇，电影中让我印象最为深刻的就是两段车尾部对准道路拍摄的长镜头，映后看了折页，也如我猜测的一样，那就是James被拖行三英里的道路。折页提及，她选择“不展示，而是召唤记忆”，所以她选择用另一种方式想让观众感受到整个事件。她还认为，美国南方的“沉默”之下，可能隐藏着致命的种族主义。

折页提及，她拒绝采访伯德的家人，认为那样做“太过残忍”，因为案件发生仅三个月，媒体已经过度消费了受害者家属的悲痛。她选择“不展示，而是召唤记忆”，类似于《浩劫》中对犹太人大屠杀的非直接表现方式，尽管她没有看过《浩劫》，但她的拍摄方式与之有异曲同工之妙，因为她认为要展示就要面临一个“该展示多少”的问题。阿克曼认为，美国南方的“沉默”令人恐惧——因为在那种沉默之下，可能隐藏着致命的种族主义。

我认为，“召唤”一个事件通常比直接展示新闻报道更有力量。但所有想拍摄一场暴行的人都会面对一个问题：该展示多少，该隐藏多少？

克劳德·朗兹曼（Claude Lanzmann）在《浩劫》（Shoah, 1985）里找到了自己的方法，其他导演也有自己的方式。总是会有一个问题：应该展示什么，不展示什么，以及如何让人真正“感受”这个事件。


《Hanging Out Yonkers》（1972）—— 纽约郊区的青年与毒品

创作背景
	•	1971年，阿克曼来到纽约，正值美国社会毒品泛滥，尤其影响青少年群体。
	•	她结识了导演 Myra Alfreds，后者在**Yonkers（纽约北部的郊区）**运营一个青少年毒品预防与治疗项目（RAP，Re-direction and Prevention）。
	•	20世纪70年代，Yonkers是一个社会分裂的城市：白人中产阶级与贫困的黑人和拉丁裔群体共存，而毒品问题影响了所有社区。
	•	阿克曼对这一社会问题产生兴趣，并决定拍摄青少年在RAP中心的日常生活。

电影的结构与手法
	•	纪实风格：阿克曼拍摄了RAP中心的青少年，记录他们的对话、活动以及他们在康复中的挣扎。
	•	社会观察：影片不仅涉及青少年毒品问题，还探讨了种族和阶级的不平等。
	•	未完成的作品：由于种种原因，这部电影没有完成，如今仅存40分钟的无声素材、部分剧本和现场照片。

阿克曼的观察
	•	她注意到Yonkers的种族隔离现象：这座城市在1980年代成为美国唯一一座因住房和学校歧视同时面临两起联邦诉讼的城市。
	•	她对RAP中心的工作方式感兴趣，特别是它如何试图将来自不同种族、阶级背景的青少年聚集在一起，提供戒毒帮助。
	•	她拍摄的镜头不仅关注青少年本身，也捕捉了Yonkers的城市景观，展示了一座被经济和种族矛盾撕裂的城市。

主题
	•	毒品与青少年：探讨毒品如何影响纽约郊区的年轻人，跨越种族和阶级界限。
	•	种族与阶级隔离：Yonkers的社会结构使得不同社区的青少年在相似的毒品问题下，却面临不同的社会支持与资源。
	•	未完成的记录：虽然影片没有完成，但它提供了阿克曼早期对美国社会现实的探索，并为她后来的社会性电影奠定了基础。

总结：两部影片的对比

	《Sud》 (1999)	《Hanging Out Yonkers》 (1972)
背景	1998年詹姆斯·伯德被杀案，美国南方种族暴力	1970年代纽约郊区的青少年毒品问题
拍摄地点	得克萨斯州贾斯珀（Jasper, Texas）	纽约州扬克斯（Yonkers, NY）
主题	美国南方的沉默与种族创伤	青少年毒品问题、城市种族隔离
风格	长镜头、环境观察、沉默的力量	纪实风格、社会观察
影响	被认为是阿克曼晚期最重要的纪录片之一	由于未完成，影响较小，但反映了阿克曼早期对社会现实的兴趣

这两部影片虽然时隔27年，但都反映了阿克曼对美国社会不平等现象的敏感性，无论是南方的种族暴力，还是纽约郊区的阶级与毒品问题，她都以独特的影像语言呈现了现实的裂痕。《Sud》成为阿克曼最具震撼力的社会性纪录片之一，而《Hanging Out Yonkers》虽然未完成，但也体现了她早年对美国社会问题的深刻关注。




尚特尔·阿克曼谈《Sud》







尚特尔·阿克曼（Chantal Akerman）的《Sud》（1999）是一部关于美国南部种族暴力的纪录片。其创作背景与阿克曼对美国历史、种族主义以及现实政治的敏感关注密切相关。

创作背景
	1.	埃米特·提尔（Emmett Till）事件的影响
《Sud》的灵感部分来源于1955年埃米特·提尔事件。提尔是一名14岁的非裔美国男孩，在密西西比州被白人暴力私刑处死，成为美国民权运动的重要转折点。阿克曼在拍摄前对美国种族历史进行了深入研究，这一事件及其延续的社会创伤成为影片的隐形背景。
	2.	詹姆斯·伯德（James Byrd Jr.）的惨案
1998年，詹姆斯·伯德（James Byrd Jr.）被仇恨犯罪分子用卡车拖行至死的事件震惊全美。这一现代私刑（lynching）案发生在得克萨斯州贾斯珀（Jasper, Texas），凸显了南方种族主义的持续性，而这正是阿克曼选择拍摄《Sud》的直接动机。她希望通过影像探索南方种族暴力的深层结构。
	3.	阿克曼对“美国南方”的个人兴趣
阿克曼曾多次拍摄美国题材（如《新闻集》（News from Home, 1977）），对美国社会，尤其是“南方”这一充满历史沉重感的地域，抱有特殊兴趣。在《Sud》中，她试图通过影像捕捉南方的地理、时间和历史记忆，借助长镜头和沉默的景观，让观众感受到种族暴力的幽灵仍在现实中游荡。

总结

《Sud》是一部关于美国南方种族主义幽灵的纪录片，以极简主义的长镜头、沉默的景观和历史记忆的挖掘，呈现了美国社会未能解决的种族暴力问题。阿克曼用影像让观众直面那片土地上过去与现在交织的创伤，提醒我们，历史的阴影仍未消散。

Sud + Hanging Out Yonkers
Chantal Akerman on 'Sud'
South was your first American film in quite a while. What drew you to the Byrd case, in particular? It was a horrifying story.
I read about it in a newspaper. It was a very strange, terrible story. But I had wanted to make a film about the American South before the Byrd case happened.
Did you start it before the Byrd case?
No, but I had had the idea at the end of April, and the Byrd case happened in June - I don't remember the exact day [Byrd was murdered on June 14, 1998].
Tread a blurb in the newspaper, and I said, I'm going to make the movie about the South and about what happened in Jasper, Texas.' I made plans to go to America and went to Jasper at the end of my trip.
Many of us who grew up in the sixties still have a fear of the South.
I know, but, of course, that's why I went there. I had read a book by James Baldwin - in French, I think it was called Harlem Quartet; in that book Baldwin conveys so well the fear and the silence of the South. That and the Faulkner books drove me to do that movie.
Any particular Faulkner books?
I've read most of them. I mix them up. But probably Light in August [1932] was important.
So when the Byrd murder happened, I said, 'I have to go there,' and I was lucky enough to get to Jasper just before that memorial service in the church, because that's an incredible scene.
Did you consider filming any of Byrd's family?
No. I did not need that. I think it would have been awful to go and say, 'Hey, your father died. What do you think about it?' - after only three months.
Someone asked me how I made the decision not to use any of the news footage of the murder. I think that evoking an event is usually stronger than showing news coverage of it. But people who want to make a film about an atrocity always face the question of how much of the event to show. Claude Lanzmann found his own way in Shoah [1985], and other people have found their own ways. There's always a question of what to show and what not to show, and how to make people feel the event.
Your last shot, that almost seven-minute tracking shot down the road, along the route where Byrd was dragged, reminds me of Shoah.
Well, as you know, one of my obsessions is the concentration camps and what happened there. But I've never seen Shoah; I've been afraid to see it, so I don't think Lanzmann has had any influence on me.
When Baldwin writes about the silence of the South and how he doesn't know what's hidden behind the silence, that behind the silence can be someone who wants to kill you - well, again, I was thinking of the camps. [...] By using that very long shot, I was trying to capture maybe a very, very, very, very tiny bit of what Byrd went through.
Scott MacDonald, A Critical Cinema 4: Interviews with Independelt Filmmakers (University of California Press, 2005)

About 'Hanging Out Yonkers' (1972)
During her first stay in New York from 1971 to 1973, Chantal Akerman met Babette Mangolte with who she set out to work with throughout the 70s, including on this project, Hanging Out Yonkers. This lesser-known project from Akerman's early career was partially lost and unfinished.
Today the Fondation Chantal Akerman and the Royal Film Archives of Belgium
(CINEMATEK) preserve silent rushes (lasting around 40 minutes), set photos and the script. As well as the oral testimony of Myra Alfreds, who was behind the Hanging Out Yonkers project, which we are transcribing here to provide the necessary context for this session.
'I met Chantal soon after she arrived in NYC in 1971 and saw her while she was staying at the loft of our NYC friend. I met Babette during this time as well.
Chantal knew about my work as a director in a drug prevention and treatment program for adolescents in Yonkers. In the early 1970's there was a drug epidemic in the United States that hit adolescents in every neighbourhood and all economic and racial groups. The city of Yonkers received federal funding to set up a city-wide programme. Chantal came to see the three RAP lounges in the community as well as the halfway house where adolescents lived - RAP stood for Re-direction and Prevention. I remember Chantal and Babette arriving one day at my RAP lounge in SE Yonkers and telling me that they had ordered someone's convertible to film the city. And there were a lot of hills!
I should stop a moment and tell you a little more about Yonkers. It is a city of almost 200,000 people located just north of the Bronx. In the 1980's, it also had the distinction of being the only city in the United States with two simultaneous federal lawsuits for discrimination in housing and schools. While the order to de-segregate the schools was carried out with little incident, the decision to build housing for families of colour in white neighbourhoods resulted in a ten-year battle. In case you're interested, there are two films about this time. One was a six-part series called Show Me a Hero and the other is a one-hour documentary called Brick by Brick, both are very good.
From reading her script, it was clear that Chantal became very aware of these deep-seated racial divides. She captured this in the voices she recorded of Black and white teenagers from RAP lounges in different areas of the city.
Hanging Out Yonkers was filmed in the largest of the three RAP centres, in Northeast Yonkers, located in a middle-class white neighbourhood. The day of the shoot, Chantal and Babette stopped another car in Manhattan to bring them and their equipment to Yonkers to meet the NE RAP lounge director and the adolescents in the programme. In the film you can see the storefront with a big window that served as a drop-in centre meant to attract kids from the street. It has a pool table, games, etc. What you don't see is the full-time programme below where kids came every day and committed to being drug-free. Chantal spent time with adolescents in similar treatment groups at the RAP halfway house supporting and encouraging them to stay with the programme. She was attracted to these kids and their struggles, related well to them, and them to her.' (NYC, March 2024)
