**《六十年代末布鲁塞尔的少女画像》**

#BFI 非常奇妙，看了一场两部作品的放映，《1960年代末一个布鲁塞尔少女的肖像》+《洋槐旅馆》，这两部的风格放一起似乎可以组成一部完整的《安娜的旅程》，孤独地找寻与那短暂的停留。电影的风格明显是致敬法国新浪潮，也如折页提及大标题后出现了一行小字标明了具体的时间，1968年4月的布鲁塞尔暗示了“1968年5月的巴黎”，那是一个马上到达但尚未到达五月巴黎的时间。电影在表达上的设计做的非常好，以一整天的时间展示了一个处于青春期少女的内心活动，单相思的小心翼翼让整个故事显得极为真实。天空的颜色变化体现了时间的变化，结尾处的那两场戏更是点睛之笔，聚会中的环绕找寻，之后的逃离，共行与转身离开依然掌握者主体性。

视觉上，主要以手持为主，大量的跟镜头体现的是一种角色行动的未知和角色主体的迷茫，尤其是女性朋友被拉走跳舞时的脸部特写镜头很好的展现了女主站在柜门前的那种不知所措的感觉。侧跟推轨也是很像《安娜的旅程》的那种悲观的无根感，结尾处的两个方向的推轨横移跟镜头很好地展现了女主的内心状态。

听觉上，特意等配乐字幕出来看了一下，使用的都是1960年代的老歌，《La Bamba》 是聚会围绕着双女主的伴奏，带有着一种欢快自由的气息，感觉也是女主迈出重要的一部的原因，而衔接的《It’s a Man’s, Man’s, Man’s World》 则是更加的直白的阐释了男性主导的社会现实，也在此时女主的女伴被一个男生拉去跳了这只慢歌。电影中印象最深的配乐其实是Leonard Cohen的《Suzanne》（唱片店内就是Cohen的LP《Songs Of Leonard Cohen》，《Suzanne》就是第一首歌），Cohen那低沉的嗓音和忧郁的歌曲非常好的衬托出了女主当时即使跟男生在一起相拥而舞内心仍然萦绕着的迷茫情绪。

折页提及，影片的动人之处正是在于对那个时代新兴的女权主义和同性恋社会运动的缺席。作为一名未成年少女，米歇尔并未认同政治少数群体的概念。阿克曼通过对青少年异性恋仪式的呈现使酷儿青少年的视角同样成为叙事的核心。这一刻的呈现与德帕尔玛的电影《魔女嘉莉》结尾的舞会场景有着异曲同工之妙，但阿克曼的处理方式更加克制。


自马克斯·奥菲尔斯（Max Ophüls）的作品以来，“圆舞曲”（la ronde）或是欲望与缺失的传递，似乎从未被如此目眩神迷又如此精准地呈现过。阿克曼在这一幕中巧妙地避免了传统的主观视角（即展现米歇尔眼中的舞蹈场面），而是通过镜头语言再现了一种“被排除在外的视角”。尽管镜头始终困于青少年圈子之内，影片仅使用了三个镜头，所有镜头的焦点始终集中在米歇尔身上：第一个镜头跟随她在圈中旋转，悄悄观察达妮埃尔，并享受被选中的喜悦；第二个固定镜头则展现她顶住周围人的目光，坚定地迈步做出选择；而在第三个镜头中，时间的延续让米歇尔被冷落的情感逐渐显现，回归到阿克曼惯用的、尊重而克制的叙事视角。与传统电影的主客体凝视不同，阿克曼的镜头语言赋予了米歇尔主动的凝视权力，同时也表现出欲望的流转与未竟之意。

影片中的画外音效——如少女们在街头行走时的脚步声——被精心构建，因此《It’s a Man’s, Man’s, Man’s World》的歌词（以及它的固有刻板印象）在此刻变得尤为震耳欲聋。

电影通过对视听语言的解构，揭示了这些元素如何塑造出那个被习以为常的“男人的世界”，正如父权制家庭、学校和流行音乐所建构的文化体系一样。

饰演米歇尔的年轻演员西尔塞·莱特姆（Circé Lethem），无论在身姿还是气质上，都与年轻时的阿克曼颇为相似。而阿克曼本人则曾出现在她的短片《炸毁我的城》（Saute ma ville）中，以疯癫的形象回到公寓，煮意大利面，最终点燃煤气，影片定格于暗示爆炸的画面。

《六十年代末布鲁塞尔的少女画像》某种程度上是对这部作品的另一种、不那么悲剧性的改写。同时，这部电影也可被视为对《我，你，他，她》（Je, tu, il, elle）的前奏，而后者由阿克曼在二十岁出头时创作，包含了她本人出演的一场仍然令人惊叹的女性同性性爱场景。

阿克曼后来曾谈及自己在《我，你，他，她》中出演裸体戏的经历：“当我拍摄这部影片时……我还没有和观众建立起关系……我不敢再这样做了——当时我完全没有意识到它的影响力。”然而，《六十年代末布鲁塞尔的少女画像》找到了“再次去做”的方式，但以另一种形式。

影片对少女单恋的刻画，使人可以看见《我，你，他，她》中“强烈的”女同性恋再现是如何发展而来，而这种表现最初源于《炸毁我的城》中导演自身的定义与少女时期的自我认知。换句话说，这部电影展现了1960年代末布鲁塞尔的一个少女如何成长为“尚塔尔·阿克曼”——一个通过电影建立独特且不断更新的观众关系的导演，而她的观众，也依旧在形成之中。

——Patricia White，《女同性恋小众电影》（Lesbian Minor Cinema），刊于《Screen》，2008年冬季刊



Portrait d'une jeune fille de la fin des années 60à Bruxelles
Portrait of a Young Girl... is Chantal Akerman's contribution to the French television series, Tous les garçons et les filles de leur âge (All the Boys and Girls of Their Age), commissioned by ARTE, of nine, hour-long programmes in which filmmakers were asked to render the period of their youth in part by drawing on the era's music. Akerman's film is at once autobiographical and circumspect; its title is generic in subject, 'a young girl', but specific in place, Brussels. Just below the title appears a small caption that gives us the specific time as well - April 1968. Thus the film's place and time evoke Paris, May 68 - a place and time whose massive student-led strikes function as a mythical origin story in narratives of the new left, poststructuralist theory and film culture - by virtue of being not quite Paris, not yet May. If the film makes no attempt to disguise that it is filmed in present-day Brussels, it may be because its girl's own story is not given meaning by the heroic (male) politics that would require historical authenticity. The protagonist, Michèle, seems stranded, bored, at one point mustering a half-hearted anti-Vietnam war chant. Part of the film's poignancy as a not-quite coming-out story is the absence of reference to the era's emerging feminist and gay social movements. A minor, Michèle does not connect with the idea of political minorities.
It is from this position of not quite, not yet, that Akerman rewrites the major language of the French New Wave of the 1960s. Each segment of the television series includes an obligatory party scene. Akerman responds to the letter of the commission while deflecting its homogenising potential (with 'boys and girls having both anti-sexist and heteronormative valences) in a rendition of the rituals of teenage heterosexuality that makes the perspective of a queer youth as devastatingly central as, yet much more restrained than, the culminating prom scene in Brian De Palma's Carrie (1976). In Akerman's penultimate scene, Michele and Danielle arrive at the party. Their separation at the film's end has, however, been prefigured. Michele's tryst with the boy from the cinema prepares the gesture that will conclude the film, as decisive as the one with which Michèle began the fateful day: she will send Danielle to Paul, cutting herself off from, yet controlling, their story.
To the sound of Trini Lopez's version of 'La Bamba', the party scene cuts in abruptly as the teenagers circle, arm in arm, to the left around Danielle, who chooses Michèle to join her as a dance partner in the middle of the circle.
When Danielle rejoins the circle, the scene cuts to a medium closeup of Michèle. She looks worried as she scans the crowd, then her face lights up. As she turns her head to choose, the film cuts to Michèle stepping up to...
Danielle. There is a beat or two of tension as her friend rejoins her within the circle, then Danielle flashes a reassuring smile. But when the anthem ends and the swirling overture to James Brown's 'It's a Man's, Man's, Man's World' starts up, the circle immediately breaks down, a tall boy cuts between the girls and sweeps Danielle into his arms. Michèle just stands there, perfectly still amidst the slow dancers, her face held in closeup as she feels and thinks. Finally she turns and walks out into the night.

Not since Max Ophuls' films has la ronde or the relay of desire and lack been so dizzyingly or economically rendered. Akerman's characteristic exclusion of a point-of-view shot (which would show the couple dancing), here brilliantly conveys precisely the point-of-view of exclusion, even as the camera remains trapped with Michèle within the circle of teens. We are given only three shots of the scene, and Michèle remains their focus: the first follows her as she circles in the chain, watching Danielle unobserved, and then reveals the even greater pleasure of being chosen; in the second, stationary shot she withstands the scrutiny of others as she moves to declare her own choice; in the third, duration is used to relay Michèle's palpable emotion at being passed over back to Akerman's signature, respectful authorial observation. Negotiating the delegation of the gaze in a way radically different from classical cinema's almost transitive uses (subject looks at and thereby acts upon object), Akerman's camera finds its anchor in a spinning, desiring and above all gazing surrogate.

The signifying effects of diegetic sound - for example, of footsteps as the girls move through the streets - have been so built up across the film that the song's clichéd lyrics (the cliché of the song) are deafening. By virtue of its deterritorialisation of cinema's audiovisual codes, the film shows how they construct that taken-for-granted man's world as surely as do the patriarchal family, the school and pop music, the targets of the film's internal critique.
Circé Lethem, the young actress who plays Michèle, bears a physical resemblance to the young Akerman in stance and presence. Akerman also appears in Saute ma ville - as a manic young woman who comes home to her high-rise apartment, cooks herself some spaghetti and finally takes a match to the gas before a final freeze-frame is accompanied by the sound of the explosion implied by the title. The later film is thus a less fatalistic revision of that first portrait of a young girl at the end of the 1960s in Brussels. But Portrait of a Young Girl, with its lovesick lesbian protagonist, is also a (less sexually explicit) revision of Je tu il elle, made when Akerman was in her early twenties.
The filmmaker later remarked about casting herself in that film, with its nudity and still remarkable lesbian sex scene: 'When I did it ... I didn't have a relation with the public ... I wouldn't dare do that again - I was completely unaware of how strong it would appear.'
Portrait of a Young Girl finds a way to 'do it again', but differently. The film's story of an almost unbearable schoolgirl crush hints at how a 'strong' lesbian representation such as that found in Je tu il elle might follow from the gesture of directorial and adolescent self-definition made in Saute ma ville. In other words, it shows how a young girl at the end of the 1960s in Brussels came to make
'Chantal Akerman's films' and to establish through them a unique and renewable relation with a 'public' itself still in the making.
Patricia White, 'Lesbian Minor Cinema', Screen, Winter 2008


Hôtel des Acacias
In 1982, the director of INSAS (the Brussels film school) asked Chantal Akerman to direct a film workshop. The filmmaker made the students work on a script written with Michèle Blondeel. Two young women go down to the Hôtel des Acacias. The owner is unattached and ready to fall in love with the first woman who comes along. Between them will come the hotel's employees and clients, all of whom are wrapped up in love stories.
