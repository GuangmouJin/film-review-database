**定格动画：庆祝大银幕上的手工动画**

**金刚**

日期是1933年3月2日星期四：距罗斯福总统就职还有两天时间。超过1500万美国人失业。尽管外面排着长长的救济粮队伍，但你却舒适地坐在世界上最壮丽的剧院之一——纽约无线电城音乐厅中，这座剧院刚刚在一片辉煌的装饰艺术风格中开幕。

你手中拿着一份纪念节目单。“来自世界一片未开发、被遗忘的角落，”你睁大眼睛读道，“一个幸存了七百万年进化历程的怪物……闯入了文明的领域……登上了有声银幕……震撼人类的想象力！”

幕布宽阔得足以需要13台电机才能拉动，部分开启，揭示了主角：*金刚*，由默里安·C·库珀和欧内斯特·B·肖德萨克为RKO电影公司制作。三段低沉的、黑暗的管弦乐和弦从电影原声响起。演员表中列出的有寡妇式的女主角费·雷、硬汉风格的角色演员罗伯特·阿姆斯特朗、新演员布鲁斯·卡博特，还有用异常大的字体标出的金刚本人，谦虚地被描述为“世界第八大奇迹”。

你的想象力准备好被震撼，尽管最初的故事场景看起来平淡无奇：纽约的码头，时代广场，一个女孩试图从水果摊上偷东西，这些场景再普通不过了。但随后，阿姆斯特朗饰演的烟斗导演，拍摄“丛林和各地的电影”，阻止了费·雷因为饥饿晕倒，并为她签下了他最新的冒险计划，带着摄制组航向一个神秘的目的地。

途中，你听到有关金刚的传闻，一个神话般的生物。接着，骷髅岛在雾中浮现，摄制组看到了像古斯塔夫·多雷插画中那样细致、充满幻想的景观。“天啊，真是一场精彩的表演！”阿姆斯特朗喊道，看到村民准备将一个年轻女子献祭给某个住在巨大墙后面的东西，而那扇巨大的门则被一根像纳尔逊纪念柱般粗大的门闩锁住。

幕布拉开40分钟后，那个恐怖的主角出现了——这个怪物闯入了文明的领域。他是一只50英尺高的猩猩，怒吼着，张开鼻孔，用胸膛敲打自己，像所有其他电影英雄一样。金发女郎费·雷是他立刻中意的对象，尽管她一直在尖叫：其他所有挡在他面前的人不是被他抓入口中，就是像尘埃一样被随意甩到一旁。他的力量是如此巨大。“多么强悍的男人！”一个年轻观众喊道，这是《纽约时报》评论中记录的反应。

更多恐怖场景接踵而至。一群史前生物与金刚或人类，或者两者交战。一只剑龙被枪火击倒。为了保护他的新女伴，金刚彻底击败了一只霸王龙，打碎了它的下颚，还像扔破伞一样甩掉了一只翼龙。费·雷的衣服凌乱，挣扎着被金刚巨大的手掌抓住。你有时会笑着面对这些恐怖场景，或许是为了掩盖恐惧。大多数时候你都坐在那里，既惊恐又紧张。接下来的几周里，人们蜂拥而至，时而惊叹，时而发笑，观看这部将彻底改变电影幻想的影片，并拯救该工作室免于破产。3月24日，金刚在洛杉矶的中国剧院正式首映。不久之后，他席卷全球的影院，广告口号如：

**第二部分**：

“人类有史以来构想出的最奇怪的故事！” 或 “世界上最伟大的电影！” 它真的如此吗？至今，《金刚》仍然是一部真正的经典，尽管我们常常对它的价值视而不见。自从它上映后，衍生了无数怪兽后代：尽管计算机已将特效带入了超越任何想象的领域，但威利斯·奥布莱恩所设想的那些特效依然让人印象深刻，这位天才导演用定格摄影和模型技术将这个大猩猩搬上了银幕。

但想象一下1933年的影响，当时那些庞大的史前生物稀如凤毛麟角。1925年，在柯南·道尔的小说《失落的世界》改编的电影中，奥布莱恩创造了一只腕龙，它用尾巴一扫就摧毁了伦敦的建筑，最后掉下了塔桥。而*金刚*的动物园则更大、更鳞片化、毛发更浓密。还有一个不同之处：它们会发出声音。在1933年之前，公众何曾听到过史前怪兽的吼叫、打嗝或尖叫？五年前，在有声片成为常态之前，电影角色依然只能在哑剧中交流。如今，这些恐怖的生物突然在原始森林中发声。音效团队用了所有可能的手法。传统动物声被录制成低一个八度、倒放的效果。它们还被混合了各种新奇的元素：比如敲击录音师背部的鼓棒的声音；有人往葫芦里发出的怪声。更不用说尖叫声了。在银幕上，从未有人像费·雷那样尖叫过：金刚一出现，她便张开嘴，场景接场景地尖叫。随后是马克斯·斯坦纳，凭借电影史上最庞大、最响亮的原声音乐为影片增添了他的一份贡献。

更有甚者，观众目睹了这只怪兽在他们坐着的电影院外肆虐的场面。也许他们从中获得了某种代入的快感，看着这个巨兽摧毁了一个让他们的生活因华尔街崩盘而接近毁灭的文明。无论如何，在金刚之后，没有任何电影怪物能离开城市全身而退。

两天后，《金刚》上映，罗斯福在华盛顿宣誓就职。“除了恐惧本身，我们别无可惧，”他在启动新政的就职演说中说道。这对许多人来说是好消息，但对于那些被一只50英尺高的大猩猩吓得瑟瑟发抖的观众来说，或许没什么帮助。《金刚》震撼了世界。它比1931年的恐怖经典《科学怪人》或《德古拉》更能激发观众的热情，并拓展了电影幻想的边界。在此过程中，它让现代的（温顺、食草的）大猩猩得到了一个不应有的坏名声，使恐怖成为一种可销售的商品，并为从《侏罗纪公园》到《哥斯拉大战雾霾怪兽》的所有作品铺平了道路。我们都生活在它的阴影下。

杰夫·布朗，《泰晤士报》，1993年12月27日

King Kong

The date is Thursday, 2 March 1933: two days away from President Roosevelt’s inauguration. Over 15 million Americans are out of work. There are breadlines, but you are seated in comfort in one of the most splendiferous theatres in the world, Radio City Music Hall in New York, just opened in a blaze of Art Deco glory.

In your hands you hold a souvenir programme. ‘Out of an uncharted, forgotten corner of the world,’ you read, eyes widening, ‘a monster surviving seven million years of evolution… crashes into the haunts of civilisation… on to the talking screen… to stagger the imagination of man!’

The curtains, vast enough to need 13 motors to get them moving, part to reveal the main attraction: King Kong, made for RKO Pictures by Merian C. Cooper and Ernest B. Schoedsack. Three descending, dark orchestral chords thunder out from the film soundtrack. The cast list promises widow heroine Fay Wray, hard-boiled character-player Robert Armstrong, a new actor, Bruce Cabot, and, in extraordinarily large letters, King Kong himself, modestly described as ‘the eighth wonder of the world.’

Your imagination prepares to be staggered, though at first the story’s settings prove nothing new: New York’s docks, Times Square, a girl trying to steal from a fruit stall: these are run-of-the-mill scenes. But then Armstrong, cast as a pipe-smoking director who makes ‘moving pictures in jungles and places,’ prevents Wray fainting from hunger, signs her up for his latest venture, and sails off with cameras and crew for a secret destination.

Along the way, you hear talk of Kong, a mythical creature. Then Skull Island is sighted through the fog, and the film crew approach a landscape with the dense detail and fantasy of a Gustave Doré illustration. ‘Holy mackerel, what a show!’ Armstrong shouts as he watches village natives preparing to sacrifice a nubile girl to something that lives behind a huge wall and a towering gate held in place by a bolt the size of Nelson’s Column.

40 minutes after the curtains parted, the terrifying leading man—this monster who crashes the haunts of civilisation—appears. He is a 50ft gorilla, who roars, flares his nostrils and beats his chest like no other movie hero. The blonde Wray he takes to immediately, despite her constant screaming: most other humans who fall in his path get scooped up into his mouth, or cast aside like dust. His strength is colossal. ‘What a man!’ a young spectator shouts: a response reported in The New York Times review.

There are more horrors yet. A supporting cast of prehistoric creatures do battle with Kong or man, or both. A stegosaurus is felled by gunfire. Eager to protect his new female playmate, Kong gives a tyrannosaurus rex a thorough hiding plus a broken jaw, and tosses away a pterodactyl as though it were a broken umbrella. Wray, her clothes in disarray, wriggles helplessly inside Kong’s huge hand. Sometimes you laugh at the horrors depicted, maybe to cover fright. Most times you sit there, awed and gripped. Over the next few weeks, people flock to marvel and occasionally giggle at a film that will revolutionise cinema fantasy and save its studio from bankruptcy. ‘The Strangest Story Ever Conceived by Man!’ or ‘The Greatest Film the World Will Ever See!’ Was it? Is it? King Kong remains a genuine classic, though one we tend to take for granted. So many monster offspring have been spawned in its wake: while computers have taken special effects beyond any realm envisaged by Willis O’Brien, the genius responsible for the stop-motion photography and model work that brought the big ape to life.

But imagine the impact in 1933, when outsize, prehistoric creatures were as rare as hen’s teeth. For a version of Conan Doyle’s The Lost World in 1925, O’Brien had created a brontosaurus who laid waste to London buildings with a whisk of its tail and ended the film by falling off Tower Bridge. The menagerie of King Kong, though, was far bigger, scalier, hairier. Another thing: they made sounds. Who in the public, before 1933, had ever heard prehistoric monsters roar, belch and screech? Five years earlier, before talkies became the norm, movie characters were still communicating in dumb show. Now, suddenly, fearsome creatures were vocalising in a primeval forest. The sound team used every trick in the book. Conventional animal sounds were recorded one octave lower, backwards, upside down. They were then combined with motley novelties: the thud of a drumstick on the soundman’s back; someone grunting unpleasantly into a gourd. Not to mention the screams. No one in cinema had ever screamed as much as Fay Wray: once Kong was sighted, she opened her mouth and let rip, scene after scene. Then came Max Steiner, adding his pennyworth on the soundtrack with one of the most extensive and noisiest film scores yet composed.

What’s more, audiences watched this monster rampage through the streets outside the very cinema where they sat. Possibly they felt vicarious pleasure, watching the brute destroy a culture that had brought so many lives close to ruin since the Wall Street crash. Two days after King Kong was unleashed, Roosevelt took office in Washington. ‘We have nothing to fear but fear itself,’ he declared in the inaugural address that launched the New Deal. Good news for many, maybe, but small comfort to audiences quaking in their shoes as this 50ft ape. King Kong shook the world. More than Frankenstein or Dracula, two horror milestones of 1931, it galvanised audiences and stretched the boundaries of film fantasy. Along the way it gave modern gorillas (gentle, vegetarian) an undeserved bad name, made terror a marketable commodity and paved the way for everything from Jurassic Park to Godzilla vs the Smog Monster.
