# 《茶泡饭的滋味》（Flavor of Green Tea Over Rice / お茶漬けの味，1952）是日本导演小津安二郎的代表作…

# 

《茶泡饭的滋味》（Flavor of Green Tea Over Rice / お茶漬けの味，1952）是日本导演**小津安二郎**的代表作之一，也被视作其战后风格逐渐成熟的重要作品。这部电影延续了小津一贯的家庭剧题材，但在内容层面与时代语境中，暗含着对**日本战后社会结构转型**的深刻观察与温和批评。

#BFI 长评-父权对阶级的驯化。结尾处时不禁让我想到贝克德尔测试，整部电影镜头虽然主要围绕着女性，她们大量的对话但内容似乎仍然围绕着男性，看到接近结尾的时候我感觉这部电影是父权对于阶级的驯化，女主最后则像是父权的延伸，担任“教化”后代的作用。妻子出身于上流阶层讲究礼仪，其战后女性的身份应该与外甥女类似，二人的选择看似不同，不过那蹦蹦跳跳的结尾感觉也没有特别大的区别。丈夫沉稳毫无浪漫象征着旧日本的男人形象，而且军人背景感觉仍然带着军国主义的象征，军歌响起，依然不适，其中也没感觉到批判，感觉仍然是对旧的某种肯定。极简主义成熟时期，低机位的仰拍呈现秩序，长镜头表现时间流动，构图上还是有不少前景遮挡呈现角色间的压抑关系，空镜仍然是最喜欢的部分，尤其是空房间，感觉舒心了不少。





#BFI 依然难受，中段还一度以为会有所改变看到结尾感觉也没有比他其他作品好多少，结尾处时不禁让我想到贝克德尔测试，整部电影镜头虽然主要围绕着女性，她们大量的对话但内容似乎仍然围绕着男性。看到接近结尾的时候我感觉这部电影是父权对于阶级的驯化，而更生气的点则是女主似乎成为了父权的延伸担任“教化”后代的作用。

角色上，妻子出身于上流阶层讲究礼仪，其战后女性的身份应该与外甥女类似，二人的选择看似不同，不过那蹦蹦跳跳的结尾感觉也没有特别大的区别。丈夫沉稳毫无浪漫象征着旧日本的男人形象，而且军人背景感觉仍然带着军国主义的象征，二人情感和生活的变化也让我想到了头几天看过的台湾电影《油麻菜籽》，两部作品中女性的命运也是相似的，《油》中是强调女性父权视角下的无根性，带有着批判视角，而本片中女主在结尾则是被驯化吃“茶泡饭”，感觉本片则是对旧时代的某种肯定，甚至扯到结尾影响到妻子的看法，甚至也要跟着肯定，片中依然展现了对军队生活的某种怀念，军歌响起，依然不适，其中也没感觉到批判。

视觉上，风格成熟，低机位的仰拍呈现秩序，长镜头表现时间流动，有些运动镜头印象也很深，像是门框嵌套下的推轨镜头，推和拉同样变现了空间感和主体的关注与脱离。构图上还是有不少前景遮挡呈现角色间的压抑关系，空镜仍然是最喜欢的部分，尤其是空房间，其实感觉反而舒心了不少。




这部片的空间比小津其他作品更开放，但它在叙事与技法上的节制却几乎是典范。小津从不浪费我们的注意力于可想而知的连接镜头。如果他真的让我们看到一个人从一个办公室走到另一个，那么这就是重要的：也许是对时间流逝的提示，或者是电影中等同于小说章节之间空白的“留白”。

没有一个叹息、耸肩或微笑比另一个更不重要，这也解释了为何小津作品中的小角色如此关键。

影片中关于拒绝包办婚姻的女孩这一副线，有时让人觉得几近“喜剧套路”。但真正的日本喜剧片，不会在高潮和解场面——设在妻子初次真正踏入的厨房中——随镜头余光扫过的威信食用油罐头中戛然而止。

——Vincent Canby，《纽约时报》1973年1月25日

⸻

《绿茶饭的滋味》中最出格的风格手法是它的摄影机运动。这是个复杂的问题：有些摄影机运动源于载具移动，有些追踪人物动作，有些是倾斜角度的推进，有些是短促、频繁的推进或拉出，依照布景空间进行。还有一个问题是：摄影机在镜头的哪个时点开始移动？

这部片的叙事将这些极其不同的摄影运动作为变化的基础。比如，场景开始时，摄影机已经在布景间向后移动；又或者人物离开后，镜头突然向前滑动，似乎没有目标；或者让人物走远后再慢慢向前推进，成为最“徒劳无功”的跟拍镜头（以这速度永远也追不上）。虽然部分摄影运动之间存在对称与反转，但整体来看，它们只能被理解为抽象的空间标记，源于一种严谨的差异网格，贯穿整部电影的形式调度。这种叙事方式是显而易见地自我展示的。小津之后再也没有使用过如此张扬的摄影机运动，但他对这些运动的编排依旧保持了严密、近似抽象的结构。

——David Bordwell，《小津与电影诗学》（BFI/普林斯顿，1988）

Well into the master's post-war golden age, this often overlooked shoshimin-eiga (contemporary domestic drama) explores once again the generational divide Ozu was so interested in. A typically precise, almost Chekhovian screenplay, co-written as always with Kogo Noda, dissects a traditional marriage, which is fraying silently in the conflict between omiai ('arranged' marriage, as translated here and modern sensibilities.
Keiko Tsushima is the errant niece who rebels against the matchmaking, a dilemma confronted mostly by her aunt and uncle (Michiyo Kogure and Shin Saburi), whose own childless arranged union is marked by spite, lies, bullying and avoidance. In a rich and organic interweaving of supporting voices and perspectives, the primary dramatic crisis comes down to weighty silences and suddenly empty rooms - always Ozu's most resonant visual flourish - but tied into the gender war are eloquent critiques of the Japanese bourgeoisie (even the marriage in question boils down to class difference) and of the modern era's loss of respect and meaning.
Of course, Ozu leavens the dynamic with ambivalence, understanding and grey regions of doubt, and the ending reaches an awkward state of happy/sad we may feel within our rights to doubt, but the master's steady gaze alone can absolve us from cynicism.
Michael Atkinson, Sight and Sound, Summer 2020
Donald Richie, the Japanese-film expert, tells us that Yasujiro Ozu took meticulous care in his choice of settings, especially the small details that, according to Mr Richie, were always intended to emphasise character rather than environment. This is true, but the passage of time has added an environmental significance to the details originally chosen to give dimension to character. In Ozu's 1952 film, The Flavour of Green Tea over Rice, the environment that Ozu more or less took for granted, since he always made his films in their own time, seems now to play a more interesting - or at least more obvious - part in the drama.
The Ozu films we have seen previously have been social comedies, but they have been told entirely in terms of character. The Flavour of Green Tea looks as much like a social history as it does a classic Ozu work. The movie is essentially a comedy, what the Japanese call a tsuma-mono, or wife film, about an upper-middle-class marriage, one that has been arranged in the old-fashioned way and now is falling gently apart as the childless couple approach middle age. She (Michiyo Kogure) gives every indication of being a rather mean-spirited snob. Among other things, she sleeps apart from her husband in an awful American-style bedroom that seems to be constructed entirely of chintz. However, he (Shin Saburi) gives every indication of being as thickheaded and boring as she says he is. That is, at the beginning.
The revelation - the surprise - of the film is not that they are eventually reconciled, but that they become such appealing characters, touched by a kind of nobility. As important as the revelations of character - to me anyway - is the casual picture of the world in which they occur. I am not sure if it is factually true, but l have the impression that more of this film is set in public places, that is, locations outside the home that is the emotional centre of any Ozu film, than in his other films. What we see of Japan in 1951 and 1952 defines the time in a fashion I am not sure I would have been as aware of had I seen the film in
1952. It is a world only seven years removed from Hiroshima. Nobody in an Ozu film seems directly affected by the American occupation, but the American influence is everywhere, in second-hand clothes, in cigarettes, in the liberation
The world of this film is more geographically open than those of the other Ozus, but the economy of narrative and technique is practically quintessential.
Ozu never wastes our interest on connecting scenes if we can take them for granted. When he does show us a man proceeding, say from one office to another, it becomes important, perhaps as an acknowledgment of time lost or as a sort of film equivalent to the white space between the chapters in a novel.
No one shrug or sigh or smile is more or less dispensible than another gesture, which explains why minor characters are so important in Ozu films.
There are times - especially in its subplot about a girl who refuses traditional wedding arrangements - when The Flavour of Green Tea is almost formula comedy. No true Japanese formula comedy, however, would punctuate its climactic reconciliation scene, set in the kitchen that the wife is discovering for the first time, with the image, seen only out of the corner of the camera eye, of a can of Wesson oil.
Vincent Canby, The New York Times, 25 January 1973
The Flavour of Green Tea over Rice's most outrageous stylistic device is its camera movements. This is a complicated matter. There are camera movements which arise from the movement of a vehicle; camera movements which follow character movement; obliquely angled movements; short, frequent track-ins or track-outs in relation to spaces in the settings. There is, finally, the question of timing when in the shot's progress the camera movement starts. The narration uses these rigorously distinct options as a basis for playful variation. At the start of a scene, the camera will already be tracking back across a set; or after the characters leave, the camera will suddenly glide forward, toward no particular target; or it will allow the characters to walk out of sight into the distance and then start to track slowly forward, creating the most perversely fruitless of 'following shots'. (At this rate we will never catch up.) While there is symmetry and inversion among some of these movements, on the whole they can be understood only as abstract spatial markers, born of a rigid grid of differences and exhaustively deployed across the film, wielded by a narration that can flaunt its operations. Ozu would never again employ such flagrant camera movements, but it is characteristic of him to subject them to rigorous, semi-abstract organisation.
David Bordwell, Ozu and the Poetics of Cinema (BFI/Princeton, 1988)
