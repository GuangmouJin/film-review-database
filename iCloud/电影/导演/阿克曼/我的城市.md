影片如同是《让娜迪尔曼》的前身，众多重要元素都已经出现了，而片中的信如同《让娜迪尔曼》中的意外，堆积后的常态只需要一根稻草，掉落在其身就能毁掉一切，不过电影中的自毁对比与《让》中的杀人张力更加的弱。影片中音画分离的处理很有趣，不过与现场对不上的后配音效不咋喜欢，感觉有些出戏。

香特尔阿克曼在 18 岁时，在没有资金，没有机构支持的情况下，拍摄了她极具胆识的首部电影《炸毁我的城》（Saute ma ville），使用 35mm 胶片完成。

折页提及，制作方曾告知她不能仅仅呈现她电影中的片段，还必须出现在镜头前。在朗读接近尾声时，阿克曼谈到了母亲的沉默——她不愿意或无法诉说自己作为大屠杀幸存者的经历。而阿克曼还谈到了自己的外祖母，她“在还不到 18 岁时写了一本日记”。外祖母写道，她只能在日记中表达自己的内心想法，因为她是一个女人。写日记是她唯一能够让自己的思想得以表达，甚至让自己思考这些思想的方式。

在阿克曼看来，电影所呈现给观众的，始终是世界本身，而不仅仅是其中的个体。她的电影创造了一种观众不会忘记自己的观看体验，观众的体验取决于摄影机与空间的关系，因而也取决于时间。阿克曼说：“观众看着时间流逝，他与时间面对面。” 这种体验并不主要来自于摄影机对演员的关系，而更多来自于摄影机对空间的关系。

对于阿克曼而言，观众才是“他者”，电影让她与观众建立联系。她说：“我的观众与‘作为观众的我’、与‘作为导演的我’建立了一种关系。”导演的“我”对于观众来说是无形的，正如观众对于导演来说也是无形的，即便——或正因如此——“我的观众站在了我的位置上”。即使在《尚塔尔·阿克曼论尚塔尔·阿克曼》中，她本人出现在镜头前，这种观看关系依然没有改变。在所有的电影中，导演和观众都是无形的、隐藏的，而阿克曼的电影则明确宣告了这一观看的本质。

《炸毁我的城》（Saute ma ville）

尚塔尔·阿克曼在 18 岁时，在没有资金、没有机构支持的情况下，拍摄了她极具胆识的首部电影《炸毁我的城》（Saute ma ville），使用 35mm 胶片完成。

她回忆道：“有一天，我想拍一部关于我自己的电影。那就是《炸毁我的城》。我需要一台摄影机、一些胶片、一些灯光，还需要有人操作摄影机。我找了一个朋友帮忙拍摄，另一个朋友借给我一台摄影机，我们买了一点胶片，一晚上就拍完了。然后我自己剪辑了它。”

阿克曼曾在布鲁塞尔的INSAS 电影学院（比利时国家高级表演艺术与广播技术学院）学习了几个月，但因为无法立即开始拍摄电影而辍学。即使完成了《炸毁我的城》，身边的人仍然不认为她是一名电影人。她的孤独与不确定感让她最终离家去了巴黎，在那里待了两年，想进入电影行业却找不到方向。

关于影片内容，她这样描述：“你看到一个 18 岁的少女走进厨房，做着日常琐事，但方式却完全失控，最终走向自杀。” 她认为，这部影片是**《让娜·迪尔曼》的对立面**：“让娜代表着顺从，而这里是愤怒与毁灭。”

电影围绕日常的家务劳动展开，但喜剧或恐怖感来自于这些行为的失序。女孩哼唱着（“啦啦啦”），像是一个试图吸引注意力的烦躁儿童；她的每一个动作都像是内心崩溃的外化，比如她在镜子前那段令人不安的舞蹈。最终，她点燃了一封无法辨认内容的信件，将自己俯身靠近未点燃的煤气灶，煤气全开，最后发生爆炸。

这一场景的声音元素在《让娜·迪尔曼》中得到了延续：电影开场字幕出现之前，就能听到喷射出的煤气声，而这个声音每次让娜打开煤气灶时都会重复出现。

这部影片在实验室被搁置了两年，因为阿克曼没有钱去取回拷贝，也不确定它是否值得被看到。最终，实验室的负责人告诉她必须取走胶片，她便请对方观看并给出意见。对方不仅喜欢这部电影，还帮她联系了比利时电视台的资源，让她与埃里克·德·凯佩尔（Eric de Kuyper）取得联系。德·凯佩尔将《炸毁我的城》放入他的“另类电影”（Alternative Cinema）节目播出，次日，比利时最著名的导演**安德烈·德尔沃（André Delvaux）**在广播节目中给予了极高的评价。

阿克曼在一夜之间“成为了一名电影人”，至少在她自己眼里是这样。

尚塔尔·阿克曼的 INSAS 电影学院入学考试短片（INSAS Entrance Exam Films，1967）

1967 年夏天，尚塔尔·阿克曼在布鲁塞尔和克诺克拍摄了四部无声的黑白短片，这些作品是她申请比利时INSAS 电影学院（比利时国家高级表演艺术与广播技术学院）的入学考试作品。
	•	在布鲁塞尔，她拍摄了每年夏天举行的“南部博览会”（Foire du Midi）。
	•	在克诺克，她拍摄了这座海滨小镇的商店，短片的主角是她的童年好友玛丽莲·瓦特莱（Marilyn Watelet），后来两人共同创建了制片公司 Paradise Films。

阿克曼最终被 INSAS 录取，但只学习了几个月便辍学。讽刺的是，几十年后，她的电影资料被收录至比利时皇家电影资料馆（CINEMATEK），并成为尚塔尔·阿克曼基金会的基地（自 2006 年起）。






在 Chantal Akerman par Chantal Akerman（《尚塔尔·阿克曼论尚塔尔·阿克曼》）中，阿克曼朗读的文本由她自己创作，她称之为“碎片”（fragments）。这些文本不仅是对她电影作品的反思，也涉及个人经历、家庭历史、身份认同、女性经验等主题。

从文本内容来看，阿克曼的书写围绕几个核心主题展开：

1. 记忆与母亲的沉默

阿克曼的母亲是一名大屠杀幸存者，但她从不谈论自己的过去。在影片的朗读过程中，阿克曼提及母亲的沉默，这种沉默既是一种历史创伤的体现，也是阿克曼作品中反复出现的主题。例如，在《无家的世界》（D’Est）或《让娜·迪尔曼》中，时间缓慢流逝，角色的沉默充满压抑感，这种表现方式可以被看作是她对母亲不言说的历史的回应。

阿克曼并没有直接解释母亲的过往，而是以碎片化的方式呈现，这种断裂的叙事与大屠杀幸存者的创伤记忆形成了隐喻性的联系。她让观众感受母亲的沉默，而不是直接讲述它，这种做法也符合她一贯的电影风格——不解释，不情绪化，而是通过影像、节奏和空间让观众自己去体会。

2. 外祖母的日记与女性书写

阿克曼讲述了自己的外祖母在 18 岁时写日记的故事，日记中写道：

	“她只能在日记中表达自己的内心想法，因为她是一个女人。”

这种对于女性书写的限制，以及自我表达的困境，深深影响了阿克曼的创作。她的外祖母需要通过写作才能整理自己的想法，而阿克曼自己则选择了电影作为媒介。但她也承认，自己并没有像外祖母那样“意识到”作为女人的局限，她甚至戏谑地说道：

	“我喜欢这样想，即使这不是真的，因为她写下了这些，我就不必思考。我从来没有告诉自己：‘我是一个女人，因此……’”

这句话表面上似乎是一种自由的宣言，但实际上也流露出了一种无奈与矛盾——女性身份在她的创作中无可避免地被探讨，但她并不希望被局限于“女性导演”或“女性主义电影人”的标签。

这种对女性身份的微妙态度，也体现在她的电影里。例如，在《让娜·迪尔曼》中，女性日常生活的机械化再现成为了一种极端的表现，而在《炸毁我的城》（Saute ma ville）中，年轻女孩在厨房中的疯狂举动最终导致自毁，这些都可以被视为对女性生活被规训的反抗。

3. 电影的观看体验——导演、观众与“面对面”

阿克曼的文本还涉及电影的观看关系。她认为，电影不仅是导演与观众的交流，同时也是导演与自己作为观众的对话。在她看来，

	“我的观众与‘作为观众的我’、与‘作为导演的我’建立了一种关系。”

这种观看关系在她的电影风格中得到了体现：
	•	她偏好正面构图，但不是关注角色的脸，而是让观众直面空间、时间和沉默。例如，在《让娜·迪尔曼》中，观众面对的是厨房、客厅、卧室，而不是角色的直接表情。
	•	她认为，电影并不只是关于演员和情节，而是关于时间的流逝。她曾说：
	“观众看着时间流逝，他与时间面对面。”
这意味着，观众被迫感受时间，而不仅仅是跟随故事发展。

她的电影让观众成为“观察者”，而不仅仅是“情绪的接收者”。即使在 Chantal Akerman par Chantal Akerman 这样一部自画像电影里，她仍然在隐藏自己，并不是直接向观众倾诉，而是通过阅读这些“碎片”，让观众自行拼凑她的形象。

4. 个人与历史的交汇

阿克曼的文本虽然是关于她自己，但它并非单纯的自传，而是涉及历史、家庭记忆、女性经验等更大的主题。她的母亲的沉默、外祖母的日记、她自己的电影实践——这些都构成了一种跨世代的女性创作脉络。她并不直接表达情感，而是通过电影和文本创造体验，让观众在观看时自行感受那些缺席的声音。

她的“碎片化写作”与她的电影风格一致——既不是传统叙事，也不是完全抽象，而是在现实与感知之间游走，让观众成为主动的解读者，而不是被动接受者。

最终思考

阿克曼的文本并不是单纯的自传，而是个人经历、历史记忆、女性身份、电影观看方式等议题的交汇点。她的“碎片”书写方式，既体现了她对女性经验的敏感性，也反映了她对电影媒介的探索。在 Chantal Akerman par Chantal Akerman 这部电影中，她既呈现自己，也隐藏自己，让观众在阅读她的文本、观看她的影像的过程中，自己去拼凑她的形象——就像她的电影一样，拒绝给予简单的答案，而是让观众直面体验本身。

CHANTAL AKERMAN: ADVENTURES IN PERCEPTION
Chantal Akerman par

In Chantal Akerman par Chantal Akerman, the self-portrait film the Belgian filmmaker made in 1997 for the Cinéma, de notre temps series for French television, Chantal Akerman, on screen and framed frontally, reads out loud the pages she wrote - she calls them 'fragments' - after the producers told her she could not simply present passages from her films, but also had to appear on camera. As Akerman nears the end of her recitation, she speaks about her mother's silence - her unwillingness or inability to say anything about her experiences as a Holocaust survivor. And Akerman speaks about her mother's mother, who 'wrote a diary when she was not yet 18 years old. She wrote that she could only put her intimate thoughts in a diary because she was a woman.' Writing in a diary was the only way Akerman's grandmother could express her
'intimate thoughts', the only way she could make such thoughts known to herself, could even think those thoughts. 'So I tell myself, Akerman continues
'And I like to think, even if it isn't true, that because she wrote that, I didn't have to think at all. I never told myself: "I'm a woman, therefore ... whatever."
Visually, Akerman's films privilege frontality, but they do not privilege human faces or even human presences. Throughout Jeanne Dielman, for example, what Akerman consistently films frontally - what she puts the spectator face to face with - are the rooms in which the people find themselves, not the people who sometimes inhabit those rooms. Always, there is a back wall perpendicular to the camera axis, and walls and other strong verticals that rhyme with the vertical borders of the film frame to create frames-within-frames that give a strong, often near-symmetrical architectural structure to the images projected on the rectangular screen.
What Akerman presents to the spectator in her films, what the spectator is always facing, is the world projected on the movie screen - the world as a whole, not the people in it. The 'face-to-face-ness' that puts the spectator in the director's place, that 'creates a spectator who does not forget himself', is, in Akerman's view, a function of the camera's relation to space, hence to time.
(In her words: 'The spectator sees time going by; he's face-to-face with it.') Only indirectly is it a function of the camera's relation to the actors present in the frame. For Akerman, the spectator, not the actor, is the Other with whom her films put her in a relationship. As she says, 'My spectator is in a relationship with "me" as a spectator, with "me" as the director.' The director's 'me' is faceless to the spectator/Other, just as the spectator/Other is faceless to the director, even though - or because - 'my spectator is in my place.' This condition is not fundamentally challenged when, as in Chantal Akerman par Chantal Akerman, the director herself is the actor the spectator is facing, when it is Akerman's own face she is presenting. In all films, both director and spectator are faceless, in hiding, but Akerman's films explicitly declare this condition.

Saute ma ville


Akerman made her audacious gem of a first film when she was only 18, in 35mm, with no money and no institutional support. 'One day I wanted to make a film about myself. That was Saute ma ville. I needed a camera, some film, some lights and someone to operate the camera. I asked somebody I knew if he would help me make the film and somebody else loaned me a camera, we bought a little film stock and we made the film in one night. And then I edited


it. Akerman had spent several months in a Brussels film school (INSAS) before she dropped out because she wasn't allowed to plunge immediately into filmmaking. But even after she had completed Saute ma ville no one around her believed she was a filmmaker. Her sense of isolation and uncertainty was so great that she left home for Paris, where she stayed for two years. She wanted to work in films but she didn't know how.


Akerman describes Saute ma ville as follows: 'You see an adolescent girl, 18 years old, go into a kitchen, do ordinary things but in a way that is off-kilter, and finally commit suicide. The opposite of Jeanne Dielman: Jeanne, that was resignation. Here, it is rage and death' The adolescent is played by Akerman.


The film is structured around ordinary domestic routines; the humour, or horror, emerges from the way simple tasks veer out of control. The girl 'sings' (la-la-la-la) with the intrusiveness of a troubled child vying for attention; every gesture looks like the externalisation of psychic implosion, for instance, her disturbing dance with her mirror image. The girl's dislocated activity is the despairing prelude to suicide. The explosion that blows up not only 'her city' but first of all herself is set off when she sets fire to a letter (which we cannot read) as she leans over the stove with the unlit gas turned on full blast. A sound-image carried over from this scene opens Jeanne Dielman: before the names appear in the credits the loud sound of a jet of gas can be heard, a noise repeated every time Jeanne turns on the stove.


Saute ma ville sat in a lab for two years because Akerman didn't have the money to claim it and because she was so uncertain about its worth. Finally, when the head of the lab told her to take it away, she asked him to watch it and give her his opinion. Not only did he like it, he also gave her contacts in Belgian television, which led her to Eric de Kuyper, who broadcast Saute ma ville in his Alternative Cinema series. The next day Akerman heard André Delvaux, Belgium's best-known filmmaker, give her film a glowing radio review.
Overnight she became 'a filmmaker', not least in her own eyes.
Janet Bergstrom, Sight and Sound, November 1999

Akerman - Examen d'entrée INSAS - Knokke 1967 - Films 1, 2
INSAS entrance exam - Chantal Akerman - Knokke, films 1, 2

Shot on 8mm in Knokke in the summer of 1967, these are two of four mute, black and white short films that Chantal Akerman shot in Brussels and Knokke.


Chantal Akerman submitted them for the entrance exam at INSAS, (National Higher Institute of Performing Arts and Broadcasting Techniques), in Brussels.
She was accepted into the class of '67-68, but only studied there for a few months. The protagonist is played by her childhood friend Marilyn Watelet, with whom, in the '70s, she created the production company Paradise Films. This fiction in two parts takes place in the shops of this seaside town.
Akerman - Examen d'entrée INSAS - Bruxelles 1967 - Films 1, 2
INSAS entrance exam - Chantal Akerman 1967 - Brussels, films 1, 2
Shot on 8mm in Brussels in the summer of 1967, these are two of four mute, black and white short films that Chantal Akerman shot in Brussels and Knokke.
For the first film, Akerman filmed the Foire du Midi, which takes place every summer in the south of Brussels. The second film features her childhood friend Marilyn Watelet, with whom, in the '70s, she created the production company Paradise Films. It also features Marilyn's mother and sister, Nicole and Claudine Watelet. Chantal Akerman filmed the courtyard of the old Hôtel de Clèves-Ravenstein, not far from a favourite haunt of theirs, the Cinema Museum of the Royal Film Archive of Belgium (CINEMATEK). Coincidentally, since 2006, this has been the home of Chantal Akerman Foundation, which was founded with the support of CINEMATEK.
