# 《来自东方》（, D’Est  1993）——阿克曼的凝视与后苏联世界的沉默诗篇

# 

《来自东方》（**, D’Est  1993）——阿克曼的凝视与后苏联世界的沉默诗篇**

**导演**：尚塔尔·阿克曼（Chantal Akerman）
**国家**：比利时/法国/德国
**类型**：纪录片、实验电影
**时长**：110分钟

⸻

**1. 电影简介**

《来自东方》（*D’Est*，意为“来自东部”）是比利时导演**尚塔尔·阿克曼（Chantal Akerman）于1993年拍摄的一部非传统纪录片**。该片没有明确的叙事主线，而是通过**静态镜头、长时间的移动拍摄以及沉默的观察**，呈现了冷战结束后，**前东欧社会主义国家**（包括俄罗斯、波兰、乌克兰、德国等地）日常生活的面貌。

#BFI 纪录片，无故事，完全的监视器的凝视呈现了各个国家群众的状态，其实很难从画面中明确国家，只能从着装和环境音的言语中简单分辨，毕竟地点不需要明确，需要明确的只有那片土地。影片中的镜头不光对准城市的街道，还有田野间和屋子内。大量的长镜头的效果非常好，缓慢反复是在深掘这个画面内时间残留的影子，环绕镜头则有一种宿命轮回之感，有场环绕的印象太深了，感觉到一种莫名的无力感。画面中飞驰的汽车，行走的人们和那一张张面孔都似乎承载了历史的痕迹，感觉《南方》采用了不少类似的镜头呈现类似的事物，都是以风景诉说土地上的历史。另一个印象较深的是光影色彩上的变化，影片中假户外的冷色调和室内的暖色对照还是很明显的，家内的固定镜头对准一个角色的呈现与户外相同，但当音乐会和舞会出现时还是能感受到一丝丝讽刺，毕竟寒风中站立的人和冰冷的街道与翩翩起舞的人和音乐大厅对照还是有些讽刺。这里也不禁思考…现实与虚幻的边界。


**2. 影评：阿克曼的反凝视与时间的沉默**

这种对比强化了一种**历史与现实的断裂感**：**东欧的过去已经消失，但未来仍然模糊不清**，人们被困在一个不确定的过渡期里。

⸻

**(2) 后苏联时代的精神状态**

在苏联解体之后，东欧国家经历了从社会主义向资本主义的剧烈过渡。

阿克曼用镜头记录了这种**集体性的沉默与迷茫**：
	•	**冷漠的面孔**：人们在街头站立、行走、等待，但他们的表情没有喜悦，更多的是疲惫、无言和焦虑。


	•	**无尽的等待**：火车站、长长的队伍、候车室里的人群……这种**“静止的时间”**成为电影的主要视觉主题之一，暗示着一个社会在历史变革后陷入的停滞状态。
	•	**空间的荒凉感**：破败的建筑、冰冷的街道、萧条的广场，都让人感受到苏联体制崩溃后的社会现实——**新的秩序尚未建立，旧的系统已然崩溃**。

⸻

**(3) 反凝视（Counter-Gaze）：被观看的主体**

阿克曼的镜头语言是**反凝视的**，这与传统的西方纪录片（尤其是关于东欧的影像）形成了鲜明对比：
	•	传统纪录片往往是带着**西方的凝视**去观察东欧，以分析或猎奇的方式呈现苏联解体后的社会状况。
	•	但阿克曼的镜头**不去介入、不过度诠释，而是让被摄者的存在本身成为影像的核心**。
	•	**凝视的主体性**：许多东欧人在镜头前停留，他们直视镜头，形成了一种反向的凝视，仿佛在质问观众：“你为什么要看着我们？我们是谁？我们该去哪里？”

这种反凝视让电影成为了一种**观看的困境**：观众被迫思考，他们究竟在“看”什么？他们是否真正理解这些人？

阿克曼的冷静观察，使东欧人民的面孔成为历史的碎片，**不带任何意识形态的立场，而是让现实本身去诉说它的故事**。

⸻

**(4) 电影的沉默：声音的缺失与环境声的力量**

整部电影几乎**没有对白、没有旁白、没有解释**，只有环境声音：
	•	**风声、火车的轰鸣、街头的喧嚣、人们脚步的回响**……这些声音强化了电影的现实感，也让时间显得更加漫长和压抑。
	•	这种声音处理方式让影片充满**疏离感**，观众无法通过语言获得“意义”，只能**沉浸在影像的情绪中**，去感受那种无声的历史重量。

这种无言的状态成为了**历史的隐喻**——苏联已经消失，但后苏联社会还没有找到新的叙述方式，人们只能在沉默中继续前行。

⸻

**(5) “东方”与“西方”之隔**

片名《来自东方》（D’Est）直接指向了**欧洲的东西方对立**：
	•	在冷战时期，**西欧（资本主义）和东欧（社会主义）形成对立**，阿克曼的电影在苏联解体后，站在“西方”导演的立场，重新观察“东方”。
	•	但她并没有采取西方主流电影中的“救赎式”叙事（如西方媒体报道东欧的贫困、苦难和被解放的幻想），而是让影像本身去表现历史的痕迹，让东欧人的沉默成为唯一的答案。

在这样的观看关系中，**“东方”不再是“西方”的他者，而是一个自身难以言说的主体**，而阿克曼的镜头为其提供了一个无声却充满力量的窗口。

⸻

**3. 结论：一部关于时间、历史与凝视的电影**

《来自东方》是一部超越传统纪录片框架的作品，它既是一部关于**后苏联世界的观察**，也是一部关于**时间、空间与观看的实验电影**。阿克曼以其标志性的**长镜头、沉默、环境声**，让观众感受到**一种历史过渡时期的失落与迷惘**。

它既不是关于过去的纪念，也不是关于未来的希望，而是关于**一种未被定义的、悬置的当下**。这正是影片最有力的地方——它不告诉你答案，它让你去感受一种正在消逝但尚未被书写的历史。

**在阿克曼的镜头下，东欧不是被解读的对象，而是自身的见证者。它不说话，它只存在。**



D'Est (From the East)
When you try to show reality in cinema, most of the time it's totally false. But when you show what's going on in people's minds that's very cinematic.'
- Chantal Akerman
If I had to describe the art of Chantal Akerman in a single word, I think I'd opt for
'composition'. This is a term that needs to be understood in its plastic as well as its musical meanings: a visual object that has to be framed in space, a musical object that has to be composed in time. And if we factor in the implied definitions offered above by Akerman regarding what's reality and what's cinematic, what's going on in people's minds and what's going on in front of a camera and microphone, then we have to acknowledge that what she chooses to compose represents a kind of uneasy truce between all four elements (or five elements, if we regard sound and image as separate). How much she and we privilege mind over matter and cinema over reality - or vice versa - has a lot of bearing on what's derived from the encounter.
The documentaries D'Est (1993), Sud (South, 1999), De l'autre côté (From the Other Side, 2002) and Là-bas (Down There, 2006) are all self-described in relation to place. Yet all are deeply involved with displacement, thanks to the uneasy truces between their elements that l've just alluded to. [...] Freed from the tyranny imposed by the necessity of following stories and characters, Akerman's best documentaries can arguably call on a fuller range of both her aesthetic resources and the imaginations of her viewers, which is implied by the title she gave to her 1995 installation related to D'Est, Bordering on Fiction.
Returning to Akerman's statement about what's cinematic, I think it's fair to say that D'Est, Sud, De l'autre côté and Là-bas are only nominally and incidentally films about a journey from Berlin to Moscow, or the town of Jasper, Texas, or the border between the United States and Mexico, or a narrow patch in Tel Aviv mainly consisting of a room in Akerman's rented flat and its immediate environs. More basically, they're films about the states of Akerman's consciousness, her thoughts and feelings of displacement and alienation, in relation to those places. Such displacements can be found throughout her work - perhaps most memorably in two films linking her sense of homelessness with the figure of her mother: News from Home (1976), occasioning what was perhaps her first major use of autobiographical and documentary materials (letters from her mother that are read by Chantal over shots of New York), and No Home Movie (2015), her final film.
Akerman's personal investments - or lack of same - in the places that she films has a great deal of relevance to her thoughts and feelings about them. Born in Brussels as the daughter of Polish Jews who were Holocaust survivors - including a mother, Natalia, whose own parents and other relatives perished in Auschwitz - and with one younger sister who eventually emigrated to Mexico (and who appears, along with Natalia and Chantal herself, in No Home Movie), she maintained throughout her career a refusal to participate in identity politics regarding her sexuality or her feminism outside the realm of her filmmaking, although her identity as a Jew remained crucial. Apart from her Judaism, she refused to accept tribal definitions and categories, although she was by no means apolitical or unaffiliated in her positions. Thus her voyage from Germany to Russia shortly after the fall of the Berlin Wall in D'Est is directly inflected by the fates of other Eastern Europeans at that historical moment; her lack of a personal connection to the American South in Sud (apart from her horrified response to the brutal murder of a Black man by several white men in jasper, Texas) obliged her to forego much of her customary lyricism in that film and to function mostly as a journalist (not generally her forte); her own perceived status as a homeless alien who moved between Brussels and Paris (and, less often, New York) for most of her career informed her treatment of borders in De l'autre côté, as, more specifically, did her various visits to her sister in Mexico; and her difficulty in engaging with Israel in Là-bas has to be seen in relation to her father's decision not to settle there after the war, choosing instead to move to Brussels, which she describes in some detail in her closing monologue. The haunting masterpiece D'Est documents without any commentary, dialogue, or subtitles Akerman's several months long trip from East Germany to Moscow, offering a formally rigorous inventory of what the former Soviet bloc looked and felt like at the time. Yet despite the overall narrative trajectory, Akerman's painterly penchant for finding Edward Hopper wherever she goes has never been more obvious. This travelogue seemingly offers vistas that any alert tourist could find, yet it delivers a series of images and sounds that are impossible to shake afterwards, seeping into our consciousness as if by osmosis: the countless tracking shots of street life, the sense of people forever waiting, the rare occurrence of a plaintive offscreen cello over an otherwise densely (and sometimes deceptively) ambient sound track, an onscreen solo cello performance (by the previous cellist's teacher), static glimpses of roadside sites and domestic interiors, everyday activities inside other domestic interiors, the periphery of an outdoor rock concert, a heavy Moscow snowfall, and a crowded terminal where weary people and baggage are huddled together like so many dropped handkerchiefs. The only other film I know that imparts such a vivid sense of being somewhere is the Egyptian section of Straub-Hullet's 1982 Too Early, Too Late. Everyone goes to movies in search of events, but the extraordinary events in Akerman's sorrowful, intractable film are mainly the shots themselves.
