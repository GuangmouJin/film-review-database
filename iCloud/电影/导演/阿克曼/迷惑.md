# 迷惑



#BFI 长评-无时无刻的在场和永恒的停留。影片给我感觉有些瘆人，除了呈现了男性对于女性凝视及变态的占有欲似乎还带有一个很强的恋尸癖的表达。男主对女主的所有行动都会产生怀疑，可以说他不想女主离开他的视野，甚至想控制她的想法，而且性欲呈现的时机都是在女主“睡熟”时，其实这点跟希区柯克《迷魂记》的恋尸癖表达类似。角色是布列松极简主义那种模特演出，女主在影片中是被极致客体化的，这种方式带有极强的讽刺性，同时看到第二次熟睡的性爱时我就大概率感觉到她会在结尾死亡。视听上强化了男主对女主的控制，画面与声音的结合让观众切身感受到这场病态关系的窒息感，男主一直在场。而相反的是女主大量的表达都被压抑，她**在男主的世界里并不真正“存在”**，她只是一个被观察的对象，甚至她的精神都不重要，可能需要的是熟睡永恒的停留。镜头上依然是非常克制，**大量固定镜头和长时间的凝视**都很像她作品风格。





只是本片的叙事性更强，所以剪辑还是相对较多。还有类似男主POV的呈现和越过男主观众的凝视，似乎观众被迫与这种窥视达成某种共谋使得影片的观看体验更加的压抑。听觉上则是使用了大量的留白，偶有出现的配乐还是帮助了叙事的部分，但那音效的使用就极其诡异了，不论是高跟鞋踩下的声音，还是男主走路木地板的声音，亦或者呼吸声，都似乎强调了男主那无时无刻的在场。而相反的是女主，她大量的表达都被压抑，似乎就如同这个社会对她所做的，她的人**在男主的世界里并不真正“存在”**，她只是一个被观察的对象，甚至她的精神都不重要，可能只有身体对男主有存在的价值。





#BFI 上周看的《焦虑》让我感觉有些恶心，这部则给我感觉有些瘆人，电影除了呈现了男性对于女性凝视及变态的占有欲之外，还夹杂了对于女同性恋身份的不解，同时似乎还带有一个很强的恋尸癖的表达。

角色上，基本是布列松极简主义那种模特演出，大量的角色情绪波动不大，这似乎也可以更好的传递电影的表达，男主就像我上面说的多少带有些“恋尸癖”的感觉，女主则明显是有同性恋的身份，那个“闭上眼睛可以幻想对方是别人”的设定可以看出女主跟形影不离的朋友是恋人，毕竟她醒的时候喊的是她的名字，而二人开场出现在录像中的部分被男主解读则可以从两方面都解释的通，一方面实锤二人的恋爱关系，另一方面则是男主的疑心病。女主在影片中是被极致客体化的，这种方式带有极强的讽刺性，同时看到第二次熟睡的性爱时我就大概率感觉到她会在结尾死亡，只是男主营救她时似乎被她“伏击”了，让我当时有一阵犹豫。

视听上，还是强化了男主对女主的控制，画面与声音的结合让观众切身感受到这场病态关系的窒息感。导演对于空间的呈现一直做的很好，这部也不例外，封闭的房间，窄长的走廊，重复的公路行驶都像是一种看不见的牢笼将女主困在男主的世界里。镜头上依然是非常克制，**大量固定镜头和长时间的凝视**都很像她作品风格，只是本片的叙事性更强，所以剪辑还是相对较多。还有类似男主POV的呈现和越过男主观众的凝视，似乎观众被迫与这种窥视达成某种共谋使得影片的观看体验更加的压抑。听觉上则是使用了大量的留白，偶有出现的配乐还是帮助了叙事的部分，但那音效的使用就极其诡异了，不论是高跟鞋踩下的声音，还是男主走路木地板的声音，亦或者呼吸声，都似乎强调了男主那无时无刻的在场。而相反的是女主，她大量的表达都被压抑，似乎就如同这个社会对她所做的，她的人**在男主的世界里并不真正“存在”**，她只是一个被观察的对象，甚至她的精神都不重要，可能只有身体对男主有存在的价值。

在阿克曼的电影体系里，《迷惑》是她少数对类型片元素进行解构的作品之一，但它的核心依然延续了她对于空间、性别和控制的讨论。**在男性构建的世界里，女性永远没有真正的自由，而当她们试图挣脱时，往往只能走向自我毁灭。**




视觉上，


《囚禁》是一部**伦敦院线中独一无二的电影**。如果**导演尚塔尔·阿克曼（Chantal Akerman）的名字让你联想到先锋派、激进的女性主义电影**，那这次你可能会错。



	“我已经忘记自己曾经是多么难以相处。我现在在业内遇到一些人，打招呼时他们会无视我，因为他们仍然记得我 20 年前有多惹人厌。”

⸻

普鲁斯特的**“囚徒”**

《囚禁》改编自**普鲁斯特《追忆似水年华》中的《囚徒》（La Prisonnière）篇章。在书中，贵族叙述者马塞尔（Marcel）讲述了自己与情妇阿尔贝蒂娜（Albertine）之间痛苦纠结的关系——他口头上宣称对她无动于衷，但行为上却表现出极端的占有欲**。

在阿克曼的电影中，**这个“痴迷的爱人”变成了西蒙（Simon）**，一个**当代富家青年**。他不仅让**共同的女性朋友安德蕾（Andrée）监视并陪伴女友阿丽安（Ariane）**，还如同**跟踪狂一样尾随她**。

相较于原著，**阿克曼加重了西蒙对阿丽安女性朋友（其中一些人几乎可以肯定是她的情人）的嫉妒**。西蒙不仅想知道**她们“做了什么”**，更想知道**她们在想什么**——但他既无法进入**阿丽安的内心**，也无法理解**同性恋的视角**。

⸻

窥视与占有

《囚禁》开场是一段**业余家庭录像**，拍摄的是**阿丽安、安德蕾及几位女性朋友**在**诺曼底海滩度假时的嬉戏**。她们展现出**封闭的亲密感**，仿佛**蒙娜丽莎般神秘的微笑**在她们之间流转。

有一刻，**阿丽安直视镜头**，似乎在**挑战观众去揭开她的身份**。随后，观众才意识到，这段影像是**西蒙在放映的**——他试图通过**唇语解读阿丽安**在视频中对安德蕾所说的话。他的结论是：**“我很喜欢你。”**

镜头一转——
阿丽安身穿**夏裙和高跟鞋**，自信地穿越巴黎**旺多姆广场（Place Vendôme）**，然后上了一辆**敞篷奔驰**。此时，**西蒙坐在劳斯莱斯**里默默注视着她，随后尾随而去。

这一跟踪场景的拍摄方式，**让人不禁联想到**希区柯克的《**迷魂记**》（Vertigo）——特别是**斯科蒂·弗格森（Scottie Ferguson，詹姆斯·斯图尔特饰）**在旧金山尾随**玛德琳·埃尔斯特（Madeleine Elster，金·诺瓦克饰）**的段落。

更具象征意义的是：
	•	**玛德琳的名字**可能本就暗示了普鲁斯特的**玛德莱娜小蛋糕（Madeleine）**——《追忆似水年华》中关于记忆的关键隐喻。
	•	配乐选用**拉赫玛尼诺夫的《死之岛》（The Isle of the Dead）**，其气氛与希区柯克的御用作曲家**伯纳德·赫尔曼（Bernard Herrmann）**的风格十分相似。

当西蒙尾随阿丽安走上**蒙马特（Montmartre）的陡峭楼梯时，她的高跟鞋敲击地面的声音与西蒙静默的滑行**形成鲜明对比——他几乎像**马格利特画作中的漂浮人物**，在**德·基里科风格的巴黎**中游走。

最终，西蒙尾随阿丽安来到一间**酒店**，但前台却告诉他，**阿丽安订的是她姑妈的房间，而不是自己的**。此刻，电影的氛围突然变得**荒诞而超现实**，让人想起**布努埃尔（Buñuel）电影中那些被现实困住的资产阶级**。

⸻

电影的独特时间感

《囚禁》的独特之处在于**它对于时间的掌控**。

在一场博物馆戏中，**阿丽安在画展中游走，西蒙在远处注视着她**——
	•	**她的高跟鞋**在木地板上敲出清脆的回音。
	•	**西蒙的步伐**缓慢而沉重，使地板**发出吱嘎作响的呻吟**。

这场**看似简单的“躲猫猫”游戏**，因为**时间的被拉长**，反而变得**梦幻般地紧张**。

⸻

爱与痴迷的循环

阿克曼始终**让观众处于西蒙的视角**——
	•	他是一个**控制欲极强的混蛋**，把阿丽安视作**自己的财产**。
	•	但与此同时，**女人们似乎都在欺骗他**——甚至连**安德蕾**（他的“盟友”）**也可能是阿丽安的情人**。

当西蒙在**阿丽安熟睡时干涩地摩擦自己**，阿丽安却在梦呓中**喊出安德蕾的名字**。这一场景的**双重性**正是**影片的关键**：
	•	**阿丽安的可能出轨**，
	•	**西蒙的控制与妄想**，
	•	**两人关系中无止境的仪式化痴迷**。

⸻

被囚禁的究竟是谁？

**西蒙被困在自己的想象里，阿丽安则被困在他的掌控中。**

阿克曼成功地展现了**人与人之间不可逾越的鸿沟**——爱与痴迷、掌控与自由、理解与误解，最终都化为**无解的谜**。

这部电影不会**在第一幕**，甚至不会在**第一次观看**时就揭晓它的全部意义——而这，正是它成为**纯粹电影体验**的原因。



A contemporary review
La Captive is a film like no other currently on London's screens, but if the director's name, Chantal Akerman, puts you in mind of an avant-garde, confrontationally feminist cinema from the past, you'd be wrong this time.
Similarly, if the fact that La Captive is another Proust adaptation made in the wake of Raul Ruiz's magisterial Le Temps retrouvé with the same producer, Paolo Branco, prompts you to expect more Visconti-like opulence and moving scenery, such preconceptions should be banished. La Captive does explore territory that once nourished many auteurs - the very borders between sexual obsession, dream logic and madness where Hitchcock and Buñuel plied their trade but it now seems Akerman's alone.
All the autobiography and deconstruction we've come to expect of Akerman since her 1975 breakthrough feature Jeanne Dielmann 23, Quai du commerce,
1080 Bruxelles are here subsumed to the cause of creating a truly cinematic world of allure, distraction and unknowing. And it may match a new mood in the director herself: 'I forget how difficult I used to be. I see some people in the industry and I say hello and they ignore me because they still remember I was obnoxious 20 years ago.'
La Captive adapts the section of Marcel Proust's A la recherche du temps perdu titled La Prisonnière, in which Proust's well-born narrator Marcel describes his tortuous relationship with his live-in mistress Albertine. To himself Marcel professes his indifference to his mistress, but outwardly he behaves with extreme jealous possessiveness. The obsessive lover in Akerman's film is Simon, a very rich young man of the present day. He not only uses a mutual female friend Andrée to spy on and chaperone his girlfriend Ariane, he himself follows Ariane like a stalker. His jealousy of Ariane's female friends (some of whom are almost certainly her lovers) is more pronounced than in Proust.
Simon wants to know not just 'what goes on' between women, but what they're thinking, and the unattainability of his girlfriend's mind is compounded only by the unattainability of the lesbian worldview.
La Captive opens with amateur film footage of the Normandy-beach frolics of Ariane, Andrée and several other women on holiday together, who all display a communal air of closed-off intimacy, a collective Gioconda smile. At one moment Ariane, a wiry, freckled presence, looks directly at the camera as if challenging the audience to discover who she is. Then we find out that the silent amateur film is being projected by Simon, and that he's trying to lip-read what Ariane is saying as she stands next to Andrée. 'I like you very much,' is his conclusion. Cut to Ariane in a summer dress and high heels striding confidently through a near-deserted Place Vendòme in Paris. She's being watched as she gets into her open-top Mercedes by Simon, in the driving seat of his Rolls. As we see her move off from Simon's viewpoint and the Rolls follows behind, the shooting style reminds us irresistibly of police detective Scottie Ferguson (James Stewart) tailing Madeleine Elster (Kim Novak - whose character is undoubtedly named with Proust in mind) through the streets of San Francisco in Vertigo, especially as the orchestral music swells: a doom-laden, watery piece by Rachmaninov, The Isle of the Dead, that's highly reminiscent of Hitchcock composer Bernard Hermann. Soon Simon is following Ariane at a distance as she climbs one of the steep stairways of Montmartre. The click-clack of her determined progress is in stark contrast with Simon's entranced silence - he seems to glide with his arms fixed at his sides, like a Magritte figure floating through a De Chirico Paris. But when he reaches the hotel he's seen Ariane enter and the receptionist tells him she's booked a room for her aunt, not for herself, we're thrown into another surrealist's arms - those of Buñuel and his discreetly charming but bewildered bourgeoisie.
It would be all too easy to over-emphasise the myriad influences here, which amount to nothing more than a drinking in of cinema on Akerman's part.
What's completely unique is La Captive's extraordinary sense of time. As Ariane explores an art museum under Simon's gaze, the tension is excruciating.
Ariane's heels clomp on the wooden floorboards as she strides through the exhibits, seemingly in real time, while Simon's slower steps only make the floor groan and creak. The elastic time frame of this ridiculous bout of hide-and-seek is typically dreamlike. It's clear Ariane must know what Simon gets up to and it may be part of the game they play in which sex can occur only after he's pretended to do something else and she's fallen asleep. You could widen that out and suggest that his suspicions of her lesbianism are equally part of their rituals and that without them the obsession would disappear.
You can read it either way, especially as Akerman makes sure her film always puts us in Simon's position. Though he's a shit who behaves as if he owns Ariane - in one scene he even drags her away from an opera-house reception without the slightest nod towards social niceties - the women do all seem as if they're lying to him. Andrée - his supposed confidante and Ariane's chaperone
- is also number-one suspect as Ariane's lover (while Simon dry-humps her sleeping form, Ariane calls out Andrée's name). These dualities all contribute to the feeling of a work of pure cinema, one that doesn't give up its meanings in the first reel, or even at the first screening. In portraying the enigma of woman from a man's point of view, Akerman successfully reveals the real enigma of the unknowability of any human being to another. And of course Simon, with his obsession and his pollen allergies (which seem analogous to post-coital disgust), is as much of a captive of his own imagination as Ariane.
