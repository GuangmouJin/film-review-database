《那里》（**Là-bas, 2006）——影评与分析**



#BFI 长评-窗帘相隔的两个空间。极简主义作品，跟她大部分的“凝视”类型的作品相似，视觉的虚幻及听觉的现实共同构建了一个稳定的时间流动性，不论是电话对话还是窗帘背后或缝隙的现实生活都似乎极其稳定。基本都是固定镜头，从公寓向窗外的长镜头基本都是由床帘隔开，我们很难分清楚窗帘前后的两个空间是否相连，就如同她与那窗外景色始终存在着“隔阂”，她依然不属于这里。她的旁白断断续续的并不连贯，关于家庭关于身份还有关于死亡，结尾处的飞机轰鸣更像是某种对于战争思考的呈现，以此作为结尾感觉她对于自己身份及这部电影的思考是极其复杂的。看完还是有些难受，不论是她探讨的死亡在自己身上发生还是现在以色列的局势的变化都让人感到唏嘘，回想《另一边》的美墨边境问题，《南方》的美国种族问题，《来自东方》苏联解体后的情况，似乎她的视角是有预见性一般，亦或者她窥见了那历史重复的可能。







	•	**导演**：香特尔·阿克曼（Chantal Akerman）
	•	**类型**：实验纪录片 / 个人随笔
	•	**主题**：流亡、身份认同、孤独、创伤、空间的界限

**1. 影片讲述了什么？**

窗帘相隔的两个空间

#BFI 极简主义作品，比斯诺的作品多了一些私人的思考，比梅卡斯的作品多了一些视觉的克制，其实跟她大部分的“凝视”类型的作品相似，同样是对“土地”及土地上的“人”的凝视，不过加入了电话对话和独白形式的台词，整体更像是《家乡的消息》那种感觉，把城市换成了一间公寓内对外的画面呈现出的效果更佳私人，也更加的压抑，而且他们似乎有着相似的隔阂，纽约街头的嘈杂与特拉维夫外景窗帘的遮挡。影片独特的视听塑造了一个非常美妙的时空，视觉上的虚幻及听觉上的现实共同构建了一个稳定的时间流动性，不论是电话对话还是窗帘背后或缝隙的现实生活都似乎极其稳定。电影的主题依然关乎于身份认同，这个主题也是出现在她的大量作品中。

视觉上，大基本都是固定镜头，从公寓向窗外的长镜头基本都是由床帘隔开，那种朦胧感带有一丝虚幻感，我们很难分清楚窗帘前后的两个空间是否相连，就如同她与那窗外景色始终存在着“隔阂”，感觉她依然不属于这里，就如同纽约一样，似乎也只有那缝隙提醒着观众那近在咫尺的现实。

听觉上，她的旁白断断续续的并不连贯，而且有些确实挺不太清楚，不过能感觉不少关于她的回忆和思考，关于家庭关于身份，还有关于死亡，低语音调更像是某种自白。结尾处的飞机轰鸣更像是某种对于战争思考的呈现，以此作为结尾感觉她对于自己身份及这部电影的思考是极其复杂的。

看完还是有些难受，不论是她探讨的死亡在自己身上发生还是现在以色列的局势的变化都让人感到唏嘘，回想《另一边》的美墨边境问题，《南方》的美国种族问题，《来自东方》苏联解体后的情况，似乎她的视角是有预见性一般，亦或者她窥见了那历史重复的可能。


**声音**在影片中发挥着至关重要的作用：
	•	影片中，我们听到阿克曼**低沉的嗓音**，讲述自己的家庭、童年、犹太身份认同。
	•	她还提及自己如何**在公寓里打破东西、吃掉食物**，并思考如何向房东赔偿。**这种琐碎的自省成为一种英雄主义行为**，因为她最终不得不**走上街头**去寻找替代品，而在战争氛围下，这种普通的外出行为本身就带有不安和危险。
	•	她的回忆涉及**她的姑姑的自杀**，以及**以色列作家阿摩司·奥兹（Amos Oz）母亲的自杀**，这些私人历史与**新闻广播中关于自杀式爆炸袭击**的报道形成对照。

此外，影片的环境音也加强了战争的现实感：
	•	影片中，阿克曼的**叙述与战斗机划破天空的轰鸣声**交织在一起，这成为本片**最明确的战争痕迹**。







**(4) 以色列的历史与现实**
	•	影片拍摄于2006年，当时**以色列正经历黎巴嫩战争**，冲突随时可能爆发。但影片没有直接拍摄战争，而是让这种**不安感隐含在画面和旁白里**。
	•	她对以色列的情感是**复杂的**，既有历史记忆，也有现实的疏离，她既认同这里是“犹太人之地”，但又质疑这片土地上存在的暴力与冲突。

**3. 台词围绕什么？**

阿克曼的旁白**断断续续**，有时是她自己的思考，有时是她的回忆，有时是对外界的观察。她的语言风格**私密、低语，像是在与自己对话**。
	•	**关于孤独**：
	•	“我并不真正属于这里，但我也不属于其他任何地方。”
	•	**关于空间的封闭感**：
	•	“我待在这里，待在这个房间里，我看着窗外，时间流过。”
	•	**关于犹太身份**：
	•	“这里的人们像我，但又不像我。”
	•	**关于创伤**：
	•	“母亲的故事一直在我脑海里回响，但她从未真正讲述过它。”

这些台词没有明显的叙事线索，**它们像是一种思绪的流动**，更接近文学随笔的方式，而非传统纪录片的

**《彼岸》（Là-bas）**

折页提及，阿克曼她从未计划拍摄一部关于以色列的电影。她深信**中立立场并不存在**，她的主观性会成为干扰。她曾确信，**只有当自己身处以色列之外时，才能真正思考“以色列问题”**。

然而，直到她在**特拉维夫大学**任教，拿起相机，并“找到”合适的影像后，她才决定制作这部电影。影片记录了阿克曼**独自在特拉维夫一间临海公寓中度过的一段短暂时光**。她将**室内戏剧（chamber play）**推向极致——影片几乎完全局限于房间内部。

她的拍摄视角始终来自公寓内部，而在她的旁白中，她谈论自己的家庭、犹太身份以及童年经历。她思考：在这样一个地方，**日常生活是否可能？电影创作是否现实可行？**

阿克曼并未带着预设的计划去拍摄，她希望自己尽可能地**保持开放和空白**，让一切自然而然地发生。

—— **Icarus Films**

⸻

阿克曼的**“书信”电影**

在她从**《让娜·迪尔曼》(Jeanne Dielman, 1975)** 到 **《囚徒》(La Captive, 2000)** 这些叙事长片确立她在世界电影史地位的同时，阿克曼也创作了**约20部作品**（涵盖电影、表演、展览装置等），其基本形式是“**书信电影（letter film）**”。

这些书信电影有时来自他人寄给她的信件，有时是她写给他人的信，但**大多数情况下，是她写给自己**的信。

这些信件：
	•	既**像日记**般私密，
	•	又比普通日记**更具正式结构**。

阿克曼用她**低沉、极具辨识度的嗓音**朗读这些信件，使声音成为影像的**个人化叙述**。

这些影像因**重复、几何式构图、冥想般的节奏**而具有某种抽象感，而阿克曼的旁白则为这些影像赋予了叙事性。

⸻

《彼岸》**(Là-bas) 的独特性**

本片在**马赛国际纪录片电影节（FIDMarseille）获得大奖**，并入围法国电影凯撒奖。

这部电影既是阿克曼**最脆弱**的作品之一，也是她**最具力量**的作品之一。

影片**大部分**在**特拉维夫**的一间公寓内拍摄。这间公寓是阿克曼在任教期间租住的。她的长期制片人**泽维尔·卡尼奥（Xavier Carniaux）** 鼓励她借此机会拍一部关于以色列的电影。然而，作为**大屠杀幸存者的女儿**，阿克曼起初**强烈抵触**，因为她认为这个议题**过于复杂，而她自身的情感联系也太过深厚**。

然而，她还是带了一台小型**DV相机**，某天，她拿起相机，将镜头**对准公寓的窗户和阳台**，拍摄对面建筑的窗户、阳台和露台。

⸻

影像与风格
	•	影片由一系列**固定机位**的长镜头构成，每个镜头长达**数分钟**，主要拍摄公寓对面的建筑、窗户和阳台。
	•	**窗户的竹制百叶帘**是视觉的核心元素。百叶帘与窗户**不完全贴合**，使得有些角度可以通过玻璃直接看到外部世界。
	•	**窗户有时开着，有时关着，有时被另一扇百叶门遮挡**，形成**不断变化的视觉层次**。
	•	观众可以透过窗户看到对面的住户日常生活，例如**一位老人不停地摆弄植物，在露台上踱步，宛如笼中困兽**。
	•	随着影片进行，我们逐渐注意到**光线的变化**——角度、强度，以及光如何被对面建筑阻挡，如何穿过半透明的百叶帘过滤进来。

⸻

阿克曼的影像哲学
	•	阿克曼受**极简先锋电影（Minimalist Avant-Garde）**影响极深。
	•	本片的窗户构图让人不禁联想到**迈克尔·斯诺 (Michael Snow) 的《波长》(Wavelength, 1967)**，那部电影也以长时间拍摄窗户和光线变化著称。
	•	其影像风格甚至让人联想到**塞尚 (Paul Cézanne) 晚期绘画**，画面空间被压缩，景深与表面几乎融为一体。

然而，与极简主义者不同，**阿克曼将人文主义内容融入形式主义**，并未完全摒弃叙事。

Là-bas
According to director Chantal Akerman, she never planned to make a film in Israel. She was convinced that neutrality does not exist and that her subjectivity would get in her way. She was sure she would only be able to reflect on 'the Israel question' while she was outside the country.
It was only when she taught at the University of Tel Aviv, picked up a camera and 'found' suitable images that she decided to make a film. Akerman spends a brief period on her own in an apartment by the sea in Tel Aviv. She takes the chamber play to its ultimate form: it is almost entirely chamber. She films from the apartment and in her narration she talks about her family, her Jewish identity and her childhood. She wonders whether normal everyday life is possible in this place and whether filming is a realistic option. Akerman does not film here with any intentions defined in advance. She wants to be as open and blank as possible to ensure that things take their own course.
Icarus Films
Before, after, and between the narrative feature films - from Jeanne Dielman
(1975) to La Captive (2000) - that anchor her status as a world-class filmmaker and one of the most indispensable of her post-Godard generation, Chantal Akerman has made some 20 works (films, performances, gallery installations) whose basic form is the letter - sometimes written to her by another, or by her to another, but most often by and to herself. As intimate as diary entries but more formal in the construction of their prose, these letters are read by Akerman in her husky, instantly recognisable voice, on soundtracks that personalise and narrativise the visual images against which they play - images that, because of their repetition, strikingly geometric composition, and the contemplative rhythm by which they're edited, might otherwise verge on abstraction.
Là-bas, which won the Grand Prize at the Marseille International Documentary Festival and was nominated for a César, is both the most fragile and most powerful of these works. It was made in Tel Aviv, mostly inside an apartment Akerman had rented for a month while she was doing a guest teaching gig.
Xavier Carniaux, who produced many of her previous documentaries, encouraged her to use the opportunity to make a film about Israel, but Akerman, the daughter of Holocaust survivors, resisted. She found the situation too fraught, her own relationship to it too personal. Nevertheless, she brought a small DV camera with her, and one day she picked it up and composed a shot, pointing the lens toward the two large windows and glass balcony door that fill one wall of the apartment and which overlook the windows, balconies, and terrace of the building opposite. The windows are covered with matchstick blinds that are not quite flush with them, so that sometimes, from certain angles, the exterior is visible through the glass alone.
Similarly, the door is sometimes open, sometimes shut, and sometimes covered by a second louvered door.
The film consists of a series of fixed, extended shots, some lasting severa minutes, of these partial and partially obscured openings onto the outside world. (There is, notably, no reverse angle onto the interior of the apartment.) Through these veiled windows, we observe people going about their daily routines, most strikingly an elderly man who constantly rearranges his plants and paces his terrace like a caged lion.
But as the film progresses we may find ourselves more aware of the changes in the light - angle, intensity, the way in which it's blocked by the opposite buildings and filtered by the semi-transparent blinds. The blinds, with their strong verticals (the cords that tie the slants) and narrow, delicate horizontals, structure the image and restructure it every time the camera changes position.
One of the strongest influences on Akerman's work is minimalist avant-garde filmmaking. It's impossible to look at Là-bas and not recall the wall of windows and the changing light in Michael Snow's Wavelength, a connection reinforced by the qualities of the image produced by Akerman's low-end DV camera, particularly its flattening of the space so that the view from the window has the look of a late Cézanne where depth and surface become one (what Snow referred to as the balancing of illusion and fact' in Cézanne and which he sought after in his own work). Akerman takes the aesthetic strategies of the minimalists and marries them to the humanist content that they suppressed.
Thus, when we look at the changing light in the room or we scan the horizon line on the nearby beach where Akerman occasionally sets up the camera, we also hear the voice of this invisible protagonist as she mulls over bits of family history or worries over her relationship with her landlady and how she will replace the things she's broken or eaten in the apartment. The quest for replacements will take her out onto the street, which we come to understand as something of a heroic act. Akerman leaves it to us to tie the clues and strands of her thought together - how, for example, her ruminations on the suicide of her aunt and of Amos Oz's mother connect to the report of a suicide bombing only a block away. Sometimes the sound of her voice mixes with that of fighter planes streaking across the sky, the most concrete sign that this is a war zone.
Amy Taubin, Film Comment, May-June 2007
