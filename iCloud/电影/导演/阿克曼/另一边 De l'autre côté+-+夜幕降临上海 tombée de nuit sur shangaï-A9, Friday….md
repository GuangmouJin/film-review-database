阿克曼：感知的冒险

《彼岸》（**De l’autre côté）**

**尚塔尔·阿克曼谈《彼岸》**

通常，我不会刻意拍摄与新闻报道相关的电影。即使新闻有时成为项目的一部分（例如《南方》（Sud）），那往往也是事后的决定。然而，这一次，有件事始终**萦绕在我心头**。

我在报纸上读到一些关于**亚利桑那州牧场主**的简短报道——他们决定**自行执法**，而且**完全不受惩罚**。他们**持枪巡逻**，有时甚至手持**马格南手枪**，在自家牧场上**追捕非法移民**。这些移民是从**荒漠的山脉**跌落到他们的土地上的。他们被**围捕**、**扣押**，直到**边境巡逻队（The Border Patrol）**前来，将他们登记造册后遣返回墨西哥。

有时，这些牧场主会在他们的土地上**扣押超过 400 人**，像**战俘**一样对待他们。而最令我不安的是**他们用来正当化这些战争行为的语言和理由**。他们不断地重复：
	•	**“我们受够了这些移民带来的污秽。”**
	•	他们把移民称作**“蛮族的洪流”**（hordes of savages）。
	•	他们在宁静美丽的公路旁竖起告示牌，黑底白字写着：**“停止入侵”**、**“我们的环境正被入侵玷污”**，甚至更骇人的标语。

**这些词——污秽（dirt）、蛮族（horde）、入侵（invasion）——始终萦绕在我脑海里**。

我无法忽视这些措辞，它们让我想到：
	•	**“我们害怕。”**
	•	**害怕入侵，害怕秩序被打破，害怕污染，害怕失去控制。**
	•	**害怕“蛮族”带来的混乱和恐怖。**

他们**害怕贫困，害怕被感染**。他们害怕这些**肮脏的、腐坏的、污染性的东西**会**侵蚀他们的土地、血统，甚至他们自认为的身体完整性**。

这种恐惧在美国**尤为强烈**，或许因为**这是一个没有悠久历史可供反思的社会**。这是一个**由清教徒建立的原始社会**，他们怀抱着**深刻的种族歧视道德观**，并执着于**纯净的起源神话**。而在亚利桑那州这一地区，这种恐惧更是根深蒂固，因为这里本身就是一个多种族混合的地方。

他们担心这种“入侵”**会摧毁他们的生活方式**。而在“蛮族的洪流”那一侧，人们却在**计算死亡人数**。

⸻

在《东方》（D’Est）中，我拍摄了一部**旅程电影**，一部**挽歌电影**，没有叙事结构，但历史的曲折让每个人都可以建构自己的叙事。之后，我拍摄了《南方》（Sud）作为它的回声。

而现在，《彼岸》（De l’autre côté）成为这个**三部曲**的第三部作品。它不仅回应了《南方》，还试图**更深入地挖掘其核心素材**。与前两部电影不同，这一次，我为它**留出了一定的叙事空间**。

这是一种**当代叙事**，但围绕的却是一个**亘古不变的主题**——我们必须一次又一次地回到它。

⸻

如何拍摄这个故事？

我不禁思考，**如何传达我所看到的东西**？我也感到**担忧**。

我**绝不想带着既定计划或剧本前往那里**。

每当我开始拍摄一部纪录片，我既**着迷**，又**恐惧**。因为我是在**发现**这部纪录片，而不是预先定义它。

我让自己**几乎是盲目地**被引导，成为一块**“敏感的海绵”**，吸收一切，**让电影浮现或显露出来**。

让我害怕的并不是**思考**，而是把纪录片**锁定在一个预先设定的框架里**。我试图**抵达“犯罪现场”**，让电影的素材占据我，而不是相反。

当然，这是几乎不可能的。我们总是**带着自身的知识和经验去拍摄**。然而，在这部电影中，**保持开放的“悬浮式聆听”**比以往任何时候都更加重要。

我们脑海中早已充满了**关于美国和墨西哥的刻板印象**——
	•	**美国是“黄金国度”（El Dorado），墨西哥是贫穷的国度。**
	•	**墨西哥有古老文化，但也有腐败和残酷，而美国代表着死亡、文化侵蚀，以及一种“原始的现代性”。**

但这一切并没有那么简单。**去到那里，我们必须忘记这些固有印象。**

⸻

如何避免道德优越感？

在纪录片创作中，我希望进入**不评判的状态**。

我不希望成为那个**自以为更有道德、更懂政治、更聪明**的人。

但这也很困难。因为当你看到移民**竭尽全力求生**，你很容易**站在他们那一边**，而把另一边的人**污名化**。

在电影中，我最终**站在了墨西哥人那一边**。但或许，在某些时刻，我对**美国人**的描绘显得极为负面。

然而，在纪录片中，**我的兴趣不是选边站队**，而是**让一切复杂性得以呈现**，并让它**在观众心中产生共鸣**。

**因为，所有这些问题，其实早已深埋在每一个人的内心深处——包括观众自己。**

 AKERMAN: ADVENTURES IN PERCEPTION
De l'autre côté
Chantal Akerman on 'De l'autre côté'
In general, I don't try to make a film about the news I read in the newspapers and even if they are sometimes part of the project (see Sud), it is often a posteriori.
This time, however, there was something that never ceased to haunt me in the few lines I read about the ranchers of Arizona, these ranchers who had decided to make their own law and do so with complete impunity and to hunt, rifle on shoulder, and even magnum in hand, the illegal immigrants who literally fell from the desert mountains onto their property. To hunt them, round them up, and keep them at bay with their weapons until 'The Border Patrol' comes to take care of them and brings them back, after having filed them, behind the Mexican border.
Sometimes, they hold more than 400 people on their ranch, treating them like prisoners of war, except that the immigrants do not know that they are in a war.
But what undoubtedly disturbed me the most in this whole story are the reasons and terms that the ranchers constantly invoke to justify this warlike behavior: they are 'fed up with the dirt that all these immigrants' - they speak of hordes of immigrants, as hordes of savages - leave in the landscape around or in their ranches, they are 'fed up with the dirt' that all these illegals leave on American land.
They talk about filth, hordes and invasion. And they put up signs all along idyllic, seemingly peaceful roads, signs that read in white letters on a black background: 'Stop the invasion' or 'Our environment is being tainted by the invasion', or worse.
These words - dirt, horde and invasion - have never ceased to haunt me, probably because in this: 'We have had enough of this dirt, of this invasion,' I could not help but hear, 'We are afraid'. Fear of the invasion of disorder and defilement. Fear of disorder which would corrupt order, our order. And fear that this disorder, this defilement would bring the reign of the irrational, the reign of terror with its cohort of irrational and deadly fears.
Fear of others, fear of their poverty and possible contagion.
No doubt afraid that this dirt, this rot, this defilement would attack something of the idea of archaic integrity, of integrity of the body, of the earth and of the blood. And perhaps even more in America than elsewhere, in a society without a distant past to reflect on it.
A fundamentally primitive society invented by Puritans with a morality of deep racial discrimination and a Puritan obsession with an origin. And perhaps even more so in this corner of Arizona so deeply mixed by its very origin.
Fear that it will destroy their way of life.
While on the other side, on the side of the horde of invaders, they were counting their dead.
After D'Est, a travel film, an elegy film, where there was no room for a narrative, even if through the twists and turns of History everyone could construct one, there was Sud as an echo. And now, there is De l'autre côté, the third part of a sort of triptych, which not only echoes Sud, but attempts to dig deeper into its very material and which undoubtedly also leaves a certain room for narration.  A contemporary narration this time, but around a situation as old as time, and to which we must return again and again.
And obviously, I wondered how to convey what I had seen and probably understood a little of what was happening there
And obviously, I was worried. I especially didn't want to go there with a plan, a scenario.
What fascinates and frightens me at the same time, when I set out to make a documentary, is discovering this documentary, discovering it while doing it.
I let myself be led, I would say almost blindly, and I become a sort of 'sensitive sponge', from which the film would float or reveal itself after a long time.
What scares me is not thinking but rather enclosing a documentary in something already 'pre-prepared' when what I'm trying to do is arrive at the scene of the crime' and let it be the very material of the documentary that comes to occupy me and not the opposite.
It's almost impossible of course, and you always arrive somewhere with everything you have, and everything that informs your being.
And perhaps for this documentary, this floating listening was even more necessary than usual because we already have so many images in our heads, so many icons, one can almost say, of a certain American world. And also, from a certain Mexican world.
And of course, the challenge here was to go beyond or shift these surface icons to locate something that we could really see and feel as if it were the first time.
And for this, among other things, it was a question of avoiding any binary system like: here is Mexico, with its poor and here is America, their El Dorado; here is Mexico with an old culture which also has its cruelty, its corruption, but which is part of life - and here is America where death, acculturation, and what is primitively modern lurks.
All of this exists, however (but it's much more complicated than that), and you have to forget it when you go there.
Find a kind of state where you don't judge. Where we would not be the one who arrives there knowing better, having better morals, or a better political point of view. And that too is difficult. Because of course, we would tend to put ourselves on the 'good side', on the side of those who try everything to survive and to stigmatize others. And if in the film, I ultimately find myself on the side of the Mexicans, and perhaps sometimes I give a terrible image of the Americans,
And in any case, what interests me in a documentary is not to take sides, but for something to be expressed in all its complexity, something which should then resonate with what is there, most often buried, but very much there, in the other, the spectator.
