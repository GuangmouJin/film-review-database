# 多重伤残


对比《高中》和《福利》更贴近于直接电影，

#BFI 长评-被看见与凝视的暴力。纪录片聚焦于一群“多重残障”人士在机构化照护体系中的日常生活，影片并非传统的纪录片，**没有访谈，没有旁白，事件自己发生，**不做戏剧化设计，镜头冷静的记录这些身体与社会结构交织的“被困状态”**，而观众**更像跟着镜头走过了无数“未曾出现”过的日与夜，但另一层面又在思考，用摄影机观看残障身体，会不会形成另一种凝视的暴力？看的时候看到于伊耶和斯特劳布的那部《太早，太迟》中的核心，死亡是风景中的空位，而当我们“看到了”这道“空位”时，我们其实已经参与了对现实的一次重新编排，这种观看本身暗含着权力——我们既在凝视，又无法逃离自己的凝视位置，而残障身体在镜头下的暴露，也会反过来提醒我们这所谓“现实”是被谁记录，以谁的视角被记录的？那道空位是风景的，还是我们的。

整部作品的空间设计上其实跟《福利》还是蛮类似的，建筑成为了封闭空间，对比与《福利》中的争吵，本片中角色的触摸变成了一种熟悉空间的手段，在视觉听觉都被剥夺的情况下，触摸反而成了唯一能够建立“世界关系”的方式，而视听障碍者间的交流没有被翻译呈现的是一种对于集中封存的讽刺，就如同我们看不懂，他们也感受不到我们一样，只是标准的不同**。**


视觉上，基本为手持拍摄，**极少的使用变焦来操纵情绪，也是我感觉对比另两部我看过的他的作品更接近于直接电影的原因**，多使用**剪辑呈现“现场捕捉”的现实，片中也出现了摄影师和收音师的画面，**感觉也是让现实意识到“现实”的间离。

听觉上，我觉得很大程度除了呈现机构的情况跟多的是那种呢喃和破含糊不清的发音呈现“主体性被压抑”的状态。

折页是91年怀斯曼访谈内容，

**Q：你是如何安排进入一个机构拍摄的？**
A：我的说明非常直接，我想拍一部电影，我不知道它最终会变成什么样，但我想在那儿待一个月或六周，在此期间拍摄六十到八十小时的素材。我不知道影片的主题会是什么，电影首先会在公共电视台播出，剪辑决策由我控制，不会有任何摆拍，设备基本是手持的，不使用额外灯光——除非没有可用光就无法拍摄。

**Q：你会不会感觉被拍摄的人在为镜头表演？
A**：没有真正的方法去知道，我的经验是摄影机或录音机的存在不会造成任何区别。我们大多数人其实没有那种能力，会在被拍照或录音时突然改变自己的行为。如果有的话，好莱坞和百老汇的表演水平会比现在好得多。如果人们真的“演”，他们也只会以他们认为适当的方式去演，而这正是你想要捕捉的，你想要他们以一种他们认为在那个情境中“应该如此”的方式行动，我认为纪录片电影的伟大主题就是“正常性”，或者说，被当作“正常行为”的东西。任何必须一直和很多人打交道的人——无论你是电影工作者，医生，律师，保险推销员，教师还是警察——都会发展出一个相当灵敏的“胡扯雷达”，用来判断自己什么时候被糊弄了，当你在拍这类电影时，如果你觉得有人在为镜头“表演”，你就停止拍摄，如果你直到进入剪辑室才意识到，那就不用那段素材，像任何职业一样，你也可能犯错，但这是一个你会变得非常敏感的问题。拍摄期间会有一种相互“评估”。一方面，我在试着弄清楚要拍什么，另一方面，片中人物或者潜在的片中人物则会问自己某种形式的问题：我们能信任这个家伙吗？所以如何呈现自己，无论是呈现给机构的管理者还是呈现给可能的拍摄对象都是极其重要的。从伦理角度和策略角度来说，最好的做法就是坦诚，他们的“胡扯雷达”跟你的一样灵。

**Q：你会不会担心侵犯某人的隐私？**
A：嗯，我认为有必要区分一个人对隐私的个人伦理观点以及法律对隐私的规定。如果人们不给我使用许可我就不会使用那段素材。有时我在拍摄前获得许可，有时是在拍完之后。如果他们说不，我就不用。但一旦我获得了许可，如果我觉得那段适合放进电影，我就会使用它。你不可能一边怀着极其精细的个人敏感性对待隐私问题，又一边拍成一部电影。隐私完全是主观的，对一个人来说，仅仅是拍到他坐在医院椅子上的镜头，都可能被视为侵犯隐私，而另一个人在镜头前呕吐却完全不介意。

**Q：你的剪辑过程是什么？**
A：我想我剪辑的方式和别人没什么不同，我坐在那里，安全带上有个计时锁，我靠静脉注射进食，然后只有在电影完成时才起身。所需做的就是不停地坐在那里一遍又一遍看素材，开始时我大约有五十到八十小时的素材，虽然你需要好的画面和好的声音，但真正决定影片成败的是剪辑中你所做的选择。我会回看所有素材，把它分类，拆解。我会先挑一个暂定的开头和结尾，只是为了让我对影片可能如何开场和收尾有个感觉，然后通常，我就从一个我喜欢的并让我感到有趣的段落开始。一旦开始，剪辑就变得强迫性了。剪辑分成两大类：“内部剪辑”和“外部剪辑”。内部剪辑是把一段原始素材压缩成某种可用的形式，例如一段真实发生的情节可能有九十分钟，我可能拍到了其中八十五分钟，经过剪辑我最后可能得到一个六分钟的段落，那六分钟可能是连续的，也可能是从不同部分拼出来的，但最终剪成看起来就像实时发生的一样。外部剪辑则是把这些已经内部剪辑完成的段落互相排列组合，形成最终的影片结构，通常要经过几个月我才会进入外部剪辑，随着时间推移，我会拼出一些彼此相关的“群岛”式段落。大概六至七个月后，我会组装出整部电影的结构，那时我已经非常熟悉素材，也已经思考了很久。当我有了影片的第一次组装版本，过程就开始加速了，这对我来说也是最有趣的部分，当我能掌控所有东西时，我就能玩起来，我能非常快速地做出改变——调整顺序，塑形，打造戏剧结构。这个过程非常令人兴奋，因为我开始从那一大坨素材中看到电影逐渐浮现。

**Q：对于你来说，剪辑是否比拍摄更有趣？**
A：都很有趣，我想我最喜欢的是剪辑，但整体来说都是一种运动，纪录片拍摄是一项运动，因为你需要保持体能，你得到处跑，你去不同的地方，这很有趣。

Frederick Wiseman on 'Multi-Handicapped'
How do you arrange for access to an institution?
My presentation is very straightforward: I want to make a movie; I don't know how it's going to come out. I want to hang around for a month or six weeks and, in the course of that time, shoot 60 to 80 hours of film. I don't know what the themes of the film are going to be. The film will be shown first on public television; I have editorial control; I'll be happy to show it to you if you want to see it before broadcast, but with the understanding that you have absolutely no control over it. Nothing will be staged, the equipment will be, by and large, hand-held. No extra lights will be used - only when it is not possible to shoot with available light, and that's it. Do you have any questions? When I get permission in a meeting, I confirm it in a letter. It's not a contract in a sense that it's full of legal language. I usually ask the person to whom the letter is addressed to send me back a signed copy. That gives me a record of what I said and what they agree to. This informal arrangement is much better, from my point of view, than having lawyers do it. Many people get very nervous when they have to deal with lawyers. This is just as effective.
Do you ever sense your subjects are performing for the camera?
Well, there's no real way you can know. My experience is that the presence of the camera or the tape recorder doesn't make any difference. Most of us don't have the capacity to suddenly alter our behaviour because our picture's being taken and our voice recorded. If we did, the level of acting in Hollywood and on Broadway would be much better than it is. People will, if they act at all, act in ways they think are appropriate. And that's what you want to get. You want people acting in a way that they think is the right way to behave in that situation. I think the great subject of documentary filmmaking is normalcy or what passes for normal behaviour.
Anybody who has to deal with a lot of people all the time, whether you're a filmmaker, doctor, lawyer, insurance salesman, teacher or policeman, develops a fairly sensitive bullshit meter, to figure out when you're being conned.
Similarly, when you're making one of these movies, if you think somebody's putting it on for the camera, you stop shooting. If you don't realise it until you get to the editing room, you don't use the sequence. Like any other profession, you can make a mistake. But it's an issue you get very sensitive to.
There's a kind of a mutual sizing-up during the shooting. On the one hand, I'm trying to figure out what to shoot; on the other hand, the participants, or the potential participants, in the film are asking themselves some form of the question, Can we trust this guy? And so the way you present yourself both to the institutional authorities and to the possible subjects of the film is extremely important. The best thing to do, both from an ethical point of view and also from a strategic point of view - it's one of those nice situations where there's a coincidence or coinciding of interests - is to be straight. Their bullshit meters are just as good as yours.
Do you ever worry about infringing on someone's privacy?
Well, I think it is necessary to make a distinction between one's own personal ethical views about privacy and the law about privacy. I don't use a sequence if

people don't give me permission. Sometimes I get it before the sequence is shot; and sometimes I get it after it's shot. If they say no, I don't use it. But once I have permission, if I think the sequence fits in the film, I'll use it. You can't have this exquisite personal sensitivity about the privacy issue and make the movie. It's totally subjective: for one person, simply a shot of him sitting on a chair in a hospital may be considered an invasion of privacy, and another person who's throwing up on camera may not mind at all.
What is your editing process?
I don't suppose I edit any different from anybody else: I sit there with a seat belt that has a time-lock on it, I get fed intravenously, and I get up when the movie's over. What's involved is sitting there and looking at the material over and over again. I have anywhere from 50 to 80 hours of material when I start. While you need good pictures and good sound, you make or break the film in the editing by the choices that you make.
I review all the material, catalogue it and break it down. I choose a provisional beginning and ending just so I have a sense of how I might open and close the film. And then usually, I just start with a sequence that I like, that amused me.
Once I get started, the editing becomes compulsive. It breaks down into two general categories: internal editing and external editing. Internal editing boils down a sequence into some kind of usable form. For example, a sequence in real time might be 90 minutes. Of that 90 minutes, I might have 85 minutes on film. As a result of editing, I end up with a six-minute sequence. It could be consecutive six minutes or it might be six minutes assembled from all over the lot but edited to appear as if it took place the way you're watching it. External editing is the relationship of the individually edited sequences to each other so as to provide the structure of the final film. It is months before I get to the external editing. As time passes, I assemble little archipelagos of sequences that are related to each other.
After six or seven months, I'll assemble the structure of the final film. At that point, I know the material very well, and I've been thinking about it for a long time. When I get the first assembly of the film, the process begins to accelerate. For me, this is the most interesting part. When I have everything under control, I can play with it. I can make the changes very quickly - change the order, shape it, work out a dramatic structure. The process is very exciting because I begin to see the film emerge from that great glop of material.
Is editing more fun for you than the actual shooting of the film?
It's all fun. I suppose I like the editing best, but it's all sport. Documentary filmmaking is a sport. Because you have to be in shape, you have to run around, you go to different locations, and it's fun.
Frederick Wiseman interviewed in American Film, May 1991
