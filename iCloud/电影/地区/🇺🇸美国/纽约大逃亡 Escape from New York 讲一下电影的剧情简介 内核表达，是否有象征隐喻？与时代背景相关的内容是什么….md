**什么是“邪典电影”？**
邪典电影是一种拥有狂热追随者、但并非人人都喜欢的电影。仅仅因为一部电影是邪典电影，并不自动意味着它是高质量的。有些邪典电影非常糟糕，而另一些则非常、非常好。有些在票房上赚得盆满钵满，另一些则一分没赚。有些被视为高质量作品，另一些则是纯粹的剥削片。

从 1988 年到 2000 年，**Moviedrome** 由 Alex Cox 主持，后来由 Mark Cousins 接手。在这段时间里，播放了 200 多部电影，几代影迷和电影人都从片单和每次放映前充满机智与见解的导语中获得启发。Moviedrome 是通向奇特与精彩电影世界的入口。本次为期两个月的放映季，将展映其中一些最值得关注的片目，并在条件允许的情况下，附上当年电视播出时的原始导语。

**Alex Cox：**
这是一部约翰·卡朋特较为令人失望的作品。卡朋特是一位拍过一系列恐怖与科幻片的导演，其中最著名、最成功的要数《月光光心慌慌》（Halloween）。他的第一部电影，从某种意义上说也是他最好的作品，是一部名为《黑星球》（Dark Star）的科幻喜剧，讲的是“太空冲浪者”的故事。《黑星球》的编剧是丹·欧班农（Dan O’Bannon），后来写了《异形》（Alien）。尽管《异形》的预算大得多，并有雷德利·斯科特独特的“巧克力盒”式摄影加持，但本质上还是同一个故事（太空船上有疯狂外星人，一个个杀掉船员），只是去掉了卡朋特原作那种超低预算下非常搞笑的幽默感。

卡朋特是个水准参差不齐的导演。有时他真的能拍出好东西，比如《十三号警署》（Assault on Precinct 13）、他的《怪形》翻拍，或者被严重低估的《极度空间》（They Live）。《极度空间》是少数以无家可归者为英雄的美国电影之一，在第一小时里，它几乎是催眠般地揭露出“雅痞”和索霍区的录像、广告圈人士其实是外星人——他们接管了地球，忙着让剩下的人失业。《极度空间》的问题出在结尾，像许多现代科幻片一样，跌入了沉闷的走廊追逐战和高科技枪战的泥潭——《全面回忆》（Total Recall）和《终结者2》（Terminator 2）也患有类似的病。

《纽约大逃亡》本该是一部精彩绝伦的电影。它的设定是：五年后的未来，社会秩序彻底崩溃，纽约被改造成一个由巨墙包围的超级监狱。1981 年，这部片的预算是 700 万美元，是卡朋特当时最昂贵的电影。片中有一些出色的演员，包括李·范·克里夫（Lee Van Cleef）、哈里·迪恩·斯坦顿（Harry Dean Stanton）、约翰·迪尔（John Diehl），以及库尔特·拉塞尔（Kurt Russell）那段非常搞笑的克林特·伊斯特伍德模仿秀。拥有绝佳的设定、可观的资金和优秀的演员阵容，为什么《纽约大逃亡》还是没拍好？

我认为部分原因在于拙劣的特效，这些特效是在洛杉矶一处木材场里的罗杰·科尔曼（Roger Corman）廉价工作室完成的。如果你仔细看片尾字幕，会看到一位名叫 Jim Cameron 的人，被列为特效摄影和绘景艺术家。这会是后来成名的詹姆斯·卡梅隆（《终结者》《深渊》）吗？也许是的——如果你还记得《终结者1》里的模型和绘景有多糟的话。不过，更重要的是，这部片显然不是在纽约拍的。为了节省预算，它在圣路易斯和洛杉矶世纪城拍摄，这两个地方看起来完全不像曼哈顿。剧本还有一种懒散和松垮的感觉，从头到尾都没有真正进入状态，除了那些角色名字叫“罗梅罗”和“柯南伯格”之类的内部玩笑。

这只是卡朋特这个偶尔能出奇制胜的导演作品库里的一部小品。影片依旧由导演本人作曲并演奏配乐。

**Alex Cox 的 Moviedrome 原始导语（也刊登在 BBC 1993 年出版的《Moviedrome: The Guide 2》中，感谢 moviedromer.tumblr.com 提供）**

即便牵强甚至纯属荒谬，把曼哈顿设想为一个用高墙封起来的超级监狱，让罪犯们在他们自己扭曲的社会版本里混迹，依然是个颇具观赏性的电影概念。片中字幕说，隔离墙建于 1988 年，影片故事发生在 1997 年。但如果说预言不是卡朋特的强项，那么他的概念、执行和主角设定，还是构成了一个迷人的酸性童话。

卡朋特笔下的废墟曼哈顿，是一个阴暗、肮脏的地狱世界。影片用原始的绘景和模型，构造出真实景象的荒凉版本，这座支离破碎的城市景观被阴郁地赋予了生命。这是一个阴沉、噩梦般的未来图景，有着自己的阶层结构，而且值得注意的是，它比《银翼杀手》（Blade Runner）早至少一年诞生。

在这个达尔文主义的地狱坑里，美国总统（唐纳德·普莱森斯饰）在“空军一号”的逃生舱中坠落，手里紧握着一盘能阻止与苏联爆发世界大战（影片制作于“公开化”之前）的演讲录音带。为了救出他和那盘带子，需要一个极端的自我生存主义者。于是，蛇·普利斯金登场——前士兵，后转行做银行劫匪、社会弃儿，以及超级酷的冷面角色。

拉塞尔饰演的普利斯金堪称精彩。在他后来的演艺生涯被一连串平庸动作片和与歌蒂·韩的糟糕浪漫喜剧毁掉之前，他在这里塑造了超级反英雄（下一步他把这种无懈可击的生存本事用在了《怪形》上），把一种干脆利落的行动力和带点嘲讽的不屑注入到每个动作中（有意思的是，最初的首选演员其实是汤米·李·琼斯）。粗哑而慵懒的嗓音、慢热的风格，直接源于赛尔乔·莱昂内和克林特·伊斯特伍德的传奇“无名氏”角色，并在无数后续的孤胆硬汉英雄（如《疯狂的麦克斯》、 《未来水世界》）身上得到回响。让李·范·克里夫饰演狡诈的霍克，更是进一步强调了这种致敬。

不过，普利斯金比伊斯特伍德那个披斗篷的外来者更专注、更少了些纯粹的雇佣性，因为他脖子里有一颗 24 小时倒计时的爆炸装置——这是一个极其直接有效的戏剧性设定，让影片有了真正的紧迫感。

影片充满一种黑色讽刺，因此它并不像你预想的那样是部纯粹的动作片。拉长的段落让观众细细体会美术指导乔·阿尔维斯粗砺的现实主义，以及卡朋特那套有效但显得过时的“叮叮咚咚”合成器配乐，偶尔才被普利斯金不可阻挡的硬汉气场和相对平庸的枪战打断（暴露出预算限制）。卡朋特在这里创造的是一种末世角色剧兼黑色喜剧。除了普利斯金之外，厄内斯特·博格宁饰演的纽约出租车司机、艾萨克·海耶斯饰演的反派“公爵”、哈里·迪恩·斯坦顿饰演的“脑袋”，以及范·克里夫的奸诈霍克，都像漫画人物一样鲜活。

这是一部诞生在越战后/水门事件后的美国电影，充满了政治不信任和广泛的反体制情绪——主人公谁都不为，但我们依然被引导去欣赏他，哪怕只是因为他的能力。纽约（作为美国的缩影）是一个道德荒原，只有最强、最残酷的人才能生存。对“美国梦”的打击，是卡朋特许多电影中固有的主题。在《纽约大逃亡》之前，导演还拍过另一部讲述社会动荡的优秀影片《十三号警署》。值得注意的是，质量低劣的《逃出洛杉矶》完全没有这种冰冷的犬儒感，因为它出自一个缺乏这种尖刻感——也许也缺乏相关性的——年代。

—— Ian Nathan，《Empire Online》

Alex Cox: This is one of the more disappointing John Carpenter films. Carpenter is the director of a series of horror and science fiction films, the most famous and commercially successful of which was Halloween. His first film, in some ways still his best, was a science-fiction comedy called Dark Star, about surfers in space Dark Star was written by one Dan O'Bannon, who later wrote Alien, which, in spite of a much larger budget and the inimitable chocolate-box photography of Ridley Scott, is essentially the same story (mad alien aboard space ship, picking off the crew) minus the very funny humour of Carpenter's original, super-low-budget film.
Carpenter is a variable director. Sometimes he really pulls it off, as with Assault on Precinct 13, his remake of The Thing, or the highly underrated They Live. They Live is one of the few recent American films to feature a homeless person as the hero, and for the first hour it's a mesmeric revelation of how yuppies and the Soho video and advertising crowd are really aliens who, having taken over the planet, are busy throwing the rest of us out of work. They Live falls down at the end, stumbling as do many modern science-fiction films into a dull welter of chases down corridors and high-tech gunfire - similar malaises afflict Total Recall and Terminator 2.
Escape from New York really should have been a brilliant film. Its premise is that five years from now all order has broken down and New York has been turned into a vast maximum-security prison encircled by a giant wall. Budgeted in 1981 at $7 million, it was Carpenter's most expensive film. It features some splendid actors, including Lee Van Cleef, Harry Dean Stanton, John Diehl and Kurt Russell doing a very funny Eastwood imitation. With a great premise, decent money and a good cast, why does Escape from New York go wrong?
I think it's partially because of the inadequate special effects, which were done at Roger Corman's cheapo studio in a Los Angeles lumber yard. If you study the credits closely, you'll see the name of one Jim Cameron listed as effects photographer and matte artist. Could this be the renowned James Cameron, of Terminator and Abyss fame? Maybe it could, if you recall how bad the model work and mattes were in Terminator 1. More to the point, though, is that the film obviously doesn't take place in New York. For budgetary convenience it was shot in St Louis and Century City, Los Angeles, neither of which resemble Manhattan at all. And there's a certain laziness and sprawling quality to the script, which never really gets going, beyond the level of in-jokes such as subsidiary characters named Romero and Cronenberg.
it is a minor offering from the sometimes extraordinary stable of John Carpenter. It features, as usual, music composed and performed by the director.

With thanks to moviedromer.tumblr.com Far-fetched if not plain daft, still there is something enjoyably cinematic in the idea of Manhattan as a walled-in maximum security prison where the criminals swil around in their own skewed version of society. According to the screen captions the barricade went up in 1988, the events of the film occur in 1997. But if prophecy isn't Carpenter's strong suit, his concept, execution and lead character make for a fascinating acid-fairy tale.
Carpenter's ruined Manhattan is a grim, grungy netherworld. Utilising primitive matte and modelwork to construct desolate versions of the real thing, the fragged cityscape is brought to sullen life. A bleak, nightmarish vision of the future with its own class strata, it's pertinent to note that it was created at least a year before Blade Runner. Into this Darwinist hell hole tumbles the US President (Pleasance) in his Airforce One escape pod, clutching a tape of an address which will stave off an imminent world war with the USSR (this is pre-Glasnost moviemaking). To get him, and the tape, out, the ultimate self-preservationist is required. Enter Snake Plissken, ex-soldier turned bank robber turned social reprobate and ultracool sourpuss.
Russell as Plissken is magnificent. Long before the meaty actor's credibility was undone by a parade of indifferent action roles and bad romcoms with Goldie, he embodied the superantihero (next he applied his infallible resourcefulness to The Thing) instilling a sense of can-do physicality and sneery indifference into everything he did (significantly, Tommy Lee Jones was the preferred choice). The voice raspy and laconic, Plissken's slowburning style stems directly from Sergio Leone and Clint Eastwood's legendary Man with No Name and is echoed in any number of subsequent loner-arsehole-heroes (Mad Max, Waterworld's Mariner et al).
Casting Lee Van Cleef as the duplicitous Hauk simply underlines the reference.
Plissken, though, is more preoccupied, less loosely mercenary than Eastwood's ponchoed outsider. Mainly because he's got an explosive implant in his neck with 24 hours on the clock, a superbly direct dramatic device to give the film a real urgency.
Fuelled by a dark strain of irony, this is far less an action movie than you might expect. Drawn-out sequences, absorbing all of production designer Joe Alves' gritty realism and Carpenter's effective but dated plinky-plonky synth score, are only intermittently punctuated by bursts of Snake's indomitable machismo and fairly mediocre gunplay (revealing budget limitations). Instead, Carpenter has created a kind of apocalyptic character piece-cum-black comedy. And beyond Plissken, Borgnine as NY cabbie Cabbie, Isaac Hayes as badguy Duke, Harry Dean Stanton's Brain and Van Cleef's snidey Hauk make big comic book
This is a product of post-Vietnam/Watergate America, a world of political distrust and widespread anti-establishment fervour - the hero serves no one but himself, yet we're still invited to admire him, simply for his capability if nothing else. New York (a microcosm of America) is a desolate, moral wasteland where only the fittest and cruellest survive. Putting the boot in on the American Dream is a theme inherent in many of Carpenter's movies. The director preceded EFNY with another excellent tale of civil unrest, Assault on Precinct 13. And it's notable that the poor Escape from LA manages none of this cold cynicism, born as it is out of a less caustic - less relevant? - decade.
