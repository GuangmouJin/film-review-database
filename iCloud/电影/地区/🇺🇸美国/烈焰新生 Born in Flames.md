🧩 **总结一句话：**

《Born in Flames》是一部将女性主义、政治激进性、媒介批判与DIY美学融为一体的前卫实验电影，它不仅是对“革命”的质疑，更是一次提出女性如何主导自身历史书写的响亮呼声。

影片设定在美国“解放战争”后十年，民主社会主义未能兑现其社会正义承诺，有色人种女性在“平等者”中仍居末位。影片前半段描绘“女性解放军”的缓慢形成：由海盗电台DJ（由Honey与朋克艺术家Adele Bertei饰演）代表的混血女性主义团体，最终与白人女编辑一起围绕激进分子阿德莱德·诺里斯（Adelaide Norris）被警察拘留致死事件，展开组织抗争。虽然是科幻设定，《烈焰新生》却通过纪录片式的拍摄手法落地其现实性：故事发生在1970年代末的下曼哈顿，一个多样而未经历绅士化的城市空间。



“你希望观众在看作品时理解你在其中做出的观念性决定吗？”——在一次关于理查德·塞拉为2011年回顾展所画素描的访谈中，导演莉兹·博登（Lizzie Borden）这样问他。塞拉原本可以将这个问题反问博登（她曾是他的录像剪辑师）。博登的作品——四部以叙事为主的长片和若干商业电视集——始终在观念艺术的边界上推进。她的作品既具思想性，也极具感官刺激，将“观念”具体化为肉身体验，反之亦然，清晰地源自她首部电影《再编组》（*Re-grouping*, 1976）所描绘的纽约下城艺术场景。

1970年代初，年轻的博登搬到纽约追求视觉艺术事业，并为《Artforum》杂志撰写关于以塞拉为代表的观念艺术界，以及表演艺术家如伊冯娜·雷纳（Yvonne Rainer）与琼·乔纳斯（Joan Jonas）的评论。她早已是个叛逆者——少女时期将原名琳达改为臭名昭著的“杀人犯”博登的姓氏，自称那是“我能做出的最棒的反叛”。她在观念艺术与左翼女权主义的美学实验中受到激进化影响，进而对电影产生兴趣，灵感来源包括戈达尔与庞特科沃等人。这种混合背景孕育出一个即便在1970年代纽约独立电影蓬勃发展的语境中也独树一帜的导演生涯。可以对比的是她的好友及早期合作者凯瑟琳·毕格罗（Kathryn Bigelow），她曾借用博登的车拍摄处女短片《设定》（*The Set-up*, 1978），并在博登的两部作品《再编组》与其最知名的《烈焰新生》（*Born in Flames*, 1983）中客串登场。两人都来自视觉艺术与批判理论教育背景，毕格罗最终成为将女权主义注入好莱坞类型片的代表性导演；而博登的作品，直到近年才因美国电影资料馆（Anthology Film Archives）修复了35mm版本的《烈焰新生》而重新受到国际关注。

《烈焰新生》耗时近五年，以约四万美元预算完成——资金主要来自艺术小额资助与博登本人的收入。影片逐渐成为邪典经典。博登回忆道，这部片是用反转片拍摄的：“我会去看胶片，小心别刮伤，然后剪掉大部分，只保存我想保留的部分再复制。”早在1985年，特蕾莎·德·劳雷蒂斯（Teresa de Lauretis）便以此片为灵感，提出“女性凝视”理论，认为博登的影片打破了“白人女性电影中黑人女性的不可见性……或主流电影中对女同性恋的隐匿”。她认为，《烈焰新生》不仅政治上具有革命性，在美学上同样如此，是一种从未真正成型的“无浪潮（no-wave）”游击女权电影的引发者。

博登的导演圈子里不乏其他创作者：爱尔兰导演帕特·墨菲（Pat Murphy）、编剧贝基·约翰斯顿（Becky Johnston，数年后为王子编剧《樱桃月下》）都出现在片中，和毕格罗一同饰演社会主义青年报的编辑。

影片设定在美国“解放战争”后十年，民主社会主义未能兑现其社会正义承诺，有色人种女性在“平等者”中仍居末位。影片前半段描绘“女性解放军”的缓慢形成：由海盗电台DJ（由Honey与朋克艺术家Adele Bertei饰演）代表的混血女性主义团体，最终与白人女编辑一起围绕激进分子阿德莱德·诺里斯（Adelaide Norris）被警察拘留致死事件，展开组织抗争。虽然是科幻设定，《烈焰新生》却通过纪录片式的拍摄手法落地其现实性：故事发生在1970年代末的下曼哈顿，一个多样而未经历绅士化的城市空间。

影片原声也抓住了那个时代的节奏：DJ的节拍与怒吼回荡全城，预示着日后斯派克·李电影《为所应为》（1989）中“Mister Señor Love Daddy”角色的节奏感；片名则源于主题曲《Born in Flames》，由60年代lo-fi原始朋克乐队Red Krayola创作（博登原想以莫妮克·维蒂格1969年的小说《女游击队员》为片名）。

在对纽约下城艺术场景的研究《（运动的）影像世代》（*The (Moving) Pictures Generation*, 2012）中，Vera Dika 将博登视作叙事实践转向与独立电影更广泛发行的关键人物。她强调其对主流与先锋电影实践的双重颠覆，重点落在“影像中的身体，其姿态与情感，在越界性别与性取向的状态中呈现”。她的研究和Celine Danhier 2010年纪录片《空白之城》（*Blank City*，含博登访谈）共同见证了对这一创造性时期的复兴——从越战后的虚无主义出发，经由激进政治与新技术展开，最终却以绅士化收场。雷纳与舞蹈编导特丽莎·布朗（Trisha Brown）的作品近年来在伦敦新一轮紧缩时代中被重新上演；博登即兴而贴近日常的美学，与二人一脉相承。

《烈焰新生》曾在加拿大被审查；部分和平主义女权人士批评其角色以武力对抗父权制；而纽约电影学会（Society for Cinema Studies）则称其“政治幼稚”。她的另一部叙事长片《打工女孩》（*Working Girls*, 1986），以性工作为题材、语调冷峻诙谐，也遭安大略审查局删剪，并卷入1980年代中期的“坏女孩艺术战争”（bad girl art wars）——这场风波中，包括阿比盖尔·查尔德在内的导演因其作品中对性欲的描绘被各大电影节拒之门外，符合当时反色情女权主义的主流倾向。《打工女孩》的演员皆为无名之辈，主角是一位女同性恋者，影片采用客观、纪实语言讲述妓院内部生活，风格完全不同于迈克·尼科尔斯1988年的《上班女郎》（*Working Girl*）。博登花了六个月跨越阶层地采访性工作者，了解她们的劳动条件与行业认知。此举部分回应了加拿大纪录片《不是一场爱情故事》（*Not a Love Story*, 1982，Bonnie Sherr Klein导演），该片在美国女权主义者如凯瑟琳·麦金农（Catherine MacKinnon）中引发强烈反色情讨论。

博登在《打工女孩》与《爱之罪》（*Love Crimes*, 1991）中对性议题的坦率、无畏且往往幽默的处理，使她的职业生涯一度转入电视行业，早于苏珊·赛德尔曼与玛莎·库里奇等纽约女导演执导《欲望都市》之前。她曾为《花花公子》旗下的软色情系列《Inside Out》执导两个单元，并向辛西娅·卢西亚表示，这样的工作让她重拾70年代“狂野的性表达”理念。随后，她为全女性单元电影《欲望旅社》（*Erotique*, 1994）编写剧本，与新德国电影激进导演莫妮卡·特罗伊特（Monika Treut）及香港新浪潮导演罗卓瑶共同参与创作。

随着性工作去罪化话题的回归，包括国际特赦组织对该政策的支持，《打工女孩》正逐步受到重视。与此同时，《烈焰新生》依然切中当下。在一次展映活动上，博登谈到：“这些议题从未远去。经济问题、桑德拉·布兰德（2015年死于警方拘留中）、黑人男性被谋杀、女性议题、性别议题……在好莱坞（博登现居之地）仅一英里外拍摄的《橘色》（*Tangerine*）中，跨性别者遭遇袭击的事件几乎每几个月就会发生一次……数十年过去了，我们比以往更需要奋起反抗。”

正当《烈焰新生》如凤凰般重生，博登那跨越领域、独一无二的作品体系，为观众提供了一次**革命性的再编组**机会：回到下城，重新将观念与身体、理念与现实结合。

——索·梅耶，《视与听》杂志，2016年10月刊


Born in Flames
*Do you expect people who see the piece to understand the conceptual decisions that have gone into it?' the filmmaker Lizzie Borden asked the artist Richard Serra in an interview about his drawings for a 2011 retrospective. Serra could have returned the question to Borden (his former video editor). Her oeuvre, four predominantly narrative features and a handful of commercial TV episodes, pushes at the limits of the conceptual. Simultaneously intellectually and sensorially exhilarating, Borden's work puts the conceptual into the corporeal and vice versa, clearly emerging from the downtown New York scene that she charted in her first film Re-grouping (1976).
Having moved to New York to pursue visual arts in the early 197Os, the young Borden wrote for Artforum about the conceptual art scene around Serra and about performance artists such as Yvonne Rainer and Joan Jonas. Already a rebel - her teenage decision to change her birth name Linda to that of the notorious supposed murderer was 'the best rebellion I could make' - she found herself radicalised by this aesthetic experimentation and by leftist feminism; she channelled that admixture into a curiosity about film, inspired by the work of Jean-Luc Godard and Gillo Pontecorvo. What resulted was a filmmaking career unique even in the fluid zone of 1970s New York independent cinema. One marker is a comparison with her friend and early collaborator Kathryn Bigelow, who borrowed Borden's car to shoot her first short The Set-up (1978) between her cameo appearances in Re-grouping and Borden's second, and best-known, feature Born in Flames (1983). Bigelow, who emerged from a similar educational background in visual art and cntical theory, went on to become the premier feminist filmmaker reimagining Hollywood genre movies; by contrast, Borden has only recently seen interest in her work revived on an international scale with Anthology Film Archive's restoration of a 35mm print of Born in Flames.
Shot over nearly five years for around $40,000 - scraped together from small artists' grants and Borden's own income - the film became a cult classic.
Borden said it was shot 'on reversal. I would look at it, try not to scratch it, and edit it down and throw most of it away and then just make duplicates of what I wanted to save.' As early as 1985 Teresa de Lauretis took it as inspiration for her argument for the emergence of a 'female gaze', noting that Borden's was a rare film that corrected 'the invisibility of black women in white women's films... or of lesbianism in mainstream cinema. Arguing for its revolutionary aesthetic as well as its politics, de Lauretis perceives Born in Flames as the instigator of a no-wave, guerrilla feminist cinema that was never quite realised. Bigelow was not the only filmmaker in Borden's circle: the Irish director Pat Murphy and the screenwriter Becky Johnston (who wrote Under the Cherry Moon for Prince a few years later) both appear in Born in Flames as co-editors with Bigelow of the Young Socialist newspaper.
The film opens ten years after a social democratic War of Liberation in the USA, with the promise of social justice unfulfilled and women of colour finding themselves last among supposed equals. The first third of the film focuses on the slow formation of a Women's Liberation Army: mixed-race feminist groups represented by pirate DJs (played by Honey and the punk artist Adele Bertei) and, eventually, the white newspaper editors come together around the murder in police custody of the militant Adelaide Norris. The science fiction of Born in Flames is grounded by the film's documentary aspect: its world is the lower Manhattan of the late 1970s, diversely populated and ungentrified.
The soundtrack also captures its moment: the way the DJs' beats and verbal blasts move out across the city prefigures the rhythmic presence of Mister Señor Love Daddy in Spike Lee's Do the Right Thing (1989); the film takes its name from its theme song, by the 60s lo-fi proto-punk band Red Krayola Borden initially intended to call it Les Guérillères after Monique Wittigs novel of 1969).
In her study of the downtown New York art scene, The (Moving) Pictures Generation (2012), Vera Dika situates Borden as part of a shift toward narrative practices, and concomitant wider distribution, in downtown New York film, destabilising "both established avant-garde and mainstream film practices through its focus on 'the cinematic body, its poses and affects, now seen in transgressive states, and across the boundaries of sexuality and gender.' Dika's book and Celine Danhier's 2010 documentary about the scene, Blank City, which includes an interview with Borden, are testimony to the revival of interest in an extraordinarily generative moment, which echoes our own - emerging from post-Vietnam nihilism through radical political formations and new technological possibilities that ended in gentrification. The work of Rainer and the choreographer and dancer Trisha Brown has been revived in London in this new era of austerity; Borden's improvisatory, everyday aesthetic is of a piece with theirs.
Born in Flames was censored in Canada, criticised by feminists in the peace movement for showing its characters taking up arms against patriarchy, and described as 'politically naïve' at the Society for Cinema Studies in New York.
Likewise, Working Girls (1986), a funny, downbeat narrative feature about sex work, met the scissorhands of the Ontario Censor Board and became associated with the 'bad girl" art wars of the mid-1980s, which saw filmmakers such as Abigail Child excluded from festivals, in line with the era's anti-pornography feminism, because of their depiction of sexuality. With its cast of unknowns, its lesbian protagonist, and its use of an objective, vérité cinematic language within the close confines of the brothel, Working Girls was definitely not Working Girl (Mike Nichols, 1988). Borden spent six months interviewing sex workers across class boundaries about their working conditions and perceptions of the industry. She was responding in part to the Canadian documentary Not a Love Story (Bonnie Sherr Klein, 1982), which played a leading role in the anti-pornography stance of American feminists such as Catherine MacKinnon.
Borden's frank, fearless and often funny approach to sexuality in Working Girls and Love Crimes (1991) led to an unusual career in television, long before downtown New York contemporaries such as Susan Seidelman and Martha Coolidge were directing Sex & the City. She directed two segments of Playboy's softcore video anthology Inside Out, telling Cynthia Lucia that working for Playboy allowed her to return to the 1970s idea of 'wild sexual expression'. She then wrote the screenplay for the all-female portmanteau film Erotique (1994), which brought together an international quartet of fimmakers, including New German Cinema provocatrice Monika Treut and Hong Kong Second Wave director Clara Law. With a resurgence of debate around sex work, including Amnesty International's endorsement of decriminalisation, Working Girls is due for a revival. Born in Flames, too, remains relevant; talking to Alison Kozberg about a screening earlier this year as part of the Walker Art Gallery's exhibition 'Downtown New York: 1970s Art and Film', Borden noted that the issues the film addresses 'haven't gone away. Economic issues, Sandra Bland [who died in police custody in 2015), the murders of black men, woren's issues, gender issues... in Hollywood, a mile away (from where Borden lives), where Tangerine was filmed, a transgender assault happens every couple of months... It's been decades and we need to fight harder than ever.' As Born in Flames rises phoenix-like, Borden's uniquely wide-ranging oeuvre offers viewers the opportunity for a revolutionary Re-grouping: to go downtown and bring together the conceptual and corporeal once more.
So Mayer, Sight and Sound, October 2016
