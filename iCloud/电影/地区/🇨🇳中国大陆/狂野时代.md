**作者：**<u>**Arjun Sajip**</u>

这里有一个“魔鬼交易”：放弃做梦的能力，你就可以永生。在<u>Resurrection</u>（毕赣七年来的首部长片，也是他迄今最令人目眩的作品）中，除了少数“沉迷幻觉者”（deliriants）之外，所有人都接受了这笔交易，而这些少数人则坚持紧抓无意识幻象的世界不放。

一位风格化的私家侦探（<u>Shu Qi</u>饰）决定去寻找其中一位拒绝者，但要找到他，她必须先进入一个早已逝去的电影时代。她通过将自己投射进一个融合了<u>Fritz Lang</u>式戏剧、<u>Lotte Reiniger</u>童话，以及战间期好莱坞所创造的那种鸦片馆式幻想的中国混合影像中，找到了这个长期做梦的人。为了给他一个配得上的“告别”，她打开他的胸腔，放入几卷胶片和一个35毫米放映卷轴，它们随后开始运转播放。

这引出了全片六个章节中的第二章，这是一部跨越一个世纪的旅行式叙事，其中“沉迷幻觉者”（由演员兼流行歌手<u>Jackson Yee</u>饰演）在六个截然不同的电影时代中循环，每一次都化身为完全不同的形象：一个苦恼的特雷门琴演奏者、一个曾经的佛教僧人、一个中年骗子、一个在第二个千年最后一夜寻找爱情的小混混。或许“轮回”（Reincarnation）比“复活”（Resurrection）更贴切，但无论如何，毕赣的兴趣似乎并不在宗教，而更偏向形式主义；这部电影明显让人联想到<u>Italo Calvino</u>1979年的小说《<u>If on a Winter’s Night a Traveler</u>》，后者同样在一个戏谑的元叙事框架中跳跃于多种文学风格之间。

整部影片似乎弥漫着超现实主义对“梦境可以开启新的社会与政治现实”的信念；而在第一章中，那些复杂的布景设计、狂乱的现场特效以及对镜子与通道的迷恋，使人强烈感受到<u>Jean Cocteau</u>的精神影响。但在当代导演中，毕赣比几乎任何人都更接近<u>David Lynch</u>，能够捕捉梦境独特的逻辑与流动性，并坚持认为电影本身就是一种终极的集体幻觉。他的上一部作品《<u>Long Day’s Journey into Night</u>》以一个长达一小时、用3D拍摄的一镜到底作为高潮，这一段似乎开拓了电影在沉浸体验上的新可能；从结构上看，那一段是“电影中的电影”，但没有其他作品能让我如此真实地感觉自己进入了他人的梦境。

《地球最后的夜晚》似乎旨在让我们切身体会时间与记忆的运作方式，深入皮肤之下。《复活》则更关注观念，更明确地是一首献给讲故事艺术的颂歌，尽管它对电影的致敬并不总是成功。画幅比例在不同段落中不断变化，但关于特雷门琴演奏者的那一段——一个带有间谍意味的黑色1940年代场景——却是以数字方式、彩色拍摄，而非胶片或黑白。因此，它更像一部现代的时代片，而非真正唤起某个电影时代的影像，尽管其中一个镜厅场景明显向<u>Orson Welles</u>的《<u>The Lady from Shanghai</u>》致意。

关于堕落僧人的故事并不明显对应电影史中的某一时期；那个骗子的段落（似乎发生在1980年代）亦是如此，但这些章节共同传达出一种微妙的精神衰退、幻灭与庸俗机会主义的氛围。因此，《复活》或许相比电影史，更是在讲述20世纪的中国历史。不过，如果你不过度纠结于它的自反结构，你会被毕赣生动而不可预测的叙事能力所吸引，而<u>Dong Jinsong</u>的摄影和<u>M8z</u>那种催眠般、不断变形的配乐，也都紧随导演的多变风格。

你可以称它为一场感官盛宴，但问题在于，毕赣让每一个章节都围绕一种感官的毁灭或扭曲展开：特雷门琴演奏者刺穿自己的耳朵；一个小偷对石佛撒尿，然后让僧人用“味觉”去辨认最苦的石头；骗子教一个孤儿用嗅觉来作弊。至于视觉——正是它启动了整个叙事漩涡：沉迷做梦（也就是我们对电影的沉迷）最终只能走向死亡。（如果听起来太沉重，毕赣也带着幽默：他甚至加入了可能是电影史上最哲学的一个“放屁笑话”。）

在每个章节之间，都插入一个蜡烛融化的短镜头。这既呼应了开头字幕中用燃烧的蜡烛来解释“做梦如何加速死亡”的比喻，也是一种燃烧的意象——影片的核心母题之一。每一章都以某种形式包含火焰；电影开场甚至是银幕被烧出一个洞，让我们看到战间期的中国观众反过来凝视我们。这一影像的激动人心，在结尾被呼应又反转：随着字幕出现，我们看到一个电影院连同其中的蜡像观众一起燃烧、融化。火焰在片中的反复出现难以简单解释，但它让我想起<u>Kiyoshi Kurosawa</u>的《<u>Cure</u>》，其中一个催眠杀人者通过打火机来操控受害者。毕赣同样关注闪烁影像的催眠潜力——以及它所带来的危险与狂喜。

他当然深谙此道：他完全具备催眠观众的能力。最后一段叙事中，毕赣再次展现了他对流动长镜头的掌控——这一次“仅仅”持续半小时。我们的“沉迷幻觉者”，在最后的身份中成为名叫阿波罗的小混混，在雨水与红光浸透的港口城市里，面对“雨衣帮”及其神秘首领的威胁，却仍坠入与一位年轻女子（<u>Li Gengxi</u>饰）的爱情之中，时间从1999年跨入2000年。两人最终登上一艘船顺流而下，这种前行的动势为整部电影提供了一个情感上的收束——一部（就像导演本人一样）从未停止向前流动的电影。

REVIEWED BY ARJUN SAJIP
Heres a devils bargain: give up the ability to dream, and you could live forever. In Resurrection, Bi Gans first feature in seven years and his most head-spinning work yet, that's the deal taken by everyone except a handful of 'deliriants, who insist on dinging to the world of unconscious visions.
A stylish gumshoe (Shu Qi) decides to seek out one of these refuseniks, but to find him she must first enter a bygone era of cinema. She locates the chronic dreamer after projecting herself into a Chinese hybrid of Fritz Lang dramas, Lotte Reiniger fairytales and the kind of opium-den fantasies Hollywood cooked up between the wars. To give him the kind of send-off he deserves, she opens up his ribcage and inserts a couple of spools and a reel of 35mm, which then begin to play.
This kicks off the second chapter in a six-part, century-spanning travelogue in which the Delir-
¡ant (played by the actor and pop star Jackson Yee) cycles through six distinct cinematic eras, adopting a wildly different guise each time: a troubled young theremin player, a former Buddhist monk. a middle-aged swindler, a small-time hoodlum in search of love on the last night of the second mil-lennium. Perhaps "Reincarnation' would be a more apt title than Resurrection, but in any case Bis preoccupations seem not so much religious as formal-ist; the film distinctly recalls Italo Calvinos 1979 novel If on a Winter's Night a Traveller, which similarly hopscotches through a range of literary sensibilities under a playful meta-narrative.
The film seems suffused with the surrealist belief in the power of dreams to unlock new social and political realities; and in the first chapter, with its intricate set design, delirious in-camera effects and fascination with mirrors and portals, one of the spir-is most keenly felt is that of Jean Cocteau. But Bi himself has done more than any filmmaker this side of David Lynch to capture the peculiar logic and foing of dreams - and to insist on cinema itself as the ultimate collective delirium. His previous fea-ture. Long Day's Journey into Night (2018), culminated in an hour-long one-take shot, filmed in 3D, that scemed to break new ground in the transportative potential of cinema; structurally, the sequence is a fim within a film, but no other movie has made me fel quite so convincingly like I'm in someone disés dream.

Long Day's Journey seemed designed to make us feel the very workings of time and memory in ways that get under our skin. Resurrection is more interested in ideas, and more explicitly a pacan to the art of storytelling, though its homages to cinema don't always come off. Aspect ratios mutate faithfully throughout, though the sequence with the theremin player - a noirish 194os-set piece with undertones of espionage - is shot digitally, in colour, rather than on film or in black and white.
In consequence, it fecls more like a modern-day period movie than an evocation of an era of cinema, despite a hall-of-mirrors scene that tips its fedora to Orson Welless The Lady from Shanghai (1947).
The story about the fallen monk does not feel as though it refers to any particular period in film his-tory; nor does the hornswoggler's tale, seemingly set some time in the 198os, though the segments together convey a subtle sense of spiritual decline, disillusionment and petty opportunism. So perhaps Resurrection has more to say about zoth-century Chinese history than cinema history. But don't think too hard about the self reflexive framing and you'll be drawn in by Bis knack for vivid, unpredictable storytelling, with Dong Jinsong's cinematography and M8z's hypnotic, shape-shifting score taking their cues from the director's versatility.
You might call it a feast for the senses, except that Bi makes each chapter revolve around the destruction or perversion of a different sense. The theremin player stabs himself in the ear; a thief pees on a stone Buddha, then asks the monk, who is none the wiser, to locate the bitterest stone by taste; the con artist teaches an orphan how to cheat at cards by using her nose. As for sight, that's the sense that sets this whole whirligig in motion: the Deliriant's love of dreaming - that is, our addiction to cinema - can only end in death. (If that all sounds a bit heavy, Bi is having a gas: he makes sure to include what might be the most philosophical fart joke in film history.)
Between each chapter is a brief shot of a wax candle melting. This is both a callback to the opening titles, in which the analogy of a burning candle is used to explain how dreaming hastens our own death, and an image of combustion - one of the film's key motifs. Each chapter includes fire in some form; the movie opens with a hole burning through the screen, allowing us to see an interwar Chinese movie audience peering back at us. The excitement of this image is mirrored but subverted by the films melancholy final frame: as the credits roll, we watch a cinema and the waxwork audience inside it catch fire and melt away. This recurrence of flames in the film resists easy interpretation, but it put me in mind of Kurosawa Kiyoshis Cure (1997), in which a murderous hypnotist spellbinds his victims by playing with a lighter. Bi, too, is interested in the hypnotic potential of flickering images - the dangers and the delirious joys they might bring.
He should know: he can hypnotise with the best of them. The final narrative segment sees Bi flexing his command of the sinuous single take, in this case a mere half-hour. Our Deliriant, in his final guise as a hoodlum named Apollo, heedless of the looming threat of the Raincoat Gang and its mysterious leader, falls for a young woman (Li Gengxi) in a port city drenched in rain and red light as 1999 turns into 2000. The lovers wind up on a boat that sets off down the river, its sense of momentum providing a neat emotional ending for a film that (like the filmmaker) never stops moving forward.
