#ICA 长评-没有下车。在电影拍摄于苏联解体的前两年，从影片中能看到对于社会混乱的呈现，大量的现实与荒诞交织呈现似乎是后苏联神经崩溃社会的风情画。片中男主的猝睡状态的隐喻还是有比较强的指代性，甚至直接展现了结尾处没有下车的他。电影分为两个部分，黑白的影片中的影片开场就是一个象征性极强的镜头，泡泡在一个破损被丢弃的娃娃上破掉，似乎寓言了接下来发生的一切，接下来就看到与洋娃娃一起被丢弃的，那拐杖（战后），钟表（计划）和鲜花（美好）。丈夫（苏慈宗？）的葬礼就是一切的开始。第二部分彩色的影外似乎直接到了苏献总时期，开场男主在影院睡着就挺讽刺的，而整部电影以他为基础呈现的就是某种无力。听觉上完全后配音处理呈现出的就是一种格格不入的癫狂。

视觉上，整体的呈现效果很好，跳切，重复，跃轴的不适感还挺强的，手持的情绪表达也很好。片中两场戏给我印象极深，第一个就是电影的第一场戏，空镜泡泡在一个破损被丢弃的娃娃上破掉，似乎寓言了接下来发生的一切，下一个镜头就可以看到与洋娃娃一起被丢弃的，那拐杖（战后），钟表）（计划），鲜花（美好）及那个娃娃。另一个就是戏外男主回到家后在镜子前吃鱼子酱，感觉那场镜头由虚无到存在刹那的过渡。

**在一臂之距**
**作者：Hannah Bonner**

几周前，我搬到了费城，迅速陷入一种让我连自己都认不出的忧郁情绪。这里的空气与从前不同。灌木玫瑰像瀑布一样垂坠而下——鲜艳的朱红色花朵。在我住的社区主街上，有咖啡馆、餐馆和商店，路边人行道裂开、鼓起。我更喜欢独自散步，可突然间，每一次出门都变成了某种公告，甚至游行。那里有一位男人，在人民浸礼会教堂外扫地。那里有一位女人，坐在合作社门口，每次我进出时都向我打招呼。在公园里，我一边吃着豆腐越南三明治，一边观看几场棋局，很清楚那些棋手们也在看我。过不了多久，我就必然退回到室内。我的公寓里安静孤独，给了我充足的机会看片和写作。我在新居里漂浮，就像玛雅·德伦（Maya Deren）诗中的叙述者：“穿着海藻礼服的梦游者，/ 漂进那绿色、无门的走廊，/ 以一臂之距引领着自己。”

正是在这种内室的空间里，我重看了玛雅·德伦的影片《在变形时间中的仪式》（*Ritual in Transfigured Time*，1946）。开场镜头呈现的是一种我非常熟悉的空间布局：一个女人在房间之间走动。我一直以为“漂移”（dérive）这种体验只适用于城市街道，但在家里无目的地徘徊，似乎也是同样的冲动。区别只在于**公共性**——和**尺度**。在这样陌生而私密的空间里，我可能会发现些什么，关于自己，或为了自己？可能性之线团在我松散而微颤的手里渐渐展开。

在《仪式》的派对段落里，各种客人围绕彼此旋转，像行星一样，身上佩戴着黑色的华饰。高对比度的灯光毫不留情。作家安娜伊丝·宁（Anaïs Nin）曾因自己的形象而愤怒，她指责德伦的摄影机有一种“丑化（uglify）”的独特能力。两人从此再未说话。但关于社会性与性别行为准则的编舞与评论仍然留存。当德伦冲进大海时，我想到电影是如何穿越时间，延续并渗入彼此的。

也许《岸上》（*At Land*, 1944）开场时像鲸鱼一样搁浅的女人，正是《仪式》结尾走进海里的那个角色。她进入大海之后，角色又有了新的生命，另一个电影在等待她。看似溺亡的结局，其实是那位“身披海藻”的梦游者的重生。

我自己的生活也感觉像是被这样拉长了。那种拉扯的感觉贯穿了《仪式》的派对段落和双人舞，角色们在不同的剪辑中相互接续动作。就像这些角色看上去是**单一、不间断动作的不同版本或中断**，德伦的每一帧、每一部电影，构成了一系列**联想的链接**。

当我一遍遍观看《在变形时间中的仪式》时，太阳在我三层公寓外的排屋上落下。外面在下毛毛雨，春天异常寒冷。

虽然我时常被雨光、屋顶、和梧桐树分叉的树冠分心，我还是看了好几遍，然后摸索着回到床上。

在这段时间里，我偶尔会做梦，梦里我感到背部有轻微的压力或针刺感，随后陷入一种无法描述的瘫痪。梦中的一切都有着我所梦空间的轮廓，完全是我现实中的居所。梦里，**最可怕的部分是“现实感”**。我在梦里很清楚，自己被某种东西扎了一下，使我动弹不得，这个意识在冻结的时间里点亮了我的神经系统。某种东西正在我体内发生变形，但我还在学习这意味着什么。

在《衰弱症》（*The Asthenic Syndrome*，1989）中，教师尼古拉·阿列克谢耶维奇在不合时宜的时刻睡着了。他染上了“衰弱症”，这也是琪拉·穆拉托娃（Kira Muratova）这部电影的名字来源。他的症状如此严重，以至于在电影放映时，他睡过了整整四十分钟——也就是我们刚刚看过的那段影片，跟接下来的剧情毫无叙事关联。演完第一部分的女演员出现在电影院的舞台上，接受掌声。她的脸像闪光灯一样，明亮而幽魂般。她将岁月优雅地穿在骨骼上。

在《衰弱症》第一段的“片中片”里，女演员的丈夫去世，引发了一系列情绪爆发，她在冲突之间游荡。她尖叫。她训斥年迈的上司。她扑向男人们，而那些男人反过来把她推开。她也是在漂浮，但更具暴力倾向。她的动作透露的不是目的，而是一个焦躁不安的思维在运转。

我一直相信，**行走时的思考是最有助于写作的**。在运动中，散文或诗歌的形状也随之生成。只要我预设了去向，思绪就会断裂。

在影片中，教师的第一句台词之一是愤怒地喊道：“**我得把它写下来……他试图对所有人都好，但这是不可能的。他在松开旧结之前又打了新结。负担越来越重，但他无法松手。他想要一些浪漫的东西！**”

他的这番话是背诵吗？还是咒语？我无法确定。但我还是要把他的话写下来，这种动作既是对思考的完成，也是对思考的打开。

Hannah Bonner
A few weeks ago, I moved to Philadelphia and promptly fell into a melancholy that rendered me unrecognizable even to myself. The air is different here. Shrub roses cascade vertiginously - bright, vermillion blooms. On the main drag of my neighborhood are cafes, restaurants, and shops, the sidewalks cracked and swelling. I prefer to walk in privacy and suddenly each outing feels like an announcement or parade. There is the man sweeping outside the People's Baptist Church. There is the woman who sits outside the coop greeting me as I come and go. In the park, I watch various chess games while I eat tofu banh mi, well aware that these players are also watching me. After a short time, I inevitably retreat indoors. The quiet solitude of my apartment affords me ample opportunities to watch films - and to write. I drift through my new rooms like the narrator of Maya Deren's poem: a "somnambulist in seaweed gowned / floating] down the green, the doorless corridors, / leading myself at arm's length."
It was in this interior space where | rewatched Maya Deren's film Ritual in Transfigured Time (1946). The opening shot presented a spatial layout with which I was intimately familiar: a woman walking between rooms. I had always thought of dérive as applicable only to city streets, but to drift without direction or purpose in the confines of one's home seemed a similar impulse. The difference was merely one of publics
- and of scale. What might I discover for or about myself in such strange and private quarters? The yarn of possibilities unfurled in my loose and twitchy hands.
During Rituals party sequence, various guests rotate around one another like planets, bejeweled in black garb. The high contrast lighting is unforgiving. Writer Anaïs Nin was furious at her depiction, later accusing Deren's camera of a unique ability to "uglify." The women did not speak again. But the choreography of, and commentary on, sociosexual mores endures. When Deren runs into the ocean, I think of how films can extend into and through one another across time.
Perhaps the woman waterlogged and beached like a whale at the beginning of At Land (1944) is Maya Deren's character from the end of Ritual. There is another life for her character once she enters the ocean, there is another film waiting.
What appears like a drowning at the end of Ritual is a rebirth for the somnambulist "gowned" in "seaweed."
My own life feels similarly protracted. And that stretching sensation extends throughout Ritual's opening party sequence and pas de deux, as characters assume others' movements across the film's various cuts. Just as these characters sometimes feel like different iterations or interruptions of a single, unbroken movement, each frame of Deren's, and each film, creates a series of associative linkages.
As I watched Ritual in Transfigured Time over and over, the sun set across the row houses outside my third storey window. It was drizzling, the spring unseasonably cool.
Though I found myself distracted by the rain light, and the rooftops, and the sycamores' branching crowns, I watched the film several more times before fumbling back to bed.
During this period, I occasionally experienced dreams in which I felt a slight pressure or prick in my backside and was subsequently paralyzed for an indescribable amount of time. In the dream everything shared the same contours of the domestic space in which I dreamt. In these dreams, the experience of reality was the scariest part. I was aware, in the dream, that I had been pricked by something that rendered me immobile, and the realization lit up my nerve system during these freeze frames of time. Something was transfiguring within me, but I am still learning what.
In The Aesthenic Syndrome (1989), the schoolteacher Nikolai Alekseevich falls asleep at inopportune times. He has caught the aesthenic syndrome, from which Kira Muratova's drama derives its name. So disruptive are his symptoms that he sleeps through a screening, the first forty minutes of the film we have just watched - one that bears no narrative relation to what transpires next. The actress of the first forty minutes appears on the theatre's stage to applause. She has a face like a flashbulb, luminous and haunted. She wears the years elegantly on her bones.
In the film within The Aesthenic Syndrome, the death of the actress's husband prompts a series of outbursts as she careens from altercation to altercation. She screams. She berates her geriatric boss. She throws herself on men who fling her off in turn. She, too, is drifting, but with a more violent bent. Her movements reveal nothing but a restless mind at work.
I have long believed that my ambulatory thoughts are the most conducive to writing; that to be in motion foretells how an essay or a poem will later take its shape. The moment I predetermine where I am going is the moment when I lose the thread. In one of his first lines of dialogue, the schoolteacher rages that he "has to write it down...
He tried to be good to everyone, but it was impossible. He tied new knots before unloosing the old one. The weight became heavier, but he couldn't let go. He wanted something romantic!" Is his outcry a recitation? An incantation? I couldn't tell you. But I must write his words down, another gesture that completes, as well as opens, figurations of thought.
