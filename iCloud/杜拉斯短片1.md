# 杜拉斯短片1



#ICA 杂志风格的电视节目，应该被定义为电视剧的，瓦尔达和克劳德·朗兹曼是定期撰稿人，杜拉斯则成为该节目的热门记者。这次影院放映了其中五集（名称放在最后），从开场和质感能看出来不是同一个时期的作品，感觉几部短片的主题可以简述为“囚徒”。

第一部短片的被采访对象一个7岁的小孩，感觉孩子可以作为是一个未社会化的形象来批判现实的社会，通过访谈天马行空的问题和带有指向性的问题来看社会的状态，还蛮喜欢以这部作为开场的，那种放飞的感觉是一种思想自由的展望。

第二部短片则采访了动物园的一位饲养员，感觉是带有极强的讽刺的，动物园中的狮子老虎黑豹在管理者的视角被描述成为“我认为他们是开心的，他们没有悲伤”，与此对照的视觉呈现是动物们被囚禁在铁笼之中来回踱步，表达意思也是很明显了，最喜欢这部短片的镜头，倾向性非常强，蒙太奇剪辑的讽刺意味也非常强，还有最后摄影师进入笼子中模拟动物的POV视角所呈现出的压抑感更是十分直观，动物受困于笼子。（影片中黑豹黑暗中嘶吼的镜头也出现在杜拉斯的第一部故事片也是这次回顾展的开幕影片《音乐》中，当时就给观众一种极强的不安感，镜头缓缓后拉，发现是一个电视，当时大家都笑了，现在看的话似乎还是有些受困者的悲伤的感觉，不过在自己影片中看自己影片也是很不错）

第三部短片聚焦一个脱衣舞女郎，她讲述了自己家庭的情况，也讲述了她的工作和想法等，其中对于理想社会主义下的幻想非常的符合现实的讨论，非常喜欢舞台表演部分的特写呈现，开场部分的手持拍摄也非常真实，那狭小的空间呈现出的受困状态明显，而她也受困于这个社会。

第四部短片采访的是一位在女子监狱工作的教师，这部分能感觉到那话语之间的火药味和视觉上呈现出的压迫感，影片没有出现过女囚犯，但是那空荡荡的房间和对照女教师的描述看起来还是很有反思意味。

第五部短片的则是对于一位马克思主义学生的呈现，学校的设计让我直接想到了《擦鞋童》中的少管所。

杜拉斯为《Dim Dam Dom》(一个杂志风格的电视节目，从1965年播出到1970年)所做的采访中，出现了温和的叛乱。该节目由《Elle》杂志的编辑黛西•德•加拉德制作，每周日播出，主要面向女性(“les dames”)，不过也希望一些男性(“des hommes”)会觉得有趣。他们确实做到了:《Dim Dam Dom》因为内容的混杂(时尚、音乐、政治、室内设计、烹饪)和形式的新奇(德加拉德把镜头交给了时尚的、实验性的电影制作人，让他们来制作有趣的电影片段)，迅速聚集了一批狂热的粉丝。agn<s:1>·瓦尔达和克劳德·兰兹曼是定期撰稿人，而杜拉斯则成为该节目的热门记者。她采访了名人，也采访了普通人，包括一个7岁的男孩，一个脱衣舞娘和一个托洛茨基主义者的第六名前成员(他现在已经60多岁了，是马克龙的朋友)。

在1966年的一集《玛格丽特·杜拉斯与野兽们在一起》中，杜拉斯采访了巴黎动物园的一名大型猫科动物饲养员。这段16分钟的片段以一段咒语般的独白开场，她沉思着这些动物:“我出生在巴黎。他们的青春，在这里。爱与死，在这里。文森地区。”如果配上的画面不是同样的忧郁的话，这几乎会很有趣——一种对杜拉斯式忧郁和节奏的拙劣模仿:狮子在笼子里踱步，栅栏在它们的皮毛上投下阴影(这些画面后来出现在电视屏幕上的《音乐》中，这是她的第一部故事片，与保罗·塞班共同执导，她是通过《Dim Dam Dom》认识保罗·塞班的)。这一集是用超现实主义摄影的肢解强度拍摄的，光线非常稀疏，有时我们只看到黑豹牙齿的闪光。杜拉斯被吓坏了，他确信动物们并不快乐，影片以一种抽象的倒置结尾:镜头来回踱步，焦虑不安，从围栏里跟踪工作的动物园管理员。随着这种突然的悲怆，贯穿杜拉斯所有作品的折磨、渴望和排斥的尖锐边缘被抛入解脱。

在1967年的《玛格丽特·杜拉斯:娇小的罗盖特》一集中，杜拉斯遇到了法国唯一的监狱女教师。杜拉斯问她是否想过释放囚犯。“有自杀吗?”“惩罚是什么?”气氛紧张。女家庭教师用她修剪整齐的手把开信刀翻过来，有点威胁的样子。她拒绝把自己交给杜拉斯，杜拉斯对她的逃避感到愤怒。他们互相交谈，直到家庭教师突然结束采访——“我为自己的工作感到自豪。”我们从来没有见过女囚犯，只看到她们空荡荡的牢房，这并不重要:杜拉斯代表她们说话了。

在年轻的脱衣舞女洛洛·皮加勒身上，杜拉斯遇到了一个更感人的主题。在杜拉斯讲述皮加勒的传记时，摄影机夹在阁楼公寓的屋檐之间，追踪着她的一举一动。书中的许多细节都与杜拉斯自己的相似:皮加勒在贫困中长大，母亲努力抚养孩子;必须做出决定，这样她和她的兄弟姐妹才能生存下去。皮加勒并不喜欢她的工作，但她很认真地对待它:我们看到她穿着金色的蕾丝裙表演，动作巧妙，她的脸很迷人，就像一个新维尔模糊的女主角。杜拉斯也很认真地对待皮加勒，认真地听她解释性工作的本质(她认为这是工作:卡巴莱和“办公室”是一样的)，以及脱衣舞所揭示的欲望(“漂亮的脸蛋比美丽的身体更重要”)。皮加勒说，如果说脱衣让人感到绝望，那是因为工作环境，这种工作方式让人几乎没有时间做“真实的自己”。此情此景荡漾着感情的涟漪，但却没有流露出居高临下的多愁善感。在《皮加勒》中，杜拉斯找到了她一直在寻找的东西:日常痛苦的情节剧。”

摘自莉莉·欧文·罗兰兹的文章《电视上的玛格丽特·杜拉斯》，首次发表于《另一个屏幕》。

[Let Cinema Go To Its Ruin:
The Cinema of Marguerite Duras](https://www.ica.art/films/duras)

“A temperate insurgency laces the interviews Duras conducted for Dim Dam Dom, a magazine-style television show that ran from 1965 until 1970. Produced by Daisy de Galard, an editor at Elle, and broadcast on Sundays (“dimanche”), the programme was aimed at women (“les dames”) though it was hoped some men (“des hommes”) might find it interesting, too. And they did: Dim Dam Dom quickly gathered a cultish following for the promiscuity of its content (fashion, music, politics, interior design, cookery) and for the novelty of its format (de Galard gave the camera over to voguish, experimental filmmakers to create its playful cinematic segments). Agnès Varda and Claude Lanzmann were regular contributors, while Duras became the show’s popular correspondent. She interviewed celebrities but also ordinary people, including a seven-year-old boy, a stripper and a Trotskyist sixth-former (who now, in his sixties, is a friend of Macron).

In an episode from 1966, entitled Marguerite Duras chez les fauves, Duras interviews a big-cat keeper at a Parisian zoo. The 16-minute segment opens with an incantatory monologue in which she muses about the animals: “Born in Paris. Their youth, here. Love and death, here. In Vincennes.” It would almost be funny – a parody of Durassian gloom and cadence – if the accompanying images weren’t equally melancholic: the lions pacing in their cage, the bars casting shadows over their fur (these images would later appear on a television screen in [La Musica](http://ica.art/films/la-musica), her first feature, co-directed with Paul Seban whom she’d met through Dim Dam Dom). The episode is filmed with the dismembering intensity of surrealist photography and the light is so sparse that at moments we see little more than the brilliant flash of a black panther’s fang. Duras is appalled, convinced the animals are unhappy, and the film concludes with an abstract inversion: the camera now paces back and forth, agitated, tracking the zookeepers at work from inside the enclosure. With this sudden surfeit of pathos, the sharp edges of torment, yearning and exclusion that cut through all of Duras’s work are thrown into relief.

Outrage imprints itself quite forcibly on the episode entitled Marguerite Duras à la petite Roquette (1967) in which Duras meets France’s only prison governess. Duras asks her if she ever thinks about setting the prisoners free. “Are there suicides?” “What are the punishments?” The atmosphere is taut. The governess turns a letter opener over in her manicured hands, somewhat menacingly. She refuses to give herself over to Duras, who bristles at the evasions. They talk over one another until the governess brings the interview to an abrupt close – “I’m proud of what I do.” That we never see the women prisoners, only their empty cells, hardly matters: Duras has spoken on their behalf.

In Lolo Pigalle, a young stripper, Duras encounters a more affecting subject. The camera, wedged between the eaves of an attic apartment, tracks Pigalle’s movements as Duras narrates her biography. Many of its details resemble Duras’s own: Pigalle grew up in poverty with a mother who struggled to mother; decisions had to be made so that she and her siblings could survive. Pigalle doesn’t love her job, but she takes it seriously: we watch her perform in a gold lamé dress, moving artfully, her face is captivating, like a Nouvelle Vague heroine. Duras takes Pigalle seriously too, listening attentively as she explains the nature of sex work (it is work, she thinks: the cabaret is the same as “an office”) and what stripping reveals about desire (“a good face is more important than a beautiful body”). If there is a despair to stripping, Pigalle says it is because of the working conditions, the way it leaves so little time for being one’s “true self”. The scene ripples with feeling yet stops short of patronising sentimentality. In Pigalle, Duras finds what she was always looking for: the melodrama of everyday anguish.”

Extracts from Lili Owen Rowlands’ essay Marguerite Duras on Television, first published on Another Screen.
